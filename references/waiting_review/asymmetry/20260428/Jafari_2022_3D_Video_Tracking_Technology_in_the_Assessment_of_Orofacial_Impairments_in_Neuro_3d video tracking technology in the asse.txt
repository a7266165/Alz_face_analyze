Abstract & Keywords
Changes to the orofacial muscles’ movement and speech are often among the earliest signs
perceived in Neurological disorders. Detection of subtle changes in speech and facial movements
can help with the diagnosis and prognosis of neurological disorders. Deep artificial intelligent
video-based facial analysis models have the potential to be used as objective and non-invasive
clinical tools. This thesis used the V3 framework for evaluation of digital biomarkers and their
adoption into clinical settings to evaluate an automatic video-based facial analysis system as an
objective assessment tool for accessing orofacial movements. The proposed system consists of a
3D camera and Artificial Intelligent-based algorithms that automatically extract objective
clinically interpretable kinematic features from video recordings of individuals performing
standard orofacial tasks. This work investigates the analytical and clinical validation of the
proposed system to assess the severity of orofacial impairment in clinical groups.
Keywords: computer-aided orofacial assessment, neurological disorders, facial kinematics

Chapter 1 – Introduction

Neurological disorders
There is a rapid change in the demographic of the world as life expectancy is being
prolonged due to several factors such as better healthcare, improved living conditions, and
technology advancements [1]. In fact, it is estimated that the older adults aged 60 and over will
account for an estimated 22% of global population by year 2050 [2]. Despite the above-mentioned
improvements in the life expectancy, neurological conditions have arguably become the most
dreaded condition of older adults, with age being the main risk factor for such disorders, including
Alzheimer disease (AD), Parkinson disease (PD), motor neuron disease (MND), and stroke [3].
Treating and caring for individuals with such conditions are costly and expected to multiply in the
years to come. It is estimated that caring for older adults with neurological conditions already
consumed over $600 billion worldwide [4]. As the global population of older adults is on the rise,
so is the prevalence of age-related neurological conditions and the need for development of cheap,
efficient, scalable tools that are sensitive to the early signs and subtle changes associated with the
onset of neurological disorders.
Neurological disorders are brain, cognitive, or behavioural disorders that may affect
various human functions such as speaking, limb movements and cognitive/ learning capacity.
There are over six hundred known neurological impairments with the global prevalence of these
diseases found to be at 10.2% [5].
The most common observed impairments in neurological conditions are impaired motor,
sensory, visual, balance, cognition, swallowing function and communication. Motor impairments
include weakness or complete paralysis across various muscle groups e.g., in the muscles of upper
or lower limbs, trunk muscles as well as muscles of the head and neck, including orofacial muscles.
Sensory impairment includes numbness, loss of sensation of touch, pressure, or pain the affected
body structures. Cognitive impairment includes, as example, deficits in attention, memory, or
visuo-spatial orientation. A common sign of neurological disorders is the loss or altered speech
and inability to eat/ swallow normally. Communication impairments may be due to aphasia,

2

dysarthria, or apraxia of speech [6]. Dysarthria and apraxia of speech represent the defects to the
motor aspects of speech production [6], [7].
Clinical assessment of dysarthria

Changes in one’s speech might be among one of the early signs of a neurological disorder
[8]. Dysarthria - a motor speech disorder – is due to weakness, slowness, or incoordination of the
musculature used to produce speech [6], [9]. Dysarthria is the most common occurring speech
disorder in neurological conditions, with observed occurrence of 51% in patients with multiple
sclerosis, 20-30% in individuals post-stroke, and 50-89% of PD patients [6]. Speech difficulties
are observed at disease onset in approximately 30% of patients with Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis
(ALS - aka bulbar onset ALS); while in 80-95% of ALS patients, dysarthria is developed as the
disease progresses [10].
Auditory-perceptual assessment methods are considered gold standards for clinical
differential diagnosis, assessing severity of symptoms and functional changes as well as making
treatment plans and management decisions in dysarthria [11]. For example, a system developed
by Darley, Aronson, and Brown established 38 perceptual dimensions defining auditoryperceptual changes in respiration, voice, articulation, prosody, and other aspects of speech to
characterize the dysarthria type and localize the lesion site [11]. Auditory-perceptual methods have
great advantage such as convenience, economy, and robustness, however, they have been also
criticized for being susceptible to various sources of error and biases[12]. This method is heavily
subjective and factors such as listener’s shifting internal standards, past experiences and area of
expertise, and type of rating scale used may become confounding factors [13].
Assessment of orofacial impairments
Orofacial muscles are often impaired in neurological diseases. For example, patients with
PD can suffer from hypomimia, also known as masked face, in which they have reduced range and
speed of facial muscle movements. In order to diagnose this condition, neurologists use the
Movement Disorder Society – Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rating Scale (MDS-UPDRS) to
evaluate the presence and the severity of impairments. Section III of the assessment includes items
that specifically evaluate the patients’ facial expressions [14]. ALS patients have various orofacial

3

and speech difficulties that are observed in 80-95% of patients as the disease progresses with
reduced speaking rate and speech intelligibility being identified as the worst aspects of the disease
[10]. The ALS Functional Rating Scale – Revised (ALSFRS-R) is a 12-item questionnaire that
allows patients to rate their current motor functions including speech, swallowing, and saliva
management. It is a highly reliable and easily administrable system that have been used by
clinicians to keep track of disease progression [15]. In stroke patients, the orofacial impairments
depend on lesion’s size and affected cerebral structures. Orofacial impairments may occur due to
impairments of the cranial nerves, central nervous system pathways, or motoneuron pool. Stroke
patients have demonstrated reduced tongue and lip forces. Facial asymmetry is also common in
this clinical group [16].
The cranial nerve examination aims at providing information regarding the focal point of
the pathology in the brain or brainstem [17]. It includes examining the functionality of the nerves
that innervate muscles of the head and neck. The cranial nerves can be separated into four major
groups: the sensory nerves, nerves responsible of eye movements, nerves of oral cavity and
superior pharynx, and those responsible of neck movements. The neurological examination of
these nerves includes palpation, check for symmetry and wasting, and testing for weaknesses or
paralysis of muscles. The examination includes assessment of structures at rest, during nonspeech
activities for example during sustained postures and movements as well as during speech tasks,
and in response to reflex testing [18]. However, studies have shown that the assessments performed
by the experts are subjective and heavily dependent on the clinician’s expertise. Further, there are
no agreed upon standards for detection of various disorders and monitoring of disease progression
among experts in the field [15], [19], [20].
Using technology to support clinical assessment
With the advent of technology and increased availability of sensor-based solution to the
consumer marker together with an attempt to overcome the limitations of clinical assessments,
instrumentation-based protocols have been explored for the assessment of speech and orofacial
impairments [21]–[23]. Some of these instrumented systems include those based on acoustic
analyses (e.g., Phonatory aerodynamic system (PAS), nasometer) or kinematic/ movement-based
methods (e.g., 3D optical motion capture system, and 3D electromagnetic articulography (EMA)).

4

Kinematic methods are of particular interest because they allow precise measurements of the
movement associated with speech and orofacial functions.
The 3D optical motion capture system uses infrared light to track radio reflective markers
that are placed on specific facial landmarks to track the facial movement during speech and nonspeech tasks. The facial kinematics data such as range of motion and speed of each articulator
provides valuable insight on the performance of the lips, jaw and other facial muscles [22]. This
technology however requires a direct line-of-sight between the marker and the camera and cannot
be used for the assessment of tongue. As a result, electromagnetic devices, technology based on
electromagnetic field that does not require direct line-of-sight, have been used to track and assess
the movement of the tongue as well as the lips, jaw and face during speech and orofacial
assessments [24]. Studies using EMA have shown promising results and higher sensitivity to the
early subtle changes/symptoms in patients relative to healthy adults compared to both clinicians
and patients’ self-reports [21], [25]–[28]. However, such systems have their own challenges that
hinder their adoption into clinical settings including: their complexity and need of further
specialized training for clinicians to be able to analyze and understand the data, the high cost
associated with the instrumentation, and the long setup time of the sensor-based systems that result
in patient fatigue and prolonged assessment time [15].
Solution: 3D cameras
The rapid development of 3D sensing technology in the past decade has resulted in many
consumer applications. The Microsoft KinectTM was among the first 3D sensing consumer
products released that enabled new gaming experience. This product used a depth sensor
developed by Primesense that was based on structured light technology. Many other 3D sensing
devices shortly followed, some examples were Intel RealSense 3D camera range, Intel® Euclid™
(Intel Corp., Santa Clara, CA, USA), Creative Senz3D (Creative Technology Ltd., Singapore),
Asus Xtion Pro Live (AsusTek Computer Inc., Beitou District, Taipei, Taiwan), OptiTrack MoCap
(NaturalPoint, Inc., USA), Vicon Infineon 3D Image Sensor (Vicon Motion Systems Ltd., Oxford,
UK), and Kinect V2 (Microsoft, Redmond, WA, USA) [29]. As a result, various applications
started integrating these technologies, such as gesture and action recognition [30], [31], facial
expression analysis [32], and marker-less motion capture [33] just to name a few.

5

Intel RealSense 3D camera was one of the first low-cost, compact with high image quality
that was widely adopted in the research community. Intel RealSense 3D camera consists of a depth
camera (a projector and a sensor for triangulation) and an RGB camera that is full HD with
1920×1080 pixels resolution. The projector emits 860 nm near-infrared range light, and the IR
sensor has a 640×480 pixels resolution with frame rate of 600 fps (1200 fps in half resolution). It
is capable of providing a VGA-size depth map at 60 frames per second with 0.125 mm depth
resolution, i.e., submillimeter accuracy [34].
There are numerous studies that have explored the use of such 3D cameras for clinical
applications. In rehabilitation, such technologies have been considered for enabling patient
feedback and correction while performing exercise regimens. For example, Chhor et al. [35]
developed a computer game that used Intel RealSense 3D camera to aid upper body rehabilitation
by tracking hand gestures. Other examples of applications using Intel RealSense 3D camera by
tracking facial expression, movement of joints and skeletal tracking [29], [36], [37]. The depth
sensing technology has also been used in facial analysis for tasks such as emotion recognition [38]
and providing outcome measures for the assessment of neurological disorders in various clinical
populations [39]–[43]. It is the latter application that has a great potential for integration into
everyday clinical practice – for in clinic and remote assessments – as well as into clinical trials of
pharmaceutical and behavioural interventions.
Facial Landmark Detection
The development of modern artificial intelligence (AI) and deep learning techniques –
specifically, automated video analysis algorithms – afford substantial advances using video
cameras for clinical assessment and monitoring. Facial landmark detection and tracking algorithms
attempt to automatically detect the location of key facial landmarks on facial images or videos and
are the building block of many applications including facial recognition, animation, tracking,
expression analysis, and 3D facial modeling. Facial landmark detection is a challenging task for
reasons such as diverse facial appearances across subjects, expression and pose changes of a given
subject, partial occlusion and environmental factors (e.g., illumination). Therefore, earlier
algorithms aimed at detecting landmarks in controlled conditions, for example under a fixed
laboratory setting, with fixed head pose and minimal or highly specific facial expressions. In recent

6

years, more research has been focused on in-the-wild conditions, where algorithms aim at having
a robust performance for arbitrary facial expressions, head poses, environmental conditions, etc.
[44], [45].
Facial landmark detection algorithms are generally supervised; they use a large set of
manually labelled training facial images, from which the AI model learns to locate the same
landmarks on future test face images. These models first use a face detector to obtain a rectangular
bounding box of the face within a given image. Depending on the model, different number of facial
landmarks are outputted (e.g., a 17-point model, 29-point model, or 68-point model) marking the
locations of the eyes, nose, mouth etc. on the face. The output is in a form of vector x = (x1, . . . ,
xN , y1 , . . . , yN)T where (xi , yi), which denotes the pixel values of the ith landmark in the image,
refer to Figure 1-1.

Figure 1-1 Output of facial landmark detection algorithm, the pixel values of x and y coordinates are
superimposed on the input face image
Facial landmark detection models prior to deep learning are categorized into two primary
categories, based on either parametric or nonparametric methods. The parametric methods are built
based on data belonging to a particular distribution such as a Gaussian mixture model, while

7

nonparametric models do not assume a distribution. The parametric models have a fixed set of
parameters that are tuned based on the input data. Gaussian mixture models and the Active
Appearance Models are the most common methods used in this category[46]–[50]. In contrast, the
number of parameters in the nonparametric models may vary based on the number and complexity
of the training data; these models may still have implicit assumptions or shape constraints built in
because of the specifics of the training data. Graphical models such as tree structures and Markov
random field methods, in which the location of each facial landmark is optimally solved using
dynamic programming, are examples of nonparametric methods [51].
In recent years, deep networks, e.g., convolutional neural network (CNN), have shown
superior performance for facial landmark detection. These methods learn the facial shape and
appearance variations and the nonlinear mapping from facial images to landmark coordinates.
Examples include deep hierarchical face parsing [52] and coarse-to-fine autoencoder networks
[53]. In general, deep neural net-based algorithms are divided into two main categories of direct
regression methods that directly predict the coordinates of each landmark (e.g. [54]–[58]) and
heatmap regression-based methods, where a 2D heatmap is generated for location of each
landmark (e.g. [59], [60]) [61]. Some of the common architectures used as building blocks for
either of the above-mentioned approaches are ResNet [58], MobileNet [57], ShuffleNet [56],
Hourglass [59], HRNet [62], and CU-Net [60]. The most commonly used architecture is hourglass
architecture, initially introduced for pose estimation [63], which is an encoder-decoder network
block that can be sequentially stacked [59].
In facial video analysis, recurrent neural networks (RNN) [64] and long short-term memory
(LSTM) [65] architectures have been used to minimize frame-to-frame landmark jitter. Based on
a review conducted on the state-of-the-art facial landmark detection algorithms developed in recent
years [61], some of the best performing models on various datasets include SubpixelHeatmap [66],
ADNet [67], and PropagationNet [68]. In SubpixelHeatmap, the authors use local soft-argmax
computation to reduce the quantization error of the generated heatmap [66]. The authors for ADNet
use the fact that the error in the tangent direction (relative to face boundary) in general has higher
value than the error in the normal direction, thus, design the architecture such that it assigns higher
weights to normal error [67]. In PropagationNet, the authors note the importance of facial
boundary for accurate detection of the facial landmarks [68]. In this approach, convolutional

8

blocks transform landmark heatmaps into boundary heatmaps after each Hourglass module and
use it as an intermediate attention mask to improve final accuracy predictions [68].
There are several publicly available datasets for training facial landmark detection models
[69]–[72]. These datasets can be categorized into those captured in controlled conditions (e.g., in
a lab setting with fixed head pose and facial expression) [71], [73], or those captured in
uncontrolled environments, i.e., in-the-wild [70]. The latter are often collection of images from
websites such as Facebook or Google images. The ground truth facial landmarks are obtained
manually by employing workers or crowdsourcing through services such as the Amazon
Mechanical Turk. Each image is generally labelled by several workers and the averaged labelled
values are taken as final ground truth of landmark positions. The accuracy of the models is
measured by computing the distance between the landmark positions predicted by a landmark
detection model and the ground truth labels. The computed distance is commonly normalized by
the interocular distance and is referred to as the normalized mean error (NME) [61]. Another metric
used to report model performance is the failure rate (FR), which denotes the number of images
with NME above a given (e.g. 10%) threshold [61].
Facial landmark detection and tracking is an important first step (i.e., a preprocessing step)
for many application areas such as face recognition, expression and facial movement analysis, as
well as pain detection [74]–[78]. Exploring various algorithms and deep learning architectures and
methodologies to improve the tracking accuracy remains an active area of research. Currently, the
focus of the field is to balance accuracy and complexity of the models being developed as well as
leveraging semi-supervised and unsupervised learning methods in order to take advantage of the
abundant unlabeled content online to more effectively train the models [79].
The Face Alignment Network
The Face Alignment Network (FAN) [80] is a deep neural network with residual blocks
trained on a large pool of real and synthetically expanded images with ground truth facial landmark
locations. The FAN architecture is composed of four sequential components, each with an
hourglass (HG) architecture [63], in which the bottleneck blocks were replaced with hierarchical,
parallel, and multiscale blocks [81]. The outputs are 68 heatmaps of 64×64 dimensions, each of

9

which estimates the position of a facial landmark. The 2D-FAN was trained using 230,000 images
in the training set and was evaluated on 7,200 images in the test set [80].
In previous orofacial analysis studies, FAN was used to extract facial landmarks in clinical
populations [82]–[85]. Aligned with findings from Asgarian et al. [86], these studies found the
presence of biases resulting in reduced landmark localization accuracy in clinical populations
compared to healthy age-matched controls [82]–[85]. As a result, a series of studies addressed the
need to reduce such biases via fine-tuning the FAN on data from specific patient groups. Bandini
et al. released a publicly available dataset, the Toronto NeuroFace Dataset, from which more than
3,300 manually annotated images of faces from individuals with ALS, post-stroke, and agedmatched controls were used to fine-tune FAN [73]. In follow up studies, Guarin et al. showed that
fine-tuning FAN using the clinical data improved the overall model’s performance [84] as well as
improving the performance of subsequent classifiers using detected landmarks in differentiating
clinical groups from healthy controls [87]. Guarin et al. further quantified the amount of clinical
data needed to fine-tune and improve the performance of FAN [85]. It was found that fine-tuning
the model with as little as 8 annotated images from a single patient resulted in better performance
on other individuals from the same clinical group. The best performance was achieved by fine
tuning the model with 320 images from 40 patients and using more images from additional patients
did not improve the performance further. Overall, the findings showed that fine-tuning FAN using
a relatively small sample of clinically represented data improved the performance of the model as
well as the subsequent clinical facial analysis task.
Validation Framework
There have been frameworks developed specifically for the digital health technology
development and evaluation [88]. The V3 framework describes three phases for the evaluation of
digital biomarkers, namely, verification, analytical validation, and clinical validation [89].
According to this framework, verification refers to validating the hardware and sensors involved
in recording the digital measurement. Analytical validation refers to the process of ensuring that
the digital biomarker measurements are accurately measuring the intended phenomena. Clinical
validation is concerned with evaluating whether the digital biomarker provides meaningful clinical
information [89].

10

Robin et al. specifically examined this framework and provided further recommendations
of how to use this framework for speech related biomarkers and speech-based measures [90].
Using this framework, the verification step involved comparing various technologies and
establishing the adequacy of the final technical instrumentation at collecting parameters that can
be acceptable digital biomarkers [90]. The verification step of any technology is usually performed
by the manufacturer via bench top testing [89]. Analytical step varies depending on the relevant
features or methods used to compute the biomarker. This method can entail comparing the new
digital biomarker to the existing technical gold standards, obtaining concurrent validation through
comparison with other sensors, or through other methods such as cross-validation and independent
replication of results. At this phase it is also necessary to analyze and eliminate potential biases in
the data as a result of the confounding factors.
Clinical validation is the culmination of technology evaluation. At this stage, the
technology outputs are compared with clinical gold standards that have been established to detect
disease and track its progression. In the assessment of various neurological disorders, some
diseases have a clear gold standard while for many others do not. Therefore, in clinical validation,
the choice of reference measure limits the clinical validation as the digital biomarker can only be
as good as the measure used to validate it. Clinical validation can be especially challenging, when
the used reference measure has limited validity, or lower reliability, resulting in difficulties
achieving consistency [90]. Using this framework, engineers, scientists, and clinicians must work
together to bring a technology from a design and prototype phase into a clinically utilizable tool
[90].
Current Technology: Verification

The proposed system used in this thesis consists of Intel RealSense 3D Camera to collect
data from patients performing standard orofacial tasks as well as use of the FAN model to
automatically extract facial landmarks that are then used to further extract facial kinematics. The
performance of both components (the Intel RealSense 3D Camera and FAN model) has been
previously studied and verified by their developers as well as the research community. Keselman
et al. [91] performed a comprehensive study on the Intel RealSense 3D camera and verified the
performance of the system under different test conditions. Performance of FAN was tested on

11

some of available open-source datasets and its performance was reported relative to the other facial
landmark detection methods, as well as relative to manually annotated facial landmarks [80].
Together the system made from combining these two technologies has the potential to provide
clinicians with an objective way of analyzing facial kinematics. However, this system needs to
analytically and clinically validated before its adoption as a clinical tool, which are main
goals/objectives of this thesis.
Current Technology: Analytical Validation
The analytical validation process evaluates the generated physiological and/or behavioural
metrics in experiments involving human subjects [89]. The Intel RealSense 3D Camera has been
previously analytically validated in a study where, Ouni et al. [92] compared Intel RealSense 3D
against the sensor-based Electromagnetic Articulography (EMA) and found the tracking error of
the lower lip to be low and the system suitable for facial applications that requires facial motion
tracking. In another study, Bandini et al. [93] compared the Intel RealSense 3D Camera along with
Intraface facial landmark detection and tracking algorithm against the marker-based optical
tracking system and found the lips Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) to be between 1-3 mm range.
Harkel et al. [94] have conducted a reliability study comparing Intel RealSense 3D Camera against
a highly costly and accurate 3D facial tracking system and found high agreement between the two
methods in patients with unilateral facial palsy. The accurate tracking of the facial landmarks is a
prerequisite to performing clinically meaningful facial analysis. The development of new and more
accurate architectures remains an active area of research and their adoption in developing objective
clinical tools is on the rise [95]. However, according to the V3 framework, new analytical
validations are needed when there are changes/upgrades to either the software and/or hardware
[89]. Thus, as there were no analytical validation studies done previously that uses the Intel
RealSense 3D Camera together with the FAN to analyze facial kinematics, the first study of this
thesis aims at evaluating this technology.
Current Technology: Clinical Validation
Clinical validation process evaluates whether the proposed technology provides any
valuable clinical insight in a specific context [89]. Clinically, the primary goals of an assessment
are to detect clinical features that point to the presence of an abnormality that can lead to disease

12

diagnosis and/ or allows tracking the disease progression over time. Clinical gold standards carry
these two important missions – detection/ diagnosis vs. disease tracking [96], [97]. The proposed
technology has been previously validated in automatic assessment of facial video recordings
during speech and non-speech tasks to distinguishing healthy vs. post-stroke [39], healthy vs.
Parkinson’s disease [41], and healthy vs ALS individuals [23]. Several facial landmark detection
and tracking algorithms have also been clinically validated in other applications such as: automatic
pain detection from facial images or videos of participants [98], [99], automatic detection of
patients with neurological disorders (e.g. ALS, stroke, PD, and AD) from healthy participants
using facial kinematics [39], [42], [100]–[103], and automatic detection of psychiatric conditions
(e.g. depression, and schizophrenia) [77], [104], [105]. In all these studies, the main goal was to
extract clinically interpretable objective facial features from the landmarks such as facial dynamics
and kinematic measures and then to use these measures to detect the presence of disease via
classification methods.
Another important goal of these measures is to track disease progression over time. For
that, correlations with clinical gold standards must be established. The studies predicting the
severity of symptoms have been limited, but important in the effort to clinically validate novel
assessment technologies. One study showed that kinematics of facial expressions was able to
predict the depression severity estimated via the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) [105].
The goal of this thesis is to further clinically validate the use of specific set of deep learning
algorithm (FAN and fine-tuned FAN) together with the Intel RealSense 3D camera for use in the
clinical setting for detecting severity of orofacial impairments within clinical groups.
Summary: Motivation and Gap

Detection of most neurodegenerative diseases are highly dependent to the clinicians’
experience and are not well standardized [15]. This may lead to delayed diagnosis, and therefore,
delayed interventions with reduced possibilities to slow down the rapid reduction of quality of life
for the patients. Recent advances in computational power and deep learning algorithms have
provided an opportunity to develop potential solutions for automated assessment and early
detection of neurological disorders in clinic and remotely, in home settings.

13


Previous studies have established the Intel RealSense 3D camera’s technical verification
and shown its high accuracy and performance on bench top experiments [34], [91]. Some studies
have also shown analytical and clinical validation of the Intel RealSense 3D camera [29], [43],
[73], [94]. Other studies have used facial landmark algorithms to perform clinically relevant facial
analysis [41], [42], [73], [106]. However, the combination of the Intel RealSense 3D camera with
FAN to perform objective orofacial assessments in clinical groups with facial impairments have
not been fully evaluated in a context of V3 framework before. This research thesis focuses on the
analytical and clinical validation of the proposed technology (the Intel RealSense 3D camera and
FAN) with the aim of advancing its translation into clinical settings.
Research Question and Objectives
The ultimate objective of this research is to implement an affordable, clinically valid, easyto-use video-based application to support the detection and assessment of orofacial deficits in
neurological diseases in order to support clinicians with the disease diagnosis and prognosis. The
objective of this thesis is to analytically and clinically validate the use of an accessible 3D camera
and a machine learning-based facial landmark tracking method for extracting kinematic facial
features that are representative of the perceptual clinical scores provided by trained clinicians to
individuals with orofacial impairments due to neurological disorders (e.g., post-stroke and ALS).
In study 1, analytical validation was determined by comparing the kinematic signals extracted from
the proposed system (the Intel RealSense 3D camera and FAN) against the gold standard
technology – a sensor-based system EMA. In study 2, clinical validity was determined using
correlations between the objective kinematic features and perceptual clinical assessments
performed by an expert clinician. In the first study (Chapter 2) regarding analytical validation, we
hypothesize that the newly proposed system would perform as well as the accepted gold standard
method (EMA) with high correlation between the two methods. In the second study (Chapter 3)
about clinical validation, we hypothesized that the kinematic features and corresponding
perceptual clinical scores – symmetry, range of motion (ROM), and speed – would be significantly
correlated with corresponding clinical scores and could be used to predict the severity of oromotor
impairment.

Chapter 2 – Analytical Validation

2.0 Abstract
Facial movements provide valuable information for the assessment of orofacial functions
such as speech, swallowing, and facial expressions. Due to the high cost and cumbersome setup
procedures, sensor-based technologies have not been adopted in the clinical settings. With the
recent advancements in markerless facial tracking algorithms as well as developments of 3D
cameras, it has become possible to develop viable clinical applications for the diagnosis and
treatment of motor speech disorders. In this study, we assessed the performance of a video-based
system composed of a deep neural net facial tracking algorithm coupled with Intel RealSense
camera to track the lip movements. The validation was performed on three healthy control
participants performing a set of speech and non-speech tasks similar to a routine orofacial
assessment conducted in clinical settings. Preliminary results show moderate to strong correlation
between this method and the gold standard sensor based electromagnetic articulography. Further
algorithm training and tests are needed in order to improve the performance of such system in
order to develop an accurate clinical tool for the assessment of orofacial movements.
2.1 Introduction

Accurate assessment of orofacial movements is important for the diagnosis and treatment
of various neurological disorders. Changes in the movement of the lips and the jaw have been
associated with early signs of oromotor decline, disease progression, and speech intelligibility
decline in neurological diseases such as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), Parkinson’s disease
(PD), and stroke [107], [108]. Sensor-based technologies such as electromagnetic articulography
(EMA) and optical tracking systems are well established methods for the quantitative assessment
of orofacial movements. However, such technologies are not clinically feasible due to their high
cost, system complexity, and long and tedious setup procedures. As a result, the quantitative
analysis of orofacial movement has been limited to research purposes and not integrated into
clinical practice in neurology or speech-language pathology [23], [40], [92], [109]. There is a need
for an inexpensive, easy-to-use, and clinically feasible orofacial movement tracking system that is
able to accurately and quickly quantify patient performance in a clinical setting. Recent advances
in computational power and deep learning algorithms coupled with the development of 3D depth
cameras have provided an opportunity to develop affordable, markerless, automated, and clinically
objective solutions for the assessment of neurological disorders affecting orofacial functions [41],
[93], [110], [111].

16

The evaluation of 3D cameras as potential tools for facial tracking has begun by the
research community. Ouni and Dahmani assessed the performance and precision of two 3D
markerless systems (Primesense and Intel RealSense cameras) with respect to an optoelectronic
marker-based system in sentence and syllable productions. They used the commercial software
Faceshift to track the virtual markers manually placed on the 3D mesh of the facial model in a
post-processing step. The outcome of the study proved overall good performance and low RMSE
for both markerless systems, with Intel RealSense 3D depth camera being slightly more accurate
than the Primesense (RMSE < 2 mm) [92].  Bandini et al. evaluated the accuracy of Primesense
depth sensor alongside InteraFace facial landmark tracking software package [112] for 3D tracking
of lip movements in various speech tasks with RMSE range of 1-4 mm [93]. In a later study,
Bandini et al. were able to discriminate people with PD from healthy control participants using
lower lip movement during syllable repetitions under the same video-based settings and confirmed
that patients have reduced articulatory kinematics [41]. Bandini et al. also validated the videobased tracking of jaw movements with Intel RealSense SR300 camera and the RealSense SDK R3
face tracking algorithm against the EMA during speech with the similar accuracy (average RMSE
~ 2 mm) [40]. Other preliminary work has focused on and shown high promise for utilizing opensource video-based facial landmark tracking algorithms in clinical assessments of individuals with
orofacial deficits, demonstrating high accuracy (up to 87%) in detecting orofacial impairments
[110], [111]. As such, the role of computer vision research to study human face for clinical
applications has thrived over the past few years, with many applications in neurological diseases
[41], [98], [110], [111]. Despite a significant promise to revolutionize neurological assessment,
further research is needed to validate the use of deep learning facial landmark tracking models in
assessing orofacial movements. Markerless methods use various computer vision algorithms and
machine learning models to retrieve and track facial landmarks in video data. As a result, the
quality of the tracking is highly dependent on the tracking model used [92]. The goal of this study
was to evaluate the feasibility of state-of-the-art markerless video-based system, as compared to
the gold standard EMA for tracking lip movements. The markerless system consisted of a pretrained convolutional neural network (CNN) model for facial landmark tracking as well as a 3D
depth camera registered with the video camera in order to translate 2D facial landmarks tracked
by the CNN model into 3D locations.

2.2 Method
2.2.1 Experimental Setup and Procedure

Three healthy young female volunteers were recruited for the experiment. The
demographic details of participants is provided on Table A-1 in Appendix A1. The participants
were recorded via 3D (video and depth) camera as well as EMA system while performed a series
of speech and non-speech tasks typically used by clinicians in a routine assessment of orofacial
and speech function. The tasks were expanded to include conditions (e.g., fast, slow) which are
known to enhance performance variability within a speaker. The tasks and conditions were:
Sentence repetition: The sentence “Buy Bobby a Puppy” was repeated five times at normal and
slow speaking rates, both with normal loudness.
Max opening: The mouth was opened to the maximum comfortable range and then closed to the
rest position, five times. Four variations of the task were performed, namely, (1) Hold: where
participants held the maximum opening for five seconds, (2) Normal: maximum opening followed
by closing at a normal speed, (3) Fast: the task was performed as fast as possible, and (4)
Asymmetric: opened the mouth asymmetrically to one side (left or right).
Max spread: The lips were spread out to the sides without opening, starting from the rest position
without a pause (Normal), or with five seconds pause (Hold) at the maximum spread, repeated five
times.

The recordings were performed in a quiet room, with a single constant and uniform light
source placed behind the camera. Participants were seated during the experiment looking straight
at the camera. Head movements were monitored and large head movements during experimental
recordings were avoided. The EMA sensors were placed using clear surgical tape on the
participants’ face prior to data acquisition, and the camera was adjusted to be at each participant’s
eye level. The Wave electromagnetic generator was located on the right side of participants’ head,
while the 3D camera was placed at a distance of 0.3-0.4 m away from the tip of the nose.

18

2.2.2 Data Acquisition

1) The Wave speech research system (NDI – Waterloo, Ontario, Canada) was used to track
lip movements. The Wave system consists of an electromagnetic field generator and sensors that
report 5 degrees-of-freedom position information within the set boundaries of the generated EM
field with high tracking accuracy (average error = 0.8 mm), thus, making it the “gold standard” for
validation of video-based systems [109]. Eight sensors were attached to the participants’ faces in
the following positions:
N1: placed at the intersection of the frontal and nasal bone
N2: placed at the tip of the nose.
LU (LB): placed at the upper and the vermilion of the upper and lower lips, respectively.
LR (LL): placed at the right and left corners of the mouth, respectively.
JR (JL): placed on the inferior of the jaw about one quarter of the distance from the
gnathion and the right (left) gonion.

The 3D position of the sensors over time (trajectories) were collected at the sampling
frequency of 100 Hz. Data were recorded and stored using the Wavefront software v.1.1.

2) The video-based markerless system consists of an Intel RealSense SR300 (Intel Corp.,
Santa Clara, CA, USA) camera that acquires registered color and depth video recordings, as well
as the FAN model [80]. FAN is an open-source CNN model used to automatically detect the face,
localize 68 facial landmarks, and track them throughout the duration of the videos.

The videos were collected at the resolution of 640×480 pixels at 30 frames per second (fps)
rate with color (RGB) and depth recording occurring simultaneously. Videos were recorded using
custom C++ code.

19

2.2.3 Data Analysis

1) Pre-processing: A custom Python script was developed to process and analyze data
obtained from each of the two data recording methods used. Because both EMA sensor recordings
and the video recordings randomly drop a few data points resulting in inconsistencies within the
sampling rate, both data streams were resampled at a uniform rate of 100 Hz using linear
interpolation. A low-pass Butterworth filter (8-poles, with cut-off frequency of 15 Hz) was used
on the trajectories of the points of interests (landmarks and sensor trajectories) to eliminate jitter.

2) Kinematic measures: As interpretable decisions are crucial in clinical applications, the
measures of range of motion and average speed, aligned with typical clinical judgements, were
selected for the comparisons.

The range of motion (ROM) was estimated vertically and horizontally. The vertical range
of motion (VRM) was calculated as the Euclidean distance (in mm) between LU and LB (Wave)
and the distance between landmarks 52 and 58 Figure 2-1. The horizontal range of motion (HRM)
was calculated as the Euclidean distance between lip corners - RC and LC (Wave) and the distance
between landmarks 49 and 55 (refer to Figure 2-1). The velocity signals (in mm/s) were computed
as the first derivative of the calculated displacements with respect to time.
3) Validation: The phase correlation in Fourier space [113] was used to align the signals
obtained from each method for all the recordings. The zero-lag cross correlation (CC0) was
computed to measure the agreement between the two signals obtained from each of the methods
for both displacement and velocity features extracted. These measures were calculated for each
task and then averaged across the three participants. The tasks were then parsed into individual
repetitions and the maximum value of ROM and average speed were calculated for each repetition
of the two methods used. The Spearman’s correlation (SC) coefficient was then calculated for each
task across all the repetitions for each participant and across the three participants.

2.4 Discussion
This preliminary study showed high correlations between the EMA and video-based
tracking systems with zero-lag cross-correlation values > 0.90 for non-speech tasks and >0.81 for
the speech repetition task, when looking at the entire signals. However, the correlation values were
smaller when extracting the maximum ROM and average speed values on signals parsed by
repetition to obtain discrete, clinically relevant measures. This could potentially be due to the jitter
in the facial landmarks obtained using FAN [80], particularly when calculating the correlation
between velocity values. In future studies, additional signal processing or improved facial
landmarks tracking will need to be considered to reduce frame-to-frame jitter. The correlation
values were higher across participants as opposed to within each participant’s repetitions of the
task. This is due to the fact that the variability of kinematic values were low within each
participants’ repetitions compared to the trend across all the participant’s data.
As illustrated in Figure 2-2, there was an expected bias in the signals obtained from the
two methods. This bias was due to the fact that the two methods measured different distances: the
ground truth signal (blue) was the distance between two sensor locations, while the FAN tracking
signal (red) was the distance between two automatically detected facial landmarks (lip edges). The
positions of these reference points were naturally not the same. In addition, some discrepancies
between the signals are likely to be due to the sampling rate difference between the two methods,
as the video were recorded at a lower sampling rate than the EMA data (30 fps compared to 100
Hz). Future work should aim to record data at the minimum sampling rate of 50 Hz, as
recommended by Ouni and Dahmani [92], to enable fine modeling of lip movements.
Figure 2-3 shows that the video-based system was able to successfully differentiate
between different conditions within individuals (i.e., as expected, the fast condition showed
smaller ROM and greater average speed) for the maximum open task. Additionally, it was able to
differentiate between speakers, as some participants naturally use larger or smaller movements
than other participants. This differentiation is clinically relevant as clinicians are tasked to visually
identify the movement ROM and speed. These perceptual judgements have been known to have
reduced reliability, and the potential use of video-based methods to distinguish individual
differences is highly promising.

25

Clear advantages of the video-based tracking have emerged. First, the acquisition volume
for the EMA – determined by the Wave field generator placed near the participant’s head – was
smaller than the adjustable and larger volume of video-based tracking. In EMA, even small head
movements away from the generator’s field resulted in the loss of tracking by the sensors. We had
to closely monitor the head position of the participants in the EMA field. However, such small
movements were accounted for in the video tracking via automated face detection and the detection
of facial landmarks so long as the face remained within view of the camera, thus, avoiding the
complete loss of data. Furthermore, electromagnetic tracking was limited by the number of sensors
placed on the face and highly reduced/limited movement information available for EMA, while
the current state-of-the-art facial tracking algorithm tracked large number of facial features (e.g.
68 landmarks). Although we extracted relatively small number of highly straightforward features,
the work on the most important feature is still ongoing [42].
In conclusion, this study has demonstrated that the video-based tracking using the FAN
[80] has a great potential in tracking facial features relative to the gold standards. Further
improvements to its ability to detect facial landmarks needs to establish across a large number of
participants with and without neurological disease and across tasks. This ongoing work can help
in accurate tracking of facial movements, thus, providing clinicians with guidance in the
assessment of neurological disorders.

Chapter 3 – Clinical Validation Study

3.0 Abstract

This study sought to determine whether clinically interpretable kinematic features
extracted automatically from 3 dimensional (3D) videos were correlated with corresponding
perceptual clinical orofacial ratings in individuals with orofacial impairments due to neurological
disorders. 45 participants (19 diagnosed with motor neuron diseases (MND), and 26 post-stroke)
performed two non-speech tasks (mouth opening and lip spreading) and one speech task (repetition
of a sentence “Buy Bobby a Puppy”) while being video recorded in a standardized lab setting. The
colour video recordings of participants were assessed by an expert clinician – a speech language
pathologist - on the severity of three orofacial measures: symmetry, range of motion (ROM), and
speed. Clinically interpretable 3D kinematic features, linked to symmetry, ROM and speed, were
automatically extracted from video recordings, using a deep facial landmark detection and tracking
algorithm, for each of the three tasks. Spearman correlations were used to identify features that
were significantly correlated (p-value < .05) with their corresponding clinical scores. Clinically
significant kinematic features were then used in the subsequent multivariate regression models to
predict the overall orofacial impairment severity score. Several kinematic features extracted from
3D video recordings were associated with their corresponding perceptual clinical scores, indicating
clinical validity of these automatically derived measures. Different patterns of significant features
were observed between MND and post-stroke groups; these differences were aligned with clinical
expectations in both cases. The results show that kinematic features extracted automatically from
simple clinical tasks can capture characteristics used by clinicians during assessments. These
findings support the clinical validity of video-based automatic extraction of kinematic features.

3.1 Introduction

Neurological disorders often affect orofacial and speech functions [6]. For example, people
post-stroke or living with motor neuron diseases (MND) often experience orofacial motor
impairments (i.e., inability to produce facial expressions or perform volitional orofacial
movements), as well as dysarthria, a motor speech disorder [114], [115]. Accurate assessment of
orofacial speech and non-speech movements becomes important for effective disease detection,
tracking progression, and monitoring treatment outcomes [15].
Existing orofacial assessment methods rely on either subjective clinical evaluation
performed by experts or on the use of complex and expensive sensor-based techniques, such as
electromagnetic articulography (EMA), that are not suitable for integration into current clinical
workflows. Emerging video-based methods may offer easy-to-use and cost-effective solutions to

27

support objective clinical assessment of orofacial motor impairments. The development of 3D
video cameras and deep learning algorithms has enabled accurate automatic detection and tracking
of pre-defined facial landmarks in facial images [80], which is the first essential step for many
clinical facial analysis applications.
In order to be adopted into a clinical setting, novel facial tracking technologies must be
evaluated within established frameworks for digital biomarker development, such as the FDA
BEST (FDA, 2021) criteria and the V3 framework [89]. The V3 framework advocates for a 3-part
sequential biomarker evaluation procedure: (1) verification of sensor-level outputs, typically
conducted by manufacturers, followed by (2) analytical validation of a novel method with respect
to an already-validated technical gold standards, followed by (3) clinical validation to assess utility
in a specific clinical population and relative to existing clinical standards/ practices. The technical
verification of the facial tracking technologies has been previously well established [29], [34],
[91]. The analytical validation of markerless methods has demonstrated promising results as well.
Ouni and Dahmani found that facial tracking methods using two different 3D cameras, namely,
Intel RealSense (Intel Corporation, Santa Clara, California, USA) and PrimeSense Carmine
(PrimeSense, Tel Aviv, Israel) had good accuracy with respect to optoelectronic tracking, which
functioned as a sensor-based ground truth, specifically the Vicon system (Vicon Motion Systems
Ltd, Oxford, GB) [92]. Bandini et al. performed an analogous comparison between a 3D camera
(Intel RealSense) and an EMA system (NDI Wave Speech Research System – Waterloo, Ontario,
Canada), and demonstrated good accuracy of the camera compared to the Wave [40]. Harkel et al.
evaluated the intra- and inter-rater reliability and agreement of the Intel RealSense D415 camera
against a gold standard stereophotogrammetry imaging system, 3dMD, in patients with facial palsy
and found that the 3D camera was a viable option to be implemented in the context of an objective
clinical assessment [94]. Taken together, these previous studies have extensively demonstrated the
analytical validity of 3D camera-based facial tracking methods.

Beyond verification and analytical validation, the technology (both 3D camera and deep
learning algorithms) must be evaluated in clinical populations in order to fulfill the third step of
biomarker development. Previous work has shown promise for detecting orofacial impairments
associated with stroke [116], Parkinson’s disease [41], [101], and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis
(ALS) [42] relative to neurologically healthy controls, based on measures of facial kinematics

28

(e.g., velocity, acceleration, range of motion, symmetry, etc.). For example, Bandini et al used a
small feature set capturing movement symmetry, speed, and range of motion (ROM) of the lips
and jaw to automatically differentiate between post stroke patients and controls with 87% accuracy
[116]. In another study, a small number of 3D kinematic features of lip movement were used to
discriminate patients with ALS from healthy subjects with nearly 90% accuracy [42]. Neumann et
al. used a combination of kinematic features extracted from 2D video recordings together with
acoustic features extracted from audio data to distinguish patients with ALS at symptomatic versus
pre-symptomatic phases of bulbar disease as well as from the control participants [117].

The focus of previous studies was to investigate video-based methods’ use for classifying
a group of participants into either the healthy or clinical category. The studies predicting the
severity of symptoms have been limited, but important in the effort to clinically validate novel
assessment technologies. One study showed that kinematics of facial expressions was able to
predict the depression severity estimated via the Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II) [105]. In
another study, features were extracted from facial expression images of participants in order to
automatically differentiate among five levels of pain using AI-based models evaluated its efficacy
on two open-source datasets [118]. Additionally, the Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rating Scale
(UPDRS) motor score was predicted using acoustic features extracted from 5875 voice recordings
of 42 patients with PD with the final ensemble model predicting the clinical score with 99.6%
accuracy [119]. These promising results highlighted the need for more research to clinically
validate automated facial analysis technologies for their use as assessment tools, specifically for
predicting the clinical scores associated with severity of orofacial impairments. The ability to
predict disease severity using kinematic measures extracted from videos would provide evidence
of the clinical validity of the technology and support its use to enable tracking disease progression
as well as evaluating the effectiveness of intervention programs (e.g., quantifying functional
improvement post-stroke).
The first objective of this study was to establish clinical validity of a video-based system
in assessing the severity of the orofacial impairments by automatically extracting kinematic
features from video recordings of individuals with orofacial impairments either due to stroke or
MND and comparing them with respect to their corresponding clinical scores (e.g., clinical
estimates of symmetry/speed/ROM and kinematic measures of symmetry/speed/ROM). The

29

second objective was to determine whether a combination of kinematic features was able to predict
the overall severity of orofacial deficit in each patient group. We hypothesized that the selected
kinematic features and corresponding perceptual clinical scores would be significantly correlated
and could be used to predict the overall severity of orofacial impairment.

3.2.2 Clinical Assessment
A highly experienced speech-language pathologist specializing in the assessment of adults
with neurological diseases (> 10 years of clinical experience) watched the available video
recordings and rated them based on clinical measures of ROM, speed, symmetry, variability, and
fatigue of facial movements. The rater only knew the diagnosis of the participants prior to
performing the assessment and otherwise was blind to all other clinical information (e.g., scores
on other clinical assessments, site of onset, disease duration, etc.). Ratings were based on a 5-point
Likert scale with 1 indicating normal function and 5 indicating severe dysfunction. ROM, speed,
and symmetry scores were used as separate clinical outcomes; they were selected as they constitute
salient neuromuscular abnormalities in motor speech disorders [123]. The total score – a sum of
all 5 clinical ratings – was calculated and used to represent the overall orofacial deficit severity,
with score of 5 indicating normal function and score of 25 indicating the most severe dysfunction
(see [73]).
To determine the reliability of the clinician scores, the original examinator (intra-rater
reliability) and another trained examinator (a certified SLP; interrater reliability) re-scored videos
from 15% of the sample (7 participants chosen randomly; 21 recordings). To assess intra-rater
reliability, Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) estimates and their 95% confidence intervals
(CI) were calculated using SPSS statistical package version 23 (SPSS Inc, Chicago, IL) based on
a single-rating, absolute-agreement, 2-way mixed-effects model [124]. To assess inter-rater
agreement, ICC estimates and their 95% CI were calculated based on a single-rating, consistencyagreement, 2-way random-effects model [124]. A percentage of clinical agreement (percentage of
scores within 1-point difference) was also calculated to mitigate the effects of skewed distributions
or low variability of the scores in ICC values, if necessary. The intra-rater agreement was strong
(ICC = 0.966, CI = 0.908 – 0.987, p < .001; [125]). The inter-rater agreement was also excellent
(ICC = 0.996, CI = 0.990 -0.998, p < .001; [125]). All scores fell within 1-point difference for both
intra-rater and inter-rater reliability.
3.2.3 Data collection procedures

Video recordings were obtained while participants were seated in front of an Intel
RealSense depth camera (SR300 or D400) at approximately 30-60 cm distance away from the face

31

at the eye level height. The recordings were performed in a quiet room with a single source uniform
light illuminating the participants’ face. Each video recording was composed of a pair of color
(RGB) and depth videos. Both streams were recorded simultaneously at approximately 50 frames
per second at VGA resolution of 640×480 pixels.

Participants were asked to perform a set of standard speech and non-speech tasks
commonly used by clinicians during orofacial and motor speech examinations to assess the
severity of orofacial symptoms and dysarthria. The subjects were asked to avoid head and body
movements and look directly into the camera during recordings. The following set of tasks were
chosen for analysis as they captured the orofacial movements of mouth and jaw in both horizontal
and vertical directions and were affected in both clinical populations in our previous studies [23],
[39]:
•
10 repetitions of the sentence “Buy Bobby a Puppy” at a comfortable speaking
rate and loudness (BBP)
•
5 repetitions of maximum jaw opening and closing (OPEN)
•
5 repetitions of pretending to smile with tight lips (SPREAD)
Furthermore, we recorded 10 seconds of participants resting, while they maintained a neutral
facial expression (REST) with their teeth together but not clenched. This was done for distance
normalization purposes.

A total of 175 video recordings were included in the final analysis: 74 from MND, and 101
from PS. A total of 5 recordings were either missing or excluded. Reasons for exclusion of a video
recording for a task were: participant not performing the task (2 cases – in MND group, BBP task),
video recording issues, e.g., corruption of either the colour and/or depth stream data of video
recordings, or dropping of video frames (2 case – in PS group, BBP task), and failed accurate facial
landmark tracking due to poor lighting condition (1 case – in PS group, BBP task). Each of the
remaining videos, excluding the data for the REST task, were manually segmented into individual
repetitions by a trained research assistant (i.e., 5 segments for the non-speech tasks and 10
segments for the speech task).

3.2.4 Kinematic Data Analysis
Preprocessing

A fine-tuned version of the FAN [80] was used to extract 68 facial landmarks from colour
videos in 2 dimensions (2D) pixel values and track them throughout the duration of the videos (see
Figure 3-1). The 3D positions of landmarks were then calculated by aligning and sampling the
depth images at the same pixel coordinates as the landmark points identified on the color images.
The 3D real world coordinates of the landmarks (in meters) were calculated using the intrinsic
camera parameters together with aligning the depth and colour streams, thus, obtaining 3D
coordinates for each landmark, with X representing the lateral axis, Y representing the vertical
axis, and Z representing the frontal axis.

Figure 3-1 Facial landmarks obtained from the fine-tuned FAN. The landmarks used for extracting
kinematic features are named and highlighted in light blue dots. The red dashed lines are the kinematic
displacement measures extracted from specific facial landmark pairs.

It has been previously shown that the state-of-the-art deep neural facial landmark detection
models do not perform well when used on faces of older adults or patients with orofacial deficits
[73], [83]. To address this issue, the model was previously fine-tuned using 4340 images extracted
from video recordings of individuals from clinical populations as well as healthy older adults,
some of which were of a subset of the individuals in the present study, performing orofacial tasks
[126]. The normalized Root Mean Squared Error (NRMSE) of the fine-tuned model was reported

33

to be 4.7 ± 0.7 %, 6.2 ± 1.7 %, and 5.7± 1.1 % for healthy control, post-stroke group, and ALS
group, respectively [126]. The fine-tuned FAN model has been previously used to successfully
detect oromotor impairments associated with various neurological disorders [73], [83]. To check
the model’s accuracy, a video file was generated of participants performing each task with the
location of 2D landmarks superimposed on their faces. Each video was viewed to ensure adequate
detection and tracking of facial landmarks of interest.

Out of 68 facial landmarks, 10 landmarks from lips and jaw were considered in this study.
They included: Forehead (FH), left medial canthus of eye (LE), right medial canthus of eyes (RE),
upper vermillion lip border at mouth centre (UL), lower vermillion lip border (LL) at mouth centre,
left oral commissure (LC), right oral commissure (RC), centre jawline (CJ), left jawline (LJ), and
right jawline (RJ). To remove tracking noise and jitter, the trajectories of the landmarks of the
interests were lowpass filtered with an 8-pole Butterworth filter with cut-off frequency of 12 Hz
[73], [127].
Kinematic Features
Similar to previous studies, facial landmarks were used to derive clinically interpretable
and relevant kinematic features [42], [84]. These features are summarized in Table 3-2. Features
were selected to correspond to clinical judgements of ROM, speed and symmetry by the speech
language pathologist. A total of 20 kinematic features were linked to ROM, 24 to speed, and 9 to
symmetry. All the features have been used in previous studies [23], [101].
ROM Features
The 3D Euclidean distances between a set of facial landmarks were calculated to establish
lip opening, UL – LL; lip spread, RC – LC; movement of LL with respect to the FH reference
point; movement of RC with respect to RE; and movement of LC with respect to LE. In addition,
the total movement of the mouth was modelled by a sum of the areas made by two triangles defined
by lip landmarks {RC, UL, LL} and {LC, UL, LL}. Lip spread as well as other RC/LC measures
were established only for SPREAD, where horizontal movements were predominant. Since the
jaw rarely moves during SPREAD, none of the jaw-based features were considered for this task.
All the calculated distance features were normalized with respect to the corresponding values

34

calculated in the REST condition. After this normalization, ROM was determined as the maximum
value of each of these distances/areas, as well as the cumulative sum path length. This was done
for each repetition.
Speed Features
The first derivative of the above path distances with respect to time were calculated to
represent the velocity of the lips and jaw movements. The maximum, minimum, and average
values were considered.
 Symmetry Features

The symmetry of movement was characterized by (1) comparing the motion of the left and
right corners of the mouth with respect to the reference points LE and RE and (2) comparing the
areas on the right and left triangles defined by {RC, UL, LL} and {LC, UL, LL}, respectively. The
absolute differences, ratios, and concordance correlation coefficient (CCC [128]; as a measure of
agreement of two signals) between the computed values of the distances and mouth areas on the
right and left sides were calculated. Smaller CCC values corresponded to asymmetry during facial
movements. Similarly, the larger deviation of right/left ratios away from 1 indicated more
asymmetrical facial movements.

3.2.5 Statistical Analyses
The features described above were calculated for individual repetitions of all tasks. Prior
to further analyses, outliers were removed from the data using the inter-quartile range (IQR)
method [129]. In this method, the IQR of the repetitions were calculated, then multiplied by 2.5,
and added/subtracted from the third/first quartiles. The values per repetition of each task that fell
outside this range were considered outliers and were eliminated from the set. In total, 4.7% of the
repetitions were discarded as outliers (n=51). The arithmetic mean of kinematic feature values
were calculated cross the remaining repetitions per subject, per task. All subsequent analyses were
conducted separately for each task (OPEN, SPREAD, BBP) and group (MND vs. post-stroke).
The Spearman correlation coefficients were calculated between each kinematic feature and
their corresponding perceptual clinical scores. The Spearman rho (r) is used with ordinal data and
measures the degree of monotonic relationship between two variables; here, in accordance with
accepted guidelines, values between 0 to ±0.20 were considered as negligible, ±0.21 to ±0.40 as
weak, ±0.41 to ±0.60 as moderate, ±0.61 to ±0.80 as strong, and ±0.81 to ±1.00 as very strong
[130]. The significant level for each correlation was set to 0.05 to reflect that these correlations
were primarily used as a screening method for subsequent regression analyses.

36

Kinematic features with statistically significant correlations were included in the
subsequent multivariable linear regression models with the total orofacial clinical score as the
outcome. The Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) with threshold of greater than 10 was used to assess
the collinearity between covariates [131]. If the VIF for the variables were higher than the set
threshold, the features with highest VIF were eliminated iteratively until the remaining variables
all had VIF values less than 10. The normality of residuals was tested by plotting the Q-Q norm of
the residuals. The linearity of fitted regression models was also evaluated by plotting the residual
values against the kinematic features data. The adjusted R2, adjusted for the number of predictors,
was reported for the final fitted regression models.

3.4 Discussion
In this exploratory study, we investigated clinical validity of a 3D camera and vision-based
facial tracking algorithm as an objective assessment tool for patients with neurological disorders
– MND and PS. A relatively small, clinically interpretable lip and jaw kinematic feature set was
delineated from the outset; the features were chosen to conceptually represent the measures used
by clinicians to assess orofacial deficits during the oromotor examinations of non-speech and
speech tasks. The study revealed that subsets of kinematic features were significantly correlated
with their corresponding clinical scores for each clinical group. The sets of statistically significant
features that were able to explain a significant portion of data variability in the overall severity
scores in most models were unique for each of the clinical subgroups. Overall, the findings
demonstrated the potential of automated video-based systems for extraction of kinematic features
in the context of orofacial neurological assessment and established their initial validity as clinical
tools.
3.4.1 Facial kinematics in MND and PS
In total, there were 16 statistically significant correlations (out of 50, 32%) across all three
tasks for the MND group. In the PS group, 23 individual correlations (8 for SPREAD and 15 for
BBP) of kinematic features with clinical scores were significant (46%). Although less than 50%
of correlations were significant, and those that were significant ranged between 0.40 and 0.74
(moderate to good), the results were overall encouraging.  Smaller correlations and model fits have
been expected based on previous literature exploring relationships between kinematics and other
measurement domains, including perceptual and acoustic (see Lee et al., 2018; Rong et al., 2012;
Teplansky et al., 2022).
However, the findings of this study aligned strongly with those of previous work with
regards to facial kinematics and oromotor phenomena in both MND and PS groups. In the MND
group, our findings were consistent with previous literature describing abnormal jaw and lip
movements [42], [127], [135], [136]. MND has been characterized by weakness in the orofacial
musculature [137] and, expectedly, ROM and speed measures were noted to be substantially more
affected by MND than symmetry (see Shellikeri et al., 2016). As a result, we observed significant

43

correlations between the clinical scores indicating the impairment severity and kinematic features
primarily related to ROM and speed.
In the PS group, facial paresis, resulting in facial asymmetry with a unilateral muscle
impairment of lower half of the face, causing asymmetric smile and other facial gestures, is one of
the most common impairments following a stroke [116], [139]. As anticipated, the symmetry
features played a prominent role in the PS group, and our finding showed significant correlations
between kinematically assessed symmetry and the corresponding symmetry clinical scores. In both
clinical groups, the agreement between clinical characteristics sought by experts to describe the
unique clinical representation of orofacial deficits and corresponding kinematic features supported
the overall impression of clinical validity of the video-based movement assessment.
3.4.2 Task differences
Differences also emerged in this study between tasks. In the MND group, kinematic
measures of ROM and speed were linked to the corresponding clinical scores in non-speech OPEN
and SPREAD tasks, but not in the speech task-BBP. The findings for non-speech tasks were
anticipated based on past research [42]. Both tasks are classified as maximum performance tasks,
yet clinically they serve different purposes. While OPEN primarily is used to judge the integrity
of jaw depressor/elevator muscles, SPREAD is used to judge the integrity of intrinsic facial
muscles. Previously, kinematic features extracted from lower lip and jaw in SPREAD and OPEN
were used to differentiate patients with ALS from healthy control participants with 83% and 72%
accuracy, respectively [42]. The findings regarding the speech task were somewhat unexpected,
as previous studies had shown BBP to be highly sensitive to the presence of the motor neuron
disease [23], [127]. Current results for BBP may be due to the challenges associated with clinical
(visuo-perceptual) assessment of speech versus non-speech movements by SLPs. BBP is a
relatively fast task and as such it might be more challenging for clinicians to rank its clinical
characteristics (ROM, speed, and symmetry) on a 5-point scale. Further, only 2D colour video
recordings (no audio) were viewed by the expert for clinical judgement of BBP, which might have
resulted in a loss of some information (e.g., rate/slowness) essential for forming the overall clinical
impression. Limited sample size and high variability (due to factors such as heterogeneity of
disease presentation) may have also contributed to these results.

44

In the PS group, results for the vertical opening (OPEN) contrasted with the horizontal
opening (SPREAD) and speech (BBP) task. None of the kinematic features were correlated with
clinical scores in OPEN, while sets of features were correlated with the clinical scores in SPREAD
and BBP. The lack of correlation among features and the clinical scores in OPEN may be due to
the task being mainly used to assess the integrity of the jaw (i.e., cranial nerve V) and not facial
muscles, which might be differentially affected post-stroke [116]. The SPREAD task showed more
notable effect as it intended to reveal the integrity of facial muscles (i.e., cranial nerve VII).
Although relatively mild, this type of impairment predominated in our PS sample. Facial features
during BBP also revealed a link with the clinical symmetry score in these patients. Similar results
for SPREAD and BBP with respect to their sensitivity to orofacial impairment post-stroke were
reported in previous work (Bandini, Green, Richburg, et al., 2018). Overall, with the exception of
BBP task in the MND group, task effects observed clinically and kinematically in this study
demonstrated results consistent with previous research and, by extension, supported the potential
for clinical validity of the tested assessment method.
3.4.3 Modeling overall orofacial impairment severity
A combination of kinematic features was able to predict the overall orofacial impairment
severity score in each clinical group and some tasks. This was important to examine as the overall
severity score combined clinical impressions across different specific observations that might be
difficult to rank separately and individually. In the MND group, significant models were noted for
OPEN and SPREAD. All but one individual variable (LL_Max) contributed to the overall model
in OPEN explaining a substantial degree of variability of the total clinical score. The value of the
coefficients generally suggested that as the severity increased, the values of the ROM and speed
of the lips and jaw were reduced. The SPREAD model was significant, but none of the kinematic
variables significantly contributed to explaining the model’s overall variance. This may suggest
that the SPREAD was not an ideal task for feature extraction in this group, as previous research
suggested that the greatest impairment in MND affects the vertical movement plane of the jaw and
lips [136].
In the PS group, in contrast with MND, most of the features were significant in the
regression model for SPREAD, with kinematic features explaining over half of the variance in the

45

data. The results of this study are promising as the variability of the kinematic measures within
and in between subjects in the study were considerable. The value of the coefficients revealed that
as the severity increased, the lip movement became more reduced and asymmetrical. In BBP task,
total mouth area and the maximum mouth opening velocity were the two statistically significant
features with the model being able to explain substantial amount of the variance in the data. These
features align with clinicians’ judgement as they assess the overall mouth movement and speed of
movement during a fast task such as BBP. Overall, the features related to symmetrical movement
of the mouth demonstrated to be of the most importance aligned with the clinical perceptual
assessment of this clinical group, further strengthening the validity of this method for future
automatic objective assessment.
3.4.4 Limitations and future directions
This work had several limitations. The primary limitation was a relatively small sample
size and differences in sample sizes of the two clinical groups, limiting our ability to fully compare
group results. Another limiting factor was a relatively small severity range in both clinical samples.
We capitalized on the greater variability available in our samples based on specific tasks instead.
In addition, the kinematic features extracted were averaged across the repetitions for each subject
per task and thus variability of the facial movements during each repetition of the task were not
considered. Future work will extend on the results obtained in the study, focus on gathering data
from a larger/ balanced groups of participants with more severe orofacial impairments as well as
developing predictive models using timeseries of facial landmarks instead of averaging kinematic
feature values across repetitions. This work will be essential to develop and integrate modern
digital technologies into clinical care as well as outcome measures for clinical trials [88].
The present work had limitations to acknowledge and to address in future studies. The
primary limitation was a relatively small sample size and differences in sample sizes of the two
clinical groups, limiting our ability to fully compare group results. Small sample sizes remain an
issue for many clinicals studies pre and during the pandemic, which expedited the transition of this
type of work into the domain of remote data collections/ assessment, for which the current study
results are of a particular importance. Further, the intra- and inter-rater reliability was performed
on a small sub-sample (15% of the sample). Although it is relatively standard to evaluate

46

approximately that amount of data to establish reliability, the sample there may have been small,
introducing the possibility of perceptual bias. Nevertheless, we observed small confidence
intervals in the reliability analyses, which supported the adequacy of this method for its present
purposes (i.e., evaluating the performance of raters at a sample level, rather than at a participant
level).
Another limiting factor was a relatively small severity range in both clinical samples. We
capitalized on the greater variability available in our samples based on specific tasks instead.  In
addition, the kinematic features extracted were averaged across the repetitions for each subject
per task and thus variability of the facial movements during each repetition of the task were not
considered.
Future work will extend the results obtained in this study, while focusing on gathering data
from a larger/balanced groups of participants with more severe orofacial impairments as well as
developing predictive models using timeseries of facial landmarks instead of averaging kinematic
feature values across repetitions. This work will be essential to develop and integrate modern
digital technologies into clinical care as well as outcome measures for clinical trials [88]. This
study laid the foundation for larger scale studies with larger sample size across different cohorts
to ensure clinical validity of the proposed technology as well as generalizability of the results.
3.5 Conclusion
The result of this study showed that the automatic extraction of facial kinematic features
from facial video recordings of patients performing standard orofacial assessments is a viable
solution for an objective and continuous assessment of facial function. Here we demonstrated
encouraging support for the validity of our automated assessment method for detecting the severity
of kinematic facial impairments. Development of such systems and their validation with larger
datasets holds great promise for the development of clinical tools that can help clinicians
objectively assess oromotor and speech disorder and track progression of neurological diseases.

Chapter 4 – General Discussion

Need for Automatic Objective Orofacial Assessments
The goal of this thesis was to validate the use of 3D camera together with deep facial
landmark tracking algorithms (the FAN and the fine-tuned FAN) to extract orofacial features
aimed at providing objective clinically interpretable measures/ biomarkers to guide clinical
decision making in diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment management of neurological disorders.
Neurological disorders are heterogenous in their clinical presentation, making them challenging to
diagnose [140]. Furthermore, the lack of standards to follow in the clinical setting, and subjectivity
of the existing methods used by experts can result in delays in obtaining diagnosis and delayed
interventions and treatments. As a result, the clinical management and patient experience is
negatively impacted [141].
Studies have shown that subtle changes in speech and orofacial movements may be early
signs of disease [8]. The use of markerless video-based technologies can provide clinicians with
objective automatic analysis of facial movements and speech patterns that are non-invasive, cost
effective, and possibly sensitive to subtle changes that are not noticed by the clinicians [127]. In
fact, video-based technologies have been used in orofacial assessments to differentiate between
groups of individuals with various neurological diseases and healthy control participants [39], [40],
[43], [106], [127], as well as differentiating among various stages of neurological diseases [117],
[127]. Therefore, development of such technologies can help support clinicians in the assessment,
diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment planning processes.
AI-Vision based Automatic Assessment

Machine learning and deep learning techniques are the dominant methods used in
automatically analyzing videos in the field of computer vision. New architectures with higher
accuracy are being actively developed in this fast-paced research community. While the current
top performing algorithms may report high accuracy relative to manual human annotators on the
dataset that they were trained on, they perform worse on datasets that they were not trained on
[142]. The other limitation of such algorithms is their less-than-optimal performance in cases of
occlusions such as glasses, facial hair, extreme poses, or diverse environmental conditions such as

48

lighting [45]. These events are extremely difficult to control, and they can compromise the data
quality. In our work, we opt to perform data collection in a semi-structured environment, when a
research assistant or clinician instructs a patient/ participant to avoid occlusion and maintain
optimized parameters for the duration of the recording session.
In addition, most of these models are supervised methods that are trained on large amount
of labelled data that may be inherently biased. In particular, landmark detection and tracking
algorithms are mainly trained on the online available pictures of faces, which mostly consist of
young subjects with light skin tone [143]. A thorough analysis of available datasets have shown
that these corpuses are indeed sex/ gender, race, and age imbalanced [142]. Therefore, as expected,
off-the-shelf models are often biased towards older participants, darker skin tone, and patient
groups [86], [102], [144]. Training such algorithms on clinical data alone is not practical and
unrealistic as the new state-of-the-art deep learning algorithms require large corpus of training data
(in the order of thousands to millions of samples). Since most clinical studies have relatively small
sample size (~10-150 participants per class) and strict restrictions on data sharing broadly among
the research community, fine-tuning based on these samples remains challenging. Nevertheless,
technical solutions can be found to address some of the limitations and the inherent biases in these
algorithms. Guarin et al. have shown that using examples from only 40 subjects in a controlled
environment may be sufficient to improve the performance of the FAN model that was previously
shown to be biased towards the older and/or clinical participants [145]. Fine-tuning is one of the
effective methods of de-biasing existing state-of-the-art AI models [145]. Taati et al have also
successfully applied this method to algorithms for facial tracking landmarks and have shown
improved performance on tracking of facial features of older adults with dementia [102].
AI-based applications should be evaluated for their validity prior to their adoption. This
work used the well-established V3 framework [89] for the validation of digital biomarkers to
evaluate the validity of the proposed video-based technology. As the technical verification of the
used technology was previously established [34], [91], this work aimed to further validate this
technology both analytically, relative to a technical gold standard, and clinically, relative to an
assessment by an expert clinician. Ultimately, robust clinically verified AI-based models need to
be externally validated on a clinical cohort that adequately represents the target patient population
from various collection sites in order to reduce the risks of overfitting and biases [146].

49

Analytical Validation
In the first study of this thesis, the proposed technology was analytically validated relative
to the gold standard highly precise and accurate technology – EMA. It was a pilot study that
involved data collection from three healthy adults performing three standard assessment tasks
(OPEN, SPREAD, BBP) at varying conditions (e.g. fast, slow, normal, and asymmetrical). The
original intent was to expand the data set to more participants; however, the data collection was
impacted by the COVID pandemic, and the study had to be stopped due to the inability to move to
a remote data collection mode. This study is currently in progress – a data collection for 20
participants has been completed and will be analysed elsewhere.
The kinematic features extracted in the pilot study, namely ranges of motion/ speeds etc.
associated with mouth opening and lips spreading during speech and non-speech tasks were
compared between the two technologies using the zero-lag cross-correlations and Spearman
correlations. The zero-lag cross correlation measure showed strong agreement between the two
methods when comparing the entire kinematic signal per repetition. However, Spearman
correlations of the calculated averages and maximum values of kinematic features showed mostly
moderate correlations. Two conclusions were drawn from the results of this preliminary study.
First, the 30 frames rate per second of the video camera can be a limiting factor since fast facial
movements may not be captured. Ouni et al. recommended a minimum of 50 frames per second to
ensure capturing all the critical information for fast movements such as speech [92]. Second, the
jitteriness of the landmarks in the video resulted in “noisy” distance signals and high variability of
kinematic values extracted. In addition, this study demonstrated that there was high variability in
the range of orofacial kinematics of orofacial movements performed by participants both within
and in-between subjects. The high variance needs to be considered before making conclusions
regarding the results of studies using such technologies. Finally, the strengths of the correlations
were also dependant on the participants. The highest correlations were obtained on the participant
with the lightest skin tone, which is aligned with the findings of previous studies on the inherent
biases of deep facial landmark detection algorithms [86], [102], [106], [144], [145].
This study also revealed some of the challenges and limitations of the proposed system.
The RealSense video camera sometimes dropped video frames, which resulted in overall lower

50

video quality. A new recording software was developed in the lab to address this problem and
inform the researchers if a specific recording needed to be repeated, based on the number of
dropped samples and overall low recording quality. The off-the-shelf FAN model used in this
study showed high sensitivity to environmental factors such as variation in lighting as well as skin
tone of participants. As such, fine-tuning procedures were established to improve FAN’s
performance and were used in the clinical validation study and elsewhere in our research.
The main limitation of this study was the small sample size (n=3). Ongoing work focuses
on full sample (n=17) evaluation of landmark detection and tracking algorithm on the data
collected from EMA, 3D RealSense camera, and a laptop webcam [147]. The result of this study
will be reported elsewhere and will include measures of inter-method agreement, test-retest
consistency, and reliability of such technologies for performing automatic objective orofacial
assessments.
Clinical Validation
In the second study, the proposed technology was clinically validated in the MND and
Post-stroke groups. Participants performed the same tasks as first study (OPEN, SPREAD, BBP)
while being recorded in a controlled lab environment with a 3D camera. In this study, a fine-tuned
model of the landmark detection and tracking algorithm on clinical data [85], [145] was used to
automatically extract clinically relevant kinematic features from lips and jaw movements. A set of
features in each clinical group showed moderate to good correlation with the perceptual clinical
scores given by the expert clinician. The statistically significant features within each task and
clinical subgroup were representative of clinical characteristics anticipated by the group. The
regression models built by the significant features were able to explain a good amount of variability
within the data. High within and between subject variability was also observed in the extracted
kinematic features in each of the categories of the severity scale, which highlights the challenges
associated with heterogeneity of clinical characteristics and presentations across clinical
populations. A recent longitudinal study of post-stroke participants established a high internal
consistency and reliability of such technologies with the ability to identify individual and taskdependent differences over time [148].

51

In this study, we only focused on three out of the five available clinical scores; namely,
ROM, speed, and symmetry. There were no objective kinematic measures defined/extracted for
the two remaining scores of variability and fatigue. However, these measures are also important
in characterizing neurological diseases. For example, individuals with ALS have reported fatigue
as the most prevalent symptom occurring in 83% - 90% of patients compared with 20% in healthy
control group [149]. Future studies should define objective and clinically interpretable measures
for fatigue and variability sub-scores and include them for the full analysis. An example of a
clinically interpretable objective measure to consider for fatigue is the rate of change in a given
kinematic feature over consecutive repetitions of a task. It is expected that larger clinical scores of
fatigue would translate to larger differences between kinematic features from early repetitions to
final repetitions. Similarly, one can examine the standard deviation of a given kinematic feature
over multiple repetitions as a potential marker of the variability sub-score.
Finally, this study provided some insights on the usefulness of different assessment tasks
for quantifying different aspects of orofacial impairments. For example, SPREAD is mainly used
to assess symmetry of the horizontal lip motion, while OPEN assesses the vertical symmetry as
well as the range of motion of the jaw and lips. Understanding the clinical importance and
interpretation from each assessment task can help understand and validate such clinical tools as
long as the automatic kinematic features extracted remain clinically interpretable and relevant.
This study showed that predicting the severity of clinical rating scales is challenging and
may be limited by 1) the small sample size, and 2) the skewed distribution of the data across
severity scale. Future studies should focus on gathering data from participants with more severe
orofacial impairment in order to balance the dataset and have representative data in all the
categories. Overall, this study provided evidence for validity of such technologies as objective
assessment tools for orofacial movements.
Conclusion
The overall goal of this project was part of an effort to help the translation of novel videobased technologies capable of providing objective kinematic measures into a useful clinical tool
allowing for more accurate assessment of orofacial deficits within neurological disorder
populations. Adopting the V3 framework for validation of digital biomarkers [89], the two studies

52

were performed on healthy, MND, and post-stroke participant groups to analytically and clinically
validated such technologies for objective kinematic assessment of orofacial movements. measure
provided by EMA. The combined results indicated that such technology can be used to
automatically extract clinically meaningful and objective features that clinicians can incorporate
into their workflow to make more informed and data-driven decisions.
Limitations and Future Directions
Upgrading the facial detection and tracking algorithm
In present work, FAN [80] and fine-tuned FAN [85] models were used for facial landmark
detection and tracking purposes. FAN is a relatively old model (proposed in 2017) in the domain
of deep facial tracking architectures, and lacks testing on widespread face landmark datasets [61].
Current state-of-the-art models have reported higher performance and have been trained on larger
and more diverse datasets than FAN. For example, PropagationNet, ADNet, and SubpixelHeatmap
are more recent architectures that have achieved top performance on different datasets [61]. They
are all based on the similar heatmap architecture as FAN and use Hourglass for their backbone
with their own unique improvement; PropagationNet introduced a new face boundary attention
module, ADNet separates normal and tangent errors, while SubpixelHeatmap focuses on reducing
heatmap quantization error [61]. The higher performance of these algorithms on datasets used for
training and testing FAN as well as more challenging datasets [61] suggest their superior
performance and higher facial landmark detection and tracking on the clinical data collected for
these studies. Using the methods in [85] and clinical orofacial datasets such as [73], these models
can be further fine-tuned to more accurately track the facial features in clinical populations.
Increasing the performance of landmark detection and tracking algorithms will directly translate
to extracting more accurate kinematic features and thus development of more reliable and accurate
tool for assessing orofacial movements.
Using all the spatial and temporal data from facial landmarks

The current studies used the values of kinematic features averaged across multiple
repetitions which provided a single discrete value per kinematic feature, task, and participant. This
results in loss of potentially useful information within each repetition as well as more broadly
looking at the values of features across multiple repetitions. For example, useful information

53

regarding fatigue and variability clinical scores can be obtained by looking at the changes of the
kinematic measures across multiple repetitions in tasks such as sentence repetition. Future work
may explore using time series data of kinematic features rather than single values to potentially
capture more clinically relevant features. For example, in a study using an LSTM architecture on
the time series of facial landmarks data obtained from participants expressing specific emotions in
front of camera was used to distinguish between individuals with Parkinson disease and healthy
controls [100]. In this study LSTM showed promising results in analyzing sequential video data to
guide clinicians with diagnosis of neurological diseases. The main limitation of this study was
small sample size [100]. In the future, as more clinical data becomes available, such architectures
can be used to derive clinically meaningful features that are sensitive to temporal changes within
multiple repetitions.
Multimodal data
This work only considered 3D video data (colour and depth). Recent studies have shown
that multimodal data (both video and audio) can further improve the predictive power of such
algorithms [117], [150]–[153]. For example, Neumann et al. demonstrated that combining acoustic
signals obtained from the audio signal (i.e. features obtained from acoustic analysis) together with
facial kinematic features results in increase predictive power of these such algorithms [117]. Using
a set of features extracted from video as well as audio signals, the trained algorithms showed
promising results in differentiating between healthy controls, pre-symptomatic ALS, and bulbar
ALS, thus, demonstrating the utility of incorporating various speech and facial metrics to assist
early diagnosis and monitoring of ALS [117]. Similarly, the future work of this research should
consider extracting clinically valid speech features from audio recordings of the speech tasks to
potentially increase the predictive power of the regression models.
Collecting Longitudinal Data
These technologies have a great potential for diagnosis and prognosis of neurological
disorders as larger volumes of longitudinal data, accounting for objective inter and intra subject
variability, is being gathered [154]. Current clinical practice suffers from one-shot assessments,
where patients see clinicians once every few months or even years [149]. The performance of the
patient may not provide a true picture of their performance due to the day-to-day variability of the

54

disease characteristics [154]. This is of particular importance in neurological disorders in which
the disease representation itself is heterogenous and patient’s clinical characteristics may vary
from day-to-day [139], [154]. Therefore, collecting repeated assessments reduce intra-participant’s
variability while increasing the power of the study [154]. In addition, longitudinal study design
can be more efficient, cost effective, and result in more robust model selection process [155].
Previously longitudinal orofacial and speech home-based assessments in neurological disorders
resulted in characteristics of disease progression and/or response to treatment interventions [96].
The future studies will focus on collecting data longitudinally and modelling progression in
addition to symptoms’ severity.
