into seven things to keep in mind in dementia care: “know their
personality,” “move their mind,” “help them move their body,”
“expand their world,” “help each other,” “get them in shape,” and
“help them face the risks.”
The Dementia Care Method® was provided to all the BSC
nursing homes as booklets and cards to support care practices.
Care staff in each nursing home were encouraged to use the
Dementia Care Method® for daily care.
Several months after providing the methods, a survey of prac-
tices of the Dementia Care Method® showed that half of the sur-
vey participants employed the methods and felt that they were
providing essential dementia care. Care staff with <1 year of expe-
rience have shown a similar tendency. However, some had difﬁ-
culties in putting it into practice.7
To facilitate the practice of the Dementia Care Method®, we
have started an online training course. Speciﬁcally, a 1-h online
training course was developed to introduce the outline and use
cases promoting the technique. The care staff of BSC nursing
homes participated in the training voluntarily.
To verify the effect of the online training course on the
Dementia Care Method®, we asked the participants to respond to
a questionnaire after voluntarily taking the online training course.
Of 1246 participants, 782 answered the questionnaire. About
95.7% said that they would like to try this method. The method
suited their purposes in addressing current caregiving issues. In
addition, they perceived the technique to be easy and believed that
it could be applied to speciﬁc care recipients or situations. In con-
trast, the participants were concerned about whether they could
involve their colleagues, whether they will have enough time to use
the method, and apply it in a “correct” manner. Additional use
cases and tips were provided to help them overcome such anxiety.
The online training course motivated the participants to put
the Dementia Care Method® into use. As a result, more residents
have begun sharing their thoughts and requests with the care
staff,
enabling them to support
residents in realizing
their
desires and preferences. For instance, one resident who was
demotivated, now enjoys growing vegetables, and has even started
a workout routine. Another resident who was usually bored at
the nursing home and frequently expressed a desire to return
home is now actively engaging in various housework activities
and enjoying life with fellow residents. The impact can be evalu-
ated through observing changes in the frequency of behavioral
and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD) and the content
of daily reports. Similar cases have been gradually increasing,
indicating that care recipients’ quality of life has improved. More-
over, some utilization cases of BSC nursing homes have been
presented at conferences.8,9

Investigation of a model for evaluating cognitive decline from
facial photographs using AI
Dear Editor,
Japan has more than 5 million people with dementia, and if
4 million people with mild cognitive impairment are included, one
in four older adults is in a state of declining cognitive function.
Early detection of cognitive decline can prevent the onset of
dementia. Developing a less invasive, inexpensive and simple
dementia screening tool is urgently required.

Perceived age is more strongly correlated with cognitive func-
tion compared with chronological age.1 Thus, it was postulated
that cognitive decline might be expressed in a patient’s face. Then,
we carried out a study using artiﬁcial intelligence (AI), which can
likely determine more details compared with human judgment.
When deep learning was used to learn facial photographs of
patients with Alzheimer’s disease and normal facial photographs,
and a classiﬁcation task was attempted using the test images, the
correct answer rate was 92.6%, and the area under the curve
was 0.97.
The scores calculated by the AI model correlated signiﬁcantly
more strongly with cognitive decline than with chronological age.2
While perceived age can be a useful biomarker in the geriatric
ﬁeld, it requires more than 10 people to judge it. AI that deter-
mines age from facial photos is already in use, and for this study,
we opted to utilize the widely used Microsoft Azure Application
Programming Interface. We aimed to determine if AI can easily
produce reliable perceived age.
The Alzheimer’s disease group (121 people), which was used
for the previous study,2 was also used in the present study. We
evaluated their faces using the Microsoft Azure face Application
Programming Interface.
The AI Azure age showed signiﬁcant correlation with chrono-
logical age (r = 0.480, P = 2.53  108) and human-judged per-
ceived age” (r = 0.791, P = 3.88  1027; Fig. S1); however, it failed
to show a signiﬁcantly stronger correlation with the Mini-Mental
State Examination than chronological age (Table 1) in men, women
and all participants, contrary to human-judged perceived age. Fur-
thermore, although human perceived age showed a stronger corre-
lation with the Vitality Index3 than chronological age,1 AI Azure
age failed to show superiority (Data S2). To test the hypothesis that
a higher population might overcome the failure of AI Azure age, we
tried 638 faces; however, AI Azure age failed to show the superiority
to chronological age (Data S3). We tested the relationship between
“sadness” detected by Azure and GDS15. However, the analysis of
sadness also failed to detect depression (Data S4, Fig. S2).
The failure to show the superiority of AI-perceived age over
human-judged perceived age might be attributable to the weak-
ness of the Azure system in older people and Asian ethnicity. The
AI Azure age perceived age to be approximately 20 years younger
than chronological age, and the maximum value was 74 years.
Emotion judged by AI was reported to be biased by population
due to bias in training data.4
To determine the perceived age of older individuals as a use-
ful biomarker, AI trained on a substantial amount of training
data from older individuals would likely be necessary.
