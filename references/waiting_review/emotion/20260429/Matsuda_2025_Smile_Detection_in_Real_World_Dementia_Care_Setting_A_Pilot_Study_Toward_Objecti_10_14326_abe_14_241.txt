1.  Introduction
In the health and medical fields, treating acute illnesses
became important around 1950; however, population
composition, nutritional status, and public health have
changed considerably since then. In recent years, the
number of elderly and chronically ill people has been
increasing worldwide [1], which has led to a paradigm
shift from a cure-focused approach to a care-centered
approach using patient’s quality of life (QOL) as an outcome measure. This results in an increasing number of
studies focusing on QOL [2]. Meanwhile, there is no
fundamental cure for dementia; thus, the goal of care is
to promote well-being and maximize QOL [3, 4], which
has become increasingly important in recent years [5, 6].

The World Health Organization (WHO) defines
QOL as individuals’ perception of their position in life in
the context of the culture and value systems in which
they live and in relation to their goals, expectations, standards and concerns. The WHO recommends the WHO-
QOL-100 QOL assessment [7], which comprises six domains (physical health, psychological health, level of
independence, social relationships, environment, and
spirituality) [8]. To assess QOL, individuals answer a
wide range of questions, spending approximately 30
minutes thinking back over the previous two weeks.
However, for persons with dementia and declined
self-awareness and language skills, assessments based
on self-rating are difficult. Thus, the QoL in Alzheimer’s

Smile Detection in Real-World Dementia Care Setting:
A Pilot Study Toward Objective Evaluation of the Quality of Life of Persons with Dementia
Keiji Matsuda,*,**, # Kenji Nishida,** Toru Yamada*,** Katsuo Yamanaka**
Abstract
Quality of life (QOL) measurement is essential in evaluating effective interventions for persons
with dementia. Essentially, QOL is assessed based on self-ratings. As the disease progresses, persons with dementia cannot perform these self-ratings; however, their emotional expressions can be observed. Thus,
proxy-rating is used, which involves another person such as a caregiver, who answers from the perspective of
the person studied or evaluates the emotional state from the person’s facial expressions. However, the results of
proxy-rating differ from those of self-rating. This is considered to be partly due to the subjectivity of the caregiver who performs the rating. Thus, an objective method is needed to evaluate the QOL of persons with dementia. A pilot study was conducted to detect objectively the smiles of persons with dementia, because conventional QOL assessments include facial expression‐related items, and smile is strongly related to pleasantness or
friendliness. To detect smiles, we developed an image processing method including tilt correction of the facial
image and face detection of a specified person among many faces regardless of the facial expressions presented.
To verify the effectiveness of the proposed method, we measured smiles before and after a day care intervention
program that involved 1 hour of physical exercises and 2 hours of cognitive stimulation, which has been shown
to improve QOL. We developed an experimental protocol to quantify the changes in the smiles of 11 persons
with dementia at a day care facility. A facility staff member induced each person to smile using simple words
while recording video. Each recorded video was then processed to calculate the increase in detection of smiles
evoked by the staff member. By comparing the differences before and after participating in the day care program, we confirmed that the frequency of smile detection increased significantly after the program.

Disease (QoL-AD) assessment was developed, which is
answered by a proxy of the patient [9]. The QoL-AD
comprises 13 items (physical health, energy level, mood,
living situation, memory, family, marriage, friends, oneself, ability in daily living, ability to do things for fun,
money, and one’s whole life). In addition, the Quality of
Life in Late-Stage Dementia (QUALID) scale [10] was
developed to evaluate QOL by observing the facial expressions or behaviors caused by positive and negative
emotions. This assessment comprises 11 items (smiles,
appears sad, cries, discomfort, physically uncomfortable,
discontent, irritable, enjoys eating, enjoys touching, enjoys interacting, and emotionally calm), and the patient
or the proxy rates the assessment items by thinking back
over the last two weeks.

However, ratings by proxy or observation tend to
deviate from self-rating [6, 11]. Such deviation is understandable because the caregiver (proxy) is subjectively
influenced by the burden caused by the patient’s behavioral and psychological symptoms [6]. To adequately
assess the QOL of persons with dementia, any objective
method for QOL evaluation may be a useful substitute.
While the conventional QOL assessments rely on awareness by self or by the proxy, a few items including mood
and energy level in QoL-AD and facial expressions in
QUALID are assessed by the proxy based on the patient’s facial appearance. Dementia frequently causes
depression or apathy as the disease progresses [12],
which can cause reduction in the degree and frequency
of facial expressions, e.g., smiles. However, to the best
of our knowledge, no previous study has reported the
relationship between the changes in facial expressions
and progression of dementia. Thus, in this study, we explored the possibility that objective detection of facial
expressions based on imaging technology can be used as
a substitute for the assessment items related to emotions
and facial expressions in conventional QOL assessments.

In Japan, many elderly individuals choose to live in
their own homes, either alone or with their families, and
use care services as required. Others opt to reside in
nursing homes or hospitals. In such living arrangements,
images that include the facial expression of the elderly
subject frequently feature other people coming and going. It is crucial to identify the elderly person’s face accurately in these images, regardless of their facial expression or posture change (such as tilting the head). In
addition, it is important to detect the elderly person’s
smile without being influenced by the posture or other
factors.

Images frequently contain private information about
the intended subject and other persons present in the image. Thus, it is important to maintain the confidentiality
of the raw image data and not to share it outside the location at which the image was captured. To ensure privacy,
we should detect the smile of the target person and only
share that as anonymized numerical data.

Given the current economic situation in elderly care
in Japan, it is unlikely to obtain substantial increase in
funding to improve the QOL of the elderly. If the system
that implements the above techniques is too expensive, it
may not be widely adopted or it may contribute to further
disparities in QOL. To avoid this, we have implemented
a system using affordable, general purpose technologies,
and we have ensured that all the technologies have licenses that permit commercial use. These efforts allow
the system to be used commercially and contribute to the
business development of elderly care.

Generally, one can recognize other persons’ emotions from their facial expressions, and generate appropriate facial expressions associated with one’s emotions.
Previous studies including persons with dementia reported that the ease of recognition varies according to the
kind of emotion, and happiness is the easiest to recognize
[13, 14]. Also, comparing the facial expressions in response to scenarios evoking various emotions, smile is
most easily generated [15]. These studies suggest that the
smile is strongly associated with happiness and is the
facial expression that is most easily expressed and recognized even in persons with dementia. To explore the
possibility of objective QOL evaluation in persons with
dementia, we developed a specialized technical procedure to detect smiles in real-world dementia care settings
in order to evaluate the changes in persons using the dementia care programs. The remainder of this paper is
organized as follows. Section 2 describes the proposed
method comprising face detection, person identification,
and tilt correction of the detected face. The experimental
protocol used to detect the smiles of persons with dementia who used a day care facility is also described in
Section 2. Section 3 demonstrates the change in the frequency of smile detection in persons with dementia before and after the day care program comprising physical
exercises and cognitive stimulation. Finally, we discuss
the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

into conventional machine learning methods and the
more recent deep learning methods such as transfer
learning using a trained feature extraction network [16].
In this study, we employed conventional machine learning method using gradient local autocorrelation (GLAC)
features, which has demonstrated effective recognition
of curved features in localized regions [17, 18]. Thus, we

2.2  SDV Evaluation
We evaluated SDV using data from videos of frontal
view faces from the AIST Face Expression Database
2017 [23]. The videos in this dataset include 8 subjects
(four males and four females) showing 11 facial expressions, comprising anger (with a closed mouth), anger
(with an open mouth), disgust (with a closed mouth),
disgust (with an open mouth), excitement, fear, happiness, relaxation, sadness, sleepiness, and surprise. In total, 88 short videos were used for analysis. Each video
showed the change from a neutral face expression to a
full blown emotional expression. The total duration of
each video was 2.5 s, and the onset of emotional expression took 0.5 s.

The time course curves of the changes in SDV for
various facial expressions averaged across eight participants were compared for the evaluation. Positive SDVs
were associated with smiling due to the principle of the
two-class SVM. The difference patterns of change in
SDV were then classified into three types of facial expressions. The first type consisted of happiness, excitement, and relaxation, which exhibited increases in SDV
from that of neutral expression to above the zero line
(Fig. 2). For this type, the weighting vectors successfully
detected smiles. The second type comprised sadness and
sleepiness, which showed no significant changes in SDV
from that of the neutral facial expression (Fig. 3). The

weighting vector was generated to distinguish the anger,
disgust, fear, sadness, surprise, and neutral expressions

2.3  Correction of SDV affected by tilted faces
To train the SVM, we used facial images in the upright
position. Consequently, the trained weighting vector frequently failed to calculate the SDV for tilted facial images. Therefore, we corrected the images of tilted faces to
become upright prior to calculating the SDV. A line was
constructed to join the positions of the two eyes in the
originally detected face, and the face image was rotated
until the line became horizontal. To evaluate the effect of
this tilt correction process, tilted facial images were generated by rotating an upright smiling face image for up to
± 20° in increments of 1°. Then, the SDV of the corrected and uncorrected images at each rotation angle were
compared (Fig. 5). The result showed that correction by
rotation realized more stable SDV detection than without
correction. Note that the slight difference in SDV between corrected and uncorrected images at 0° was
caused by a difference in cropping of the original and
corrected images (i.e., the eye positions in the original
upright face were not aligned horizontally).
2.4  Identification of faces of multiple peoples with
various expressions
When this system is used in the clinical setting, the face
of the study subject must be differentiated from those of
other persons present in the image, even when all subjects have different expressions. To realize such personal
identification, we obtained a 128-dimensional vector for
each face image using cv::FaceRecognizerSF, and the
cosine similarities of one vector against the other vectors
were calculated. The cosine similarity (ranging from −1
to 1) has a value of 1 when two vectors are identical. We
expect that the cosine similarities of various facial expressions of a subject against the neutral facial image of
the same person are higher than the cosine similarities of
various facial expressions of the subject against the neutral facial images of the other persons. Thus, the two
populations of cosine similarities can be discriminated
using an appropriate threshold.

2.5  Identifying a specific face with various expressions
The video clips obtained in day care facilities frequently
feature many people coming and going, and various objects (other than faces) may be incorrectly detected as
faces. To identify a person’s face with various facial expressions in such clips, we designed an algorithm (Fig.
8) to perform face detection and recognition using the
OpenCV program library. We generated the 128-dimensional face feature vector for each facial image obtained,
which was given an identification number (ID), and we
made a list of face feature vectors with these IDs. Then,
for a new facial image captured, we generate the facial
feature vector of the image, and calculate the cosine similarities of this vector against the vectors in the list.
When the highest cosine similarity is less than 0.6, a new
ID is assigned to the vector and added to the vector list.
When the highest value is greater than or equal to 0.6, the
vector is assigned the ID of the vector on the list which
maximizes the similarity. However, the vector is only
added to the list if the highest value is less than 0.8.

procedure for participant identification, all recognized
faces were displayed with their IDs in each frame of the
recorded videos. As a result, we confirmed that other
persons, such as facility staff, were not assigned the par-

2.6  Data acquisition protocol
This study was approved by the institutional review
board of the Institute of Human Sciences, University of
Tsukuba (2022-199A). In addition, written informed
consent was obtained from a family member of each
participant as proxy, due to the participants’ cognitive
impairment. Eleven participants with dementia who used
the day care facility participated in the data acquisition
study. The participants were aged 66 to 91 years (mean:
82.1; SD: 7.9 years), and all participants were female.
Based on the Dementia Assessment Sheet for Community-based Integrated Care System-21 items [24], six participants had low level dementia, and five participants
had medium level dementia. The mean Mini-Mental
State Examination [25] score was 17.5 with SD of 5.6. In
addition, the mean length of participation in the day care
program was 1.78 years, with SD of 1.48 years. Note that
no participants had severe dementia that is known to reduce facial expression and the ability to respond to the
experimenter. For all participants who agreed to participate, the experimenter conducted the protocol once, as
described below. The day care program included 1 hour
of physical exercises and 2 hours of cognitive stimulation including the Cognitive Stimulation Therapy Japanese version developed by Yamanaka et al. [26].

To record the facial expression of each participant
before and after the day care program, we recorded two
short videos with sound using an Apple iPad. First, we
asked each participant for permission to record before
conducting the following protocol.
P1:	The participant was asked to remove the surgical
mask, worn as a precaution against COVID-19,
without assistance.
P2:	Started recording.
P3:	The participant was asked to look at the lens of the
iPad.
P4:	Waited approximately 2 s.
P5:	The participant was asked to smile.
P6:	Waited approximately 2 s.
P7:	The participant was asked to put on the mask without assistance.
P8:	Ended recording.
The required time, including moving to the recording
location, was less than 120 s, which was short enough to
record multiple participants at the facility. P3, P5, and P7
in the above protocol are shown in Fig. 9 at the corresponding event timing.

algorithm (Sections 2.4 and 2.5) effectively analyzes

In this study, we tested the effectiveness of using the
values obtained by subtracting the frequencies of
SDV-positive cases in the smile phase (4−5.5 s) by those
in the initial phase (0−1.5 s) for the analysis of the differences between before and after the day care program for
two calculation conditions: durations of the initial and
smiling phases, and the interval between their onset. Significant differences in the smile detection rate between
before and after the day care program were detected with
moderate level of effect sizes for certain ranges of the
conditions (Fig. 10). These results support the validity of
the proposed method and suggest that the day care program resulted in an increase in smiles among participants. The larger effect size when the smiling phase
started at 4 s rather than 3 s may be attributed to a delayed response, which may be prolonged by reduced
hearing and comprehension abilities. Considering these
personal differences in facial expressions and the diversity of the groups, such assessment of the effectiveness in
demonstrating statistical differences for the calculation
conditions (shown in Fig. 10) should be conducted in
each group to evaluate the method further. For more severe cases of limited facial expressions, the detection of
usual smiles using the training data for healthy persons
may not be suitable. Evaluation of smile in such persons
may be important in our future work. The results of this
study demonstrated that after the day care program that
included physical exercises and cognitive stimulation,
the frequency of smiles in the participants increased. As
discussed in Section 1, several QOL assessment items
are related to facial expressions, especially smiling. The
increase in objective smile detection suggests higher
scores for these items. A meta-analytic review concluded, based on 17 previous studies, that QoL-AD scores in
persons with dementia were improved by cognitive stimulation [27], and the results of that study are congruent
with the findings of the current study. While QoL-AD
assessed recent changes in QOL in persons with dementia, the current method identified changes in SDV before
and after a single intervention. In addition, the proposed
method can replace some components of the known
QOL assessments. However, it would be beneficial to
assess the effectiveness of each intervention to enrich the
contents. The current method integrates the established
face recognition and facial expression detection methods, similar to previous studies that employed commercial software to analyze expressions in persons with dementia [28–30]. However, to the best of our knowledge,
this is the first study to detect the smiles of a specific
person in a video showing multiple people in a real-world dementia care setting and evaluate the differences between before and after the program.

This study has several limitations that must be considered. First, we adopted an experimental protocol that
included asking the participants to smile. Stimuli such as
pictures of pets or grandchildren can evoke smiles, but
the variability of these methods poses difficulties in controlling the degree of stimulation. Thus, for simplification in this pilot study, the participants were asked to
smile. The experimenters (caregivers) who are familiar
with most participants asked the participants to smile
while displaying friendly smiles. Therefore, we do not
consider the participants’ smiles to be totally faked. If all
participants are showing fake smiles, there should be no
difference in the frequency of smiles before and after the
day care program. However, Figs. 10 and 11 show significant increases in SDV. Second, we focused only on
the items related to emotional states in the QOL assessments. In addition, we did not detect facial expressions
other than smiles. We focused on smile to evaluate the
emotional state because the goal of this study was to objectively evaluate the QOL of people, including those
without sufficient self-awareness. However, the emotional implications of facial expressions other than smiling vary according to the person’s cultural background,
despite the technical feasibility of detecting those facial
expressions. Therefore, in the future, other facial expressions that are related to the relevant items in the QOL
assessments should be investigated using appropriate
procedures.

In this study, the effect of a single type of intervention was evaluated in only 11 participants. Thus, further
evaluations should be performed in the future with a
larger number of experiments, participants, and types of
intervention. To obtain high quality data, the experimental protocol should also be improved such that anyone
can conduct experiments easily under identical conditions.

QOL is related to the perception of one’s daily living
under the influence of external cultural and internal value
systems, as well as mental and physical health [31].

If simpler and more natural protocols are developed
