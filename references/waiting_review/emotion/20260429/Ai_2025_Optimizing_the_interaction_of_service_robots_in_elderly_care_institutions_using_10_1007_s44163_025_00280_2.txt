Abstract
As the global population continues to age, the emotional and mental health needs of older adults are becoming increasingly significant. Conventional emotion recognition systems frequently encounter difficulties in adapting to the unique
emotional expression characteristics exhibited by this demographic. This paper proposes a multimodal emotion recognition system based on transfer learning, with the objective of enhancing the efficacy of robots in emotion recognition,
emotional engagement, and the promotion of mental well-being in older individuals. Initially, public emotion datasets
such as AffectNet and IEMOCAP are utilised for pre-training convolutional neural networks (CNN) and bidirectional long
short-term memory (Bi-LSTM) models to capture general emotional features. Subsequently, the system is fine-tuned
using a home-grown nursing home dataset, employing a hierarchical fine-tuning strategy to optimise the model, with a
particular emphasis on enhancing the facial expressions, speech and physiological signals of the elderly. To address the
challenges posed by the complexity of the nursing home environment, small batch learning and noise enhancement
techniques are incorporated. The experimental results demonstrate the model’s capacity to accurately recognise the
emotional states of the elderly by integrating multimodal data, encompassing facial expressions, speech, body language
and physiological signals. The effectiveness of the system in promoting emotional engagement and enhancing mental
health is further substantiated by heart rate and galvanic skin response monitoring. In terms of emotion recognition
accuracy, the experimental group’s average recognition accuracy for multiple emotions reaches 84.1%, with an F1 score
of 82.5%, precision of 83.2% and recall of 81.8%. In addition, the experimental group performs better than the control
group in improving mental health and mobilising emotions. However, the study is confronted with challenges such
as domain adaptation issues, the necessity for fine-tuning, and data limitations specific to older individuals, which are
addressed by hierarchical fine-tuning and data augmentation techniques.

(WHO), the elderly population is defined as individuals aged 60 years and over. A United Nations report (n.d.) states

that by 2050, the proportion of the worldwide population aged 65 and above can reach 16.3%, and is expected to
further increase to 21% in the second half of this century, with Asia becoming the region with the largest elderly
population. [3]. This trend has put higher demands on traditional elderly care services while promoting the rapid
development of smart elderly care technologies [4, 5]. As an innovative auxiliary tool, elderly care service robots
can alleviate the labor shortage problem and provide emotional support and social interaction [6]. However, existing elderly care robots face many challenges in emotion recognition, especially when facing the elderly group. The

limit existing systems’ effectiveness. Therefore, improving the accuracy, versatility, and customization of emotion
recognition has become a key direction of current research.
Traditional emotion recognition systems usually rely on a single modality, which makes them unable to handle
the complex emotions of the elderly [10, 11]. The emotional expression of the elderly is often affected by physiological changes and health problems, showing features that are significantly different from those of young people.
Meanwhile, emotion recognition technology usually requires large-scale labeled data, but in special scenarios such
as nursing homes, data collection, and labeling are very difficult, further restricting system performance. In addition,
traditional elderly care robots often lack personalized adjustment capabilities and cannot flexibly respond to the
emotional needs of each elderly person, thereby reducing the quality of interaction [12]. Therefore, current emotion
recognition systems and elderly care robots urgently need to combine multi-modal recognition and personalized
adjustment functions to improve their adaptability and accuracy in emotional interaction with the elderly. While
previous studies have explored the integration of transfer learning and multi-modal emotion recognition, our study
uniquely combines these techniques to address the specific challenges of emotion recognition in elderly care settings. Our hierarchical fine-tuning strategy and modality adaptive weighting mechanism provide a more robust and
accurate emotion recognition system tailored for the elderly.
To address these challenges, this paper proposes a multimodal emotion recognition system based on transfer learning, which aims to improve the effectiveness of robots in emotion recognition, emotional engagement,
and promoting the mental well-being of elderly people. The system integrates multiple modalities, including facial
expressions, speech, body language, and physiological signals, to provide a more comprehensive and accurate
emotion recognition capability. Specifically, the system uses convolutional neural networks (CNN) and bidirectional
long short-term memory (Bi-LSTM) models to capture common emotional features from public emotion datasets
such as AffectNet and IEMOCAP. The system is then fine-tuned using an in-house nursing home dataset, applying
a hierarchical fine-tuning strategy to optimise the model, with a focus on enhancing the facial expressions, speech
and physiological signals of the elderly. Small-batch learning and noise enhancement techniques are incorporated
to cope with the complexity of the nursing home environment.
The following multi-modal data processing methods are employed:
1.	 Facial expression recognition: An optimised CNN model is utilised to extract features from facial images, with a focus
on key areas such as the eyes, mouth, and eyebrows. The model employs smaller convolution kernels to capture
subtle changes in facial expressions and retains more local information through adjusted pooling layers.
2.	 Speech recognition: Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCC) are utilised to extract speech features, which are
subsequently processed by a Bi-LSTM network to capture long-term dependencies in voice signals.
3.	 Body language recognition: Body language, encompassing posture, gestures, and gait, is captured using cameras and
depth sensors. The system employs pre-trained CNN models to extract emotion-related features from body actions
and combines Bi-LSTM to model the time series.
4.	 Physiological signal recognition: Physiological data, including heart rate and galvanic skin response (GSR), is collected
using smart wearable devices. These signals are then preprocessed and analysed to provide an objective physiological basis for emotion recognition.

2  Literature review
Cultural factors play an important role in the expression and perception of emotions, especially among older adults.
Some studies have explored the impact of cultural differences on emotion expression and recognition in older adults,
emphasising the importance of cultural sensitivity in emotion recognition systems. to ensure effective functioning in
diverse populations [13]. Body language is an important component of emotional expression, and older adults may
exhibit unique body language due to factors such as decreased mobility, chronic pain, or cognitive decline. At the same
time, age-related declines in auditory and visual perception can significantly affect older adults’ ability to recognise
emotions [14]. One study investigated how these declines affect emotional expression and recognition, highlighting
the need for customised solutions in assistive technology. Their study found that hearing loss and vision loss can lead
to changes in speech patterns and facial expressions, which can affect the accuracy of emotion recognition systems.
This study highlights the importance of addressing age-related sensory decline in the design of emotion recognition
systems for older adults [15].
Recently, emotion recognition technologies have made remarkable progress in single-modal recognition, such as
speech, facial expression, text, and body language [16]. Morgan inferred emotions through intonation and speaking
speed [17], but environmental noise and individual differences affected accuracy. The facial feature extraction model
of Abdullah et al. was limited by the differences in facial expressions among the elderly [18]. Text sentiment analysis
method of Poria et al. still faced challenges identifying complex emotions [19]. Dzedzickis et al. recognized emotions
through body language, but data collection and noise processing were still limited [20]. Despite the progress in singlemodal emotion recognition, it has obvious limitations in complex emotional expressions [21, 22]. Therefore, effectively
integrating multi-modal information to improve recognition accuracy has become a research focus.
Multi-modal emotion recognition combines multiple information such as speech, facial expressions, body language,
etc., which is especially important for the elderly [23–25]. The complementarity between modalities enhances the
system’s robustness, but modal fusion still faces challenges such as data heterogeneity and information redundancy.
Speech and facial expressions often provide consistent emotional signals, and body language can provide more detail.
Efficiently integrating these signals and improving recognition results remain key issues. The application of transfer
learning in emotion recognition has developed rapidly [26, 27]. Through transfer learning, the pre-trained model in
the source domain can be fine-tuned in the target domain, alleviating the data scarcity problem and improving model
adaptability. Pre-training on large-scale datasets such as AffectNet and IEMOCAP supports emotion recognition in the
elderly, but the difference between the source and target domains remains a challenge in transfer learning [28, 29].
With the development of multi-modal emotion recognition and transfer learning technologies, optimizing the
emotional interaction performance of elderly care service robots has become a research hotspot [30]. Due to the
diversity and uniqueness of the emotional expressions of the elderly, robots need to be highly personalized and
adaptable. Traditional robot systems lack flexible personalized adjustment functions and are difficult to precisely
respond to the emotions of the elderly, resulting in poor interaction effects [31, 32]. Therefore, combining multi-modal
recognition with transfer learning to enhance the robot’s emotional adaptability in complex situations becomes the
key to improving the quality of interaction [33]. In recent years, multi-modal emotion recognition systems combined
with online learning have been gradually applied, which can adjust recognition strategies in real time to cope with
emotional changes in the elderly [34, 35]. However, further improving the system’s precision and flexibility remains
a challenge to be addressed.

emotion detection system in the nursing environment. This paper selects convolutional neural networks (CNN) and

(3)	 Modal fusion

  In multi-modal emotion recognition, data from different modalities provide complementary perspectives for
emotional expression. However, a single modality is often affected by noise, environmental interference, and
individual differences, limiting the accuracy and robustness of emotion recognition. Therefore, this paper adopts
a multi-modal deep neural network architecture to optimize the fusion effect of speech, facial expressions, body
language, and physiological signals. Specifically, the features of each modality are first processed independently.
Voice signals are extracted through MFCC features. Facial expression images are processed through optimized CNN.
Body language data is extracted through posture recognition and motion analysis. Physiological signals are collected
in real time through smart wearable devices. After preliminary processing, the feature data of each modality are
input into the multi-modal fusion network.

(2)	 Model training and transfer.

  In model training, this paper first uses public emotion datasets such as AffectNet and IEMOCAP to pre-train CNN
and Bi-LSTM models. These datasets cover emotion label data of modalities such as facial expressions, speech, and
body language, aiming to help the model learn common emotional features and expression patterns, laying the
foundation for subsequent emotion recognition which can then be fine-tuned for the specific emotional expressions
of the elderly in nursing homes. The hyperparameters are initially tuned, and the Adam optimizer is selected. The
learning rate is set to 0.0005, and the batch size is 32. The training efficiency and model performance are balanced
to optimize the pre-training effect. In addition, dropout (0.4) and L2 regularization (0.001) techniques are used to
prevent overfitting and ensure the model’s good generalization ability on large-scale datasets.

  Implementation details:

  The model was trained using Adam optimizer with the following hyperparameters:

  Pre-training Learning Rate: 0.0005, chosen to balance stable convergence and efficient updates for large-scale
datasets.

  Fine-tuning Learning Rate: 0.0001, reduced to adapt to the smaller elderly-specific dataset while preserving pretrained knowledge.

  Dropout Rate: 0.4, applied to prevent overfitting by deactivating 40% of neurons during training.

  L2 Regularization: 0.001, selected to impose moderate weight penalty without hindering learning capacity.

  Rationale: These values were empirically determined based on prior practices in multimodal emotion recognition.
The learning rate decay strategy (Sect. 3.3) further stabilizes fine-tuning by reducing the rate by 10% per epoch if
validation loss plateaus.

  This paper adopts a hierarchical fine-tuning strategy to adapt the model to the emotional expression of the
elderly group. The nursing home dataset constructed in this paper is used for fine-tuning. During fine-tuning,
optimization is performed on subtle changes in the facial expressions of the elderly to improve the sensitivity of
the facial expression recognition model. Regarding speech recognition, the focus is on optimizing the model’s
ability to recognize emotional expression features of the elderly, such as low pitch and rapid speaking speed. In
terms of physiological signals, the model’s recognition precision of physiological signals such as heart rate and GSR
is improved by combining the physiological fluctuation features of the elderly. During the fine-tuning process, a
smaller learning rate (0.0001) is used, and the learning rate decay strategy is adjusted according to the unique needs
of facial expressions, speech, and physiological signals so that the model can better adapt to the emotional features
of the elderly while retaining pre-training knowledge. The batch size is set to 16 to reduce noise during training

the model’s sensitivity to accommodate these slight changes.

3.4  Lightweight deployment optimization
To ensure practical applicability in resource-constrained elderly care facilities, the proposed model integrates the
following optimizations:
Model Pruning: Redundant neurons in fully connected layers were iteratively pruned based on magnitude criteria,
reducing parameter count by 35% with < 1% accuracy drop.
Quantization-aware Training: We adopted 8-bit integer quantization during fine-tuning to compress the model size
by 4 × while maintaining FP32-level performance.
Edge-friendly frameworks: The final model was converted to TensorRT and ONNX formats for efficient inference on
edge devices (e.g., NVIDIA Jetson Nano, Raspberry Pi).

5  Discussion and future work
This study integrates transfer learning with multimodal emotion recognition to enhance the effectiveness of emotion
recognition in aged care settings. This integration enables the model to utilize pre-trained knowledge from extensive
public datasets and adapt it to the specific emotional expressions exhibited by older adults. Hierarchical fine-tuning
strategies, which involve freezing low-level network layers while fine-tuning high-level feature extraction layers,
improve model adaptability and mitigate overfitting. Furthermore, an adaptive weighting mechanism dynamically
adjusts the weight of each modality based on its confidence level, optimizing the fusion effect and enhancing
the system’s capacity for accurate emotion recognition. While prior research has examined transfer learning and
multimodal emotion recognition independently, our approach uniquely combines these two techniques to tackle
challenges related to data scarcity and distinctive emotional expressions among older adults. This combination yields
a more robust and precise emotion recognition system for elder care. The implementation of hierarchical fine-tuning
strategies alongside adaptive weighting mechanisms distinguishes our study from existing methodologies that
typically employ fixed weights or simplistic joins for multimodal fusion.
The experimental group achieved an average emotion recognition accuracy of 84.1%, significantly surpassing
the control group’s average accuracy of less than 80%. This finding underscores the efficacy of our approach in
accurately identifying emotions. Additionally, variations in heart rate and electrodermal response (GSR) confirmed
that the system effectively engaged elderly individuals’ emotions. Notably, loneliness scores decreased substantially
within the experimental group, indicating an improvement in mental health outcomes. Future iterations of the
research should aim to combine physical, psychological, and behavioral assessment techniques to provide a more
comprehensive assessment of mental health improvement. Longitudinal studies could be conducted to assess the
long-term effects of the system on mental health. By addressing these issues, future research can contribute to the
development of more sophisticated service robots that provide compassionate and effective care for older adults,
ultimately improving their quality of life and well-being.
6  Conclusion
This paper constructs a multi-modal emotion recognition system based on transfer learning and applies it to the interaction optimization of service robots in nursing homes. By integrating multi-modal information such as facial expressions,
speech, body language, and physiological signals, this paper significantly improves the robot’s performance in emotion
recognition, emotion mobilization, and mental health improvement. First, public datasets such as AffectNet and IEMOCAP
