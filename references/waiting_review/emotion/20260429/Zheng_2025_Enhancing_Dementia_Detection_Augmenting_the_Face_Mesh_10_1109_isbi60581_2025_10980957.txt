ENHANCING DEMENTIA DETECTION: AUGMENTING THE FACE MESH
Chuheng Zheng, Mondher Bouazizi, Tomoaki Ohtsuki,
Momoko Kitazawa, Toshiro Horigome, Taishiro Kishimoto
Keio University
ABSTRACT
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) stands as a prevalent form of dementia,
primarily impacting the elderly, and presently lacking any known
cure. Early screening and intervention have demonstrated efficacy
for both current and prospective treatments. With the global popula-
tion aging, early detection of AD has become imperative. Language-
based approaches for dementia detection have received considerable
attention in research due to dementia’s nature of hurting cognitive
and language functions. Facial features, on the other hand, have
been comparatively neglected despite their potential in identifying
AD, due to a lack of usable dataset. AD patients show different
patterns while displaying facial expressions because of Apathy, a
phenomenon common in AD patients. This makes facial features
non-negligible in dementia detection. The face mesh, comprising
478 landmarks, serves as a rich and informative facial representa-
tion. This study concentrates on leveraging face mesh data for AD
detection.
We explored data augmentation techniques to address
the challenges of over-fitting, particularly impactful in training neu-
ral networks on a small dataset. Deep learning methods, includ-
ing transformer, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), and Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, were employed to classify
dementia patients and healthy subjects. By augmenting the dataset
by rotation, shift, and scaling, we improved the performance of the
deep learning methods. The combined use of 1D CNN networks and
data augmentation method reported the highest accuracy of 76%.
These results confirmed the effectiveness of our data augmentation
methods. They also underscore the potential of face-related features
in enhancing early detection approaches for AD.
Index Terms— data augmentation, dementia detection, deep
learning, face mesh
1. INTRODUCTION
Dementia, a degenerative neurological disorder, progressively ham-
pers an individual’s cognitive capacities and day-to-day functional-
ity. Primarily affecting the elderly, dementia afflicts approximately
55 million people globally, with an additional 10 million new cases
surfacing each year [1]. Patients grappling with dementia may man-
ifest symptoms like memory loss, impaired judgment, and bewilder-
ment, encountering challenges in articulation and comprehension.
Some may even find themselves disoriented in familiar surround-
ings. As the illness advances, those afflicted may also display be-
havioral and psychological manifestations, including but not limited
to depression, anxiety, agitation, and aggression [2].
The widespread application of conventional methods for diag-
nosing dementia, such as neuroimaging and psychological testing, is
severely hampered. Firstly, these methods need a lot of resources and
money. The conventional methods involve costly medical infrastruc-
ture, skilled professionals, and a large amount of time commitment.
Therefore, it is difficult for these diagnostic processes to scale up to
a wider population. Furthermore, people may be discouraged from
willingly engaging in these assessments due to the invasive nature
of neuroimaging and the complexity of psychological examinations,
which might lead to poor acceptance rates and possible delays in the
diagnosis of dementia. A low-cost and scalable screening method
would be highly favored to recognize possible biological indicators
for dementia, especially in the early stage. Contrary to the huge
amount of studies [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8] to search dementia indicators from
speech or transcriptions, facial features have not gotten as much at-
tention, despite being demonstrated to be useful indicators [9]. Some
researches [9, 10] have investigated the use of facial features or face
images for dementia detection; however, these studies utilized very
small and restricted datasets, which may introduce institutional bias
into the results, yielding a high performance with no generalization
ability when applied to other data.
Motivated by the under-researched condition in the area of us-
ing facial features to detect dementia, we aimed to investigate apply-
ing facial features to detect dementia. In this study, we especially
explored the use of face mesh. We proposed a data augmentation
method to mitigate the lack of data and investigated three common
deep learning models to improve the classification performance.
The structure of the paper is organized as follows: Section II
introduces the related works that used facial features to detect de-
mentia. Section III describes the dataset, face mesh, and proposed
methods used in this research. Section IV presents the experimen-
tal settings as well as the results and performance of the proposed
methods. Section V concludes this paper.
2. RELATED WORKS
Asplund et al. [11, 12] reported notable variations in facial expres-
sions of people with dementia and showed it possible to identify
dementia based on the variations in facial expressions. According
to Asplund et al. [11], certain dementia patients have a tendency
to show fewer facial expressions. Their investigation, which was
done under both happy and unpleasant stimulus settings utilizing the
Facial Action Coding System (FACS), revealed a decreased capac-
ity for expression production. They investigated two techniques for
reading facial expressions in dementia patients and found differences
between them in a follow-up research [12]. Patients with dementia
have been seen to display reduced facial cues in quantity or clarity.
These results imply that variations in the facial expressions of those
suffering from dementia may act as markers for dementia.
In recent years, several studies [9, 10, 13] have gathered fa-
cial expression data from individuals with dementia, exploring the
potential of utilizing facial expressions for dementia detection. To
overcome the under-researched aspect of facial expression in de-
mentia studies relative to speech and language data, the researchers
presented an automated method for dementia identification from a

mentia markers. Zheng et al. [14] explored the use of facial features
for dementia detection. They extracted face mesh, AUs, and HoG
(Histogram of Oriented Gradients) features from interview videos
involving dementia patients and healthy subjects. Utilizing a vot-
ing system in conjunction with SVM as the classifier, they attained a
73% accuracy, specifically leveraging the AUs data.
3. METHODS
3.1. PROMPT Dataset
Approved by Keio University School of Medicine Ethics Committee,
the Keio Medical School collected voice and video data from partic-
ipants of healthy subjects and dementia patients for the PROMPT
dataset [15] with informed consent obtained. In the collection pro-
cess, the dementia patients participated in several interview sessions
conducted by professionals, with at least a 2-week gap between ses-
sions. During each session, the patients were required to undergo
several interviews. The video and audio recordings of the sessions
were captured separately from two microphones and an Intel Re-
alsense camera. One microphone was set at the patients’ place and
another set at the doctor’s place. The camera was set on the table in
front of the patients, with a distance of about 1 meter between them.
The study involved three tasks: free speech, where participants en-
gaged in casual guided conversations with the interviewer; question
and answer (Q&A), where participants responded to complex situ-
ational queries. Additionally, participants described the content of
an image depicting a scenario with children arguing over food in the
picture description task.
The PROMPT dataset comprises 446 samples collected from de-
mentia patients and healthy subjects, encompassing both dementia
and healthy cases, obtained from 117 subjects, each sample spanning
approximately 10 minutes. Despite its richness, this dataset falls
short in size compared to conventional machine learning datasets,
posing challenges in training a generalizable and robust deep learn-
ing model. To address this limitation, our research introduces a novel
approach by leveraging data augmentation. This strategy aims to
overcome the over-fitting problems inherent in smaller datasets, en-
hancing the model’s ability to generalize effectively and to facilitate
the training of more proficient deep learning models.

3.2. Face Mesh
Face meshes can be represented as a set of points or vertices that
define the facial structure, rather than storing a complete image of
the face. This reduces the risk of identifying individuals from fa-
cial features, thus enhancing anonymity and privacy. It also hides
race, gender, or age information from the viewers. This function al-
lows medical experts to watch and evaluate facial expressions and
movements while maintaining patient privacy. This feature is espe-
cially helpful in telemedicine settings where patients are unable to
see medical professionals in person. Because no third party will be
able to determine who they are, it also motivates future AD sufferers
to get screened early. Doctors and other medical professionals may
evaluate patients’ status remotely while keeping secure the patients’
personal identification.
3.3. Data Augmentation
Although data augmentation has been extensively used in tasks deal-
ing with image data or text data, data augmentation methods for face
mesh data are not receiving attention from researchers. Face mesh
data, as opposed to image data, employs a 3- dimensional structure
as it contains x, y, z coordinates for each landmark it extracts. This
is making the data augmentation different from that applied in the
2-dimensional images. Each face mesh can be transformed in 3-D
space as long as the relative position among landmarks remains the
same. In addition to that, this research aims to detect dementia by
evaluating the temporal patterns from the face mesh time series data,
which makes it necessary to keep temporal consistency before and
after augmentation. In this research, analogous to the data augmen-
tation methods commonly used for image data, we proposed 3 kinds
of augmentation methods, featuring rotating, shifting, and scaling.
• Rotation: Randomly rotating the face mesh time series data.
Specifically, for each face mesh time series data to be aug-
mented, we randomly decided a rotation value (5 to 10 degrees)
on each of the x, y, z axes. A rotation matrix is then created and
applied to each landmark in each frame of the data.
• Shifting: Randomly shifting the face mesh time series data.
Specifically, we randomly decide a shift between −0.5 to 0.5
on each of the x, y, z axes, which were later applied to each
landmark of each frame, of the face mesh data.
• Scaling:
Randomly scaling the face mesh time series data.
Specifically, we first decide a factor between [0.9, 1.1). Then
this factor was applied to scale the face mesh data.

3.4. Models
We used Transformer, LSTM, and the 1D CNN network to perform
classification between dementia and healthy samples.
The trans-
former model contains only 1 transformer encoder layer, with 8 at-
tention heads. The dropout rate was set to 0.5. The global average
pooling layer compresses the hidden states of 32 to 1 and will be
classified by the linear layer at the end of the model. We used the
Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 2 · 10−3 and set the batch
size to 1024. The cross-entropy loss function was used to calculate
the model loss.
The LSTM network is composed of 1 LSTM layer whose hidden
state size was set to 64. The principal components are input into each
cell of the LSTM layer and the hidden states of the last cell in the
LSTM layer are forwarded to the linear layer (with 64 input features
and 2 output features) and softmax layer for the final decision. We
used Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 2·10−3 and set the batch
size to 256. The cross-entropy loss function was used to calculate the
model loss.
The 1D CNN network is composed of 3 groups of CNN net-
works. Each group of networks is composed of a 1D CNN layer, a
Max Pooling layer and a Relu activation layer. The 1D CNN layer
has 32 in and out channels with a kernel size of 3. The Max Pooling
layer has a kernel size of 2. A fully-connected network is finally ap-
plied to make predictions. We used Adam optimizer with a learning
rate of 2·10−3 and set the batch size to maximum. The cross-entropy
loss function was used to calculate the model loss.
4. EXPERIMENT SETTINGS AND RESULTS
Following the common way to measure the performance of a ma-
chine learning task, we implemented a 5-fold cross-validation in the
experiments. In each fold, 20% of the subjects were held out as the
test set. In the training of each fold, we train transformer models,
LSTM models, and 1D CNN models for 120 epochs, 400 epochs,
and 120 epochs respectively. The epoch numbers are large enough
for the model to converge on the training set, getting an accuracy
of more than 99% on training data. We train the LSTM models for
more epochs because they converge more slowly.
In this research, we implemented 3 kinds of data augmentation
methods to increase the size of the data. Each time we applied 1
method of the three methods including rotating, shifting, and scaling.
From each real face mesh sample video in the PROMPT dataset,
we created an extra virtual sample by augmentation. As such, we
created 3 extra datasets: virtual dataset 1 created by rotating, virtual

We implemented an incremental PCA method to reduce the di-
mensionality of the dataset with a batch size of 64 while processing
dataset incrementally. The PCA is an unsupervised learning pro-
cess, and it is performed on the augmented dataset (the real dataset
with one of the virtual datasets) with each frame of the face mesh
as a sample, this converts a frame of the face mesh into 32 prin-
cipal components, which represent more than 99.9% variation in-
formation of the original face mesh dataset, according to our ex-
periments. After doing the PCA reduction, a face mesh time se-
ries sample in the shape of [Nsteps, Nlandmarks, Naxes] was converted to
the shape of [Nsteps, Ncomponents], where Nsteps refers to the number of
frames, Nlandmarks refers to the number of landmarks, Naxes refers to
the number of elements each landmark has to annotate its location,
and Ncomponents refers to the number of principal components.

1D CNN model demonstrates consistent improvement with all aug-

We noticed from the tables that shifting and rotation generally
improve model performance, particularly for the Transformer and
1D CNN models. The augmentation of Mediapipe face mesh data
shows promising results in enhancing the performance of deep learn-
ing models for dementia detection, with specific augmentations ben-
efiting different models uniquely. Though applying a single data
augmentation method on the deep learning models reports improve-
ment on certain levels when all 3 methods were applied, the accuracy
performance became worse. The reason behind it probably is when
all 3 methods were used, the train data distribution was largely al-
tered, which does not share commonness with test data anymore.

frequency spectrum, and spectrum differentiation. From these, we
extracted statistical features such as mean, median, standard devia-
tion, extremes, and key percentiles (e.g., 1%, 5%, 10%, etc.). Using
a 5-fold cross-validation, we employed a Gradient Boosted Decision
Tree (GBDT) classifier with 100 estimators, a learning rate of 1, and

Unlike the LSTM and Transformer models, the GBDT model
does not consistently improve with different augmentation. Its per-
formance varies with different max depths. From Table 2, we see
that a single data augmentation technique does not improve perfor-
mance in most cases. However, it yields the highest accuracy of
0.720 when all the augmentation methods were applied, which is
similar to the baseline model with a max depth of 4 (0.719). This
inconsistency between feature engineering methods and deep learn-
ing methods was probably caused by the different processing before
classifying. Data augmentation techniques such as rotation, shifting,
and scaling primarily affect the raw data, adding variations to the
original dataset. However, if feature engineering extracts aspects of
the data that are invariant to these transformations or focuses on fea-
tures that are not significantly altered by augmentation, the impact of
augmentation might be minimized in the final model training. Com-
pared with deep learning models that automatically find patterns in
data, GBDT responds less to augmentation possibly due to the de-
signed features not altering with augmentation.
5. CONCLUSION
By implementing and analyzing various models such as LSTM,
Transformer, 1D CNN, and GBDT coupled with different data aug-
mentation methods like rotation, shifting, and scaling, we have
gained crucial insights into the potentials and limitations of deep
learning and machine learning models in medical diagnostics. The
proposed data augmentation methods improved the performance
of different deep learning models. We achieved the best accuracy
performance of 0.76 by employing a 1D CNN model with shift-
ing augmentation. These results confirmed the effectiveness of our
data augmentation methods. They also underscore the potential of
face-related features in enhancing early detection approaches for
AD.
