Biological age (BA) has proven to be a more accurate predictor of longevity
and health span than chronological age, offering a dynamic measure of
physiological integrity influenced by genetics, environment, and lifestyle.
This narrative literature review critically examines recent advances in
machine learning (ML) methodologies designed to estimate BA from
minimally invasive data sources, including facial imaging, chest X-rays, brain
MRIs, blood-based biomarkers, electrocardiograms, and psychological
surveys. As interest grows in personalized medicine and early prevention of
age-related diseases, the demand for scalable, affordable, and non-invasive
BA estimation tools is increasing. Unlike previous reviews limited to a single
modality, this review uniquely compares biological age (BA) prediction
models based on their invasiveness, cost-effectiveness, and mean absolute
error (MAE), offering a clinically oriented synthesis for non-invasive ageing
diagnostics. This paper provides a needed, comprehensive, and up-to-date
synthesis of these disparate approaches. Our analysis organizes these
diverse methods into a unified comparative framework, systematically
evaluating them on the trade-offs between predictive accuracy (MAE),
invasiveness, and translational feasibility. While DNA methylation-based
epigenetic clocks remain the reference standard, emerging ML-based
models utilizing routine clinical or digital health data demonstrate
comparable predictive performance with significantly lower cost and
invasiveness. Nevertheless, emerging challenges, including variability in data
quality, lack of external validation, issues of interpretability, and ethical
considerations, still exist and need to be addressed in further studies.
Advancing these technologies may ultimately enable the integration of noninvasive ageing biomarkers into routine healthcare, transforming risk
assessment, intervention planning, and the promotion of longevity.

| Introduction

Ageing is characterized by the progressive decline of an
organism’s
physiological
function
and
increased
vulnerability to death overtime (López-Otín et al., 2013).
An organism as a system or part of it may age faster or
slower than the “normal” rate of ageing. In humans, the
rate of progressive ageing can be influenced by internal or
external factors, and certain genetic alterations and
lifestyle habits can accelerate, decelerate, or reverse the
ageing process in time.
The concept of biological age was first introduced in
1969 and gained importance when Alex Comfort
introduced the theory of the 'Test-Battery' as a method for
measuring the rate at which humans age over short
periods (Comfort, 1969). Then, chronological age (CA) was
described as the amount of time that has passed since a
person’s birth, whereas the biological age reflects an
individual’s overall state of health and condition at a given
point in their life (Ries et al., 1984). These early concepts
have evolved since their introduction and today with
modern methods that allow for microscopic analysis of
cells. BA is most efficiently measured by epigenome
studies including the methylation at certain CpG sites, such
as GrimAge2 (Lu et al., 2022). Briefly, the GrimAge2
concept stems from the notion of 9 hallmarks of ageing,
epigenetic alteration being one of the hallmarks (López-
Otín et al., 2013) extend of introduction of altered cellular
communication, genomic instability, telomere attrition,
loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing,
mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, and stem
cell exhaustion.
Some of these hallmarks may influence one another
(e.g., cellular senescence is now known to be caused by
telomere shortening but could also be caused by other
factors). To date, many alternatives were developed to
Horvath’s method, such as those which rely on machine
learning (ML) approaches, as GrimAge and other similar
epigenetic tests, might be limited to use some sort of ML
or statistical methods to process the large data samples. In
Horvath’s GrimAge2 test, the use of Cox regression with an
elastic net penalty, as a model often used to assess the
effect of various factors on the time until an event of
interest occurs, to process data from 10,065 subjects and
13,399 samples. The data included chronological age,
gender, ten epigenetic markers estimating smoking packyears (1 pack/day for 1 year = 1 pack-year), and levels of
the
following
biomarkers:
adrenomedullin,
beta-2
microglobulin, cystatin C, growth differentiation factor 15,
leptin, log-transformed high-sensitivity C-reactive protein,
log-transformed haemoglobin A1C, plasminogen activator
inhibitor 1, and tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases 1 (Lu
et al., 2022).
Epigenetic tests, due to their high accuracy, are now
the standard way for measuring BA. At first glance, they
might seem ideal. They provide valuable insights into
overall health and can encourage individuals to adopt
healthier habits or pursue targeted treatments if the
results are concerning. However, while these tests hold
promise, their broader adoption and currently, their
popularity is comparable to that of genealogy genetic tests
and reveals certain limitations that warrant further
consideration, such as cost. While not prohibitively
expensive, this cost may deter some individuals if the test
is not necessary. These tests are considered as invasive and
require an additional effort to perform in safe manner.
Even though services like myDNAge offer an easier athome sampling experience, they aren't necessarily cheap.
The process still requires users to collect a sample — either
blood from a skin prick or urine — and then return the kit
for analysis. This lack of true convenience remains a turnoff for many, significantly reducing their motivation to find
out their biological age.
With
the
recent
advancements
in
artificial
intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML), new methods
arise to fill in the gaps that epigenetic tests cannot. ML
algorithms can process images, written text, and numerical
values and find patterns within them. This ability opens up
new ways in which BA can be measured. AI can help make
biological age testing more efficient, less costly, and
minimally invasive, thus popularizing its assessment. Apart
from that, they allow doctors to measure the BA of their
patients without needing a dedicated test (like in the case
of epigenetic clock tests).
With ML, factors that not only cause ageing, but also
the ones influenced by it can be analysed: wrinkling of the
skin, changes in blood biochemistry, senescence of brain
and lung cells, or decline in heart function, just to name a
few. While previous reviews have provided valuable
insights into specific modalities, such as neuroimaging or
blood-based biomarkers, the rapid pace of innovation
across
multiple
disciplines
necessitates
a
more
comprehensive and updated synthesis. The primary
contribution of this review is its breadth and currency. By
structuring our analysis around a direct comparison of
predictive accuracy (MAE), invasiveness, and accessibility,
we create a practical, holistic framework for researchers
and clinicians. This approach allows us to map the current
state-of-the-art, identify the most promising strategies for
clinical translation, and highlight the shared challenges
that must be addressed to integrate BA estimation into
personalized medicine.

The world's record population which in 2022 reached
8 billion people was driven predominantly by enlargement
of elderly people due to life span increasing (The Lancet
Healthy Longevity, 2022). The share of the oldest adults in
the age structure of populations is progressively enlarged.
While 20 years ago the fastest growing age group was the
80+ years, now it is the 85+ year: according to prognosis in
the USA their number will double by 2036 and triple by
2049 compared to 2010 (Arias, 2010). At the same time,
healthy life expectancy, which is a global indicator of
population health, will not change significantly (WHO,
2016). The discrepancy between life expectancy and
healthy life expectancy led to the fact that Europeans
spend 20–25% of their lives in poor health (Jagger et al.,
2013; Rezaei, 2024).
The well-known decline in physical capacity with
aging is a focus of attention for geriatricians. Maintaining
muscle strength, endurance and other parameters of
physical health is crucial for preserving the functionality of
older adults, ensuring their independence (Santos et al.,
2016). There were elaborated a lot of questionnaires and
objective tests to obtain characteristics of physical activity
limitation: in a systematic review thirty-one assessment
tests for mobility evaluation in elderly were identificated
(Soubra et al., 2019). Various tests can assess body
composition, cardiorespiratory endurance, muscular
strength and endurance, and flexibility and balance
separately or in combination such as The Vitality Test
Battery (Mack-Inocentio et al., 2020).
Although numerous mobility assessment tools exist,
evidence regarding their relative diagnostic value in
functionally independent older adults with essential
hypertension remains limited. This study aims to fill this
gap by ranking widely used mobility tests according to their
association with age and screening results for frailty,
sarcopenia risk, and fall risk in middle-aged and older
hypertensive patients.

| 1. Biomarkers used in biological age assessment

BA assessment relies on identifying and quantifying
biomarkers that reflect the functional state of tissues,
organs, and systems more accurately than CA alone. As
ageing affects nearly every organ and cellular system,
researchers should turn to ML approaches of determining
BA instead of the costly and invasive epigenetic testing:
analyzing blood samples (markers other than epigenetic),
MRIs, X-Rays, images of faces and skin, as well as
psychological profiling. These shift toward multidimensional, integrative biomarkers would mark a critical
step in making this sort of diagnosis more accessible. This
section focuses on how different areas of the body display
accelerated ageing.

1.1 Systemic biomarkers of ageing

Systemic biomarkers of ageing, particularly those derived
from blood biochemistry, offer a dynamic and integrative
perspective on the molecular and physiological changes
that accumulate over time. Blood-based markers reflect
ongoing processes such as chronic inflammation, oxidative
stress, metabolic dysregulation, and organ system decline,
all key hallmarks of biological ageing. Routine clinical
parameters, including levels of glucose, lipids, creatinine,
C-reactive protein (CRP), albumin, and liver enzymes, have
shown consistent associations with age-related morbidity
and mortality, as well as offer the good accessibility and
cost-effectiveness
of
blood
tests
make
systemic
biomarkers being scalable for ageing research and
personalized health monitoring.
Ageing is marked by progressive declines in hepatic,
renal, and endocrine function, which alter blood biomarker
profiles. For instance, albumin levels drop due to reduced
hepatic synthesis and increased capillary permeability,
glucose
rises
reflecting
insulin
resistance
and
mitochondrial inefficiency, and urea and creatinine
increase with reduced glomerular filtration rate (GFR)
(Putin et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2024).
Oxidative stress amplifies these changes. Elevated γglutamyltransferase (GGT) and uric acid mediate 17.1% of
the association between cardiovascular health (Life’s
Essential 8 score) and accelerated phenotypic ageing (Liu
et al., 2018). With age, the amount of inflammation and
immunosenescent cells increases. C-reactive protein (CRP)
increases by 0.2 mg/L per decade, driven by senescent cell
accumulation and inflammation. In NHANES cohorts, proinflammatory diets elevated white matter hyperintensity
volume.

1.2 Biomarkers of facial and skin ageing

Ageing of the skin and facial structures involves molecular,
structural, and functional changes driven by mitochondrial
dysfunction, oxidative stress, hormonal shifts, and
cumulative DNA damage. These processes manifest as
wrinkles, volume loss, pigmentation irregularities, and
altered facial contours. The human face and skin offer a
visually accessible and biologically informative source of
ageing biomarkers, making them increasingly valuable in
the development of machine learning models aimed at
estimating biological age. Facial morphology, skin texture,
wrinkle formation, pigmentation changes, and loss of

ageing and reflect underlying physiological and molecular
alterations, including collagen degradation, oxidative
stress, and reduced cellular turnover. Advances in
computer vision and deep learning have enabled the
quantification of subtle facial features and skin attributes
from digital images, allowing for the construction of
predictive models that can estimate biological or perceived

visible signs of chronological ageing but also integrate cues
associated with lifestyle, environmental exposures, and

Intrinsic ageing arises from genetically programmed
processes that impair cellular repair and regeneration.
Over time, fibroblasts in the dermis produce less collagen
and elastin, leading to reduced skin elasticity and tensile
strength (Guinot et al., 2002). The decline in collagen
synthesis is exacerbated by the accumulation of senescent
cells, which secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines that
degrade the extracellular matrix (ECM) (P. Chevalier et al.,
2020). Hormonal changes further modulate ageing
trajectories. Postmenopausal estrogen depletion reduces
skin thickness and hydration, while androgens in males
contribute to sebaceous gland hyperactivity and thicker,
more resilient skin (Keaney et al., 2016).
Extrinsic ageing is predominantly driven by ultraviolet
(UV) radiation, which accounts for up to 80% of visible
facial ageing. UVB rays penetrate the epidermis, inducing
DNA crosslinks and thymine dimer formation. UVA rays,
however, generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) in the
dermis, activating matrix metalloproteinases that degrade
collagen (Krutmann et al., 2003). Chronic sun exposure also
disrupts
melanocyte
function,
leading
to
hyperpigmentation and lentigines. Smoking compounds
these effects by constricting dermal blood vessels,
reducing oxygen delivery, and inhibiting collagen synthesis
(Guinot et al., 2002).
Over time, structural changes also arise. The medial
cheek fat pad, which provides midface support, diminishes
with age, leading to nasolabial fold prominence and jowl
formation (Bravo et al., 2020) while repetitive facial
expressions induce wrinkles that become permanent over
time (Rohrich et al., 2021).





1.3 Biomarkers of the cardio-respiratory system
ageing

The cardio-respiratory system undergoes significant agerelated remodelling, offering valuable biomarkers for the
estimation of biological age. In the respiratory system,
ageing induces both structural and functional changes,
which can all be visualized through subtle radiographic
alterations in chest imaging. The cardiovascular system
also
exhibits
hallmark
features
of
ageing.
The
electrocardiogram (ECG), a widely available and noninvasive tool, reflects many of these age-related cardiac
alterations.
The aortic knob widens laterally, with calcifications
frequently outlining the arch. In longitudinal studies, 79%
of healthy men exhibited aortic enlargement over 16 years
(independent of hypertension). While the cardiac
silhouette may slightly enlarge due to pericardial fat
accumulation, the cardiothoracic ratio rarely exceeds 0.5
in healthy ageing.
Flattened diaphragms and increased retrosternal
airspace are common, reflecting loss of elastic recoil.
Hyperinflation prevalence doubles from 27% to 54%
between the sixth and ninth decades, even in
asymptomatic individuals. Costophrenic angle blunting
occurs in 30% of individuals over 70, related to subpleural
fibrosis rather than prior inflammation (Ensor et al., 1983).
Fibrosis (scarring) of the sinoatrial node and His-
Purkinje system prolongs P-wave and QRS durations.
Interatrial conduction block, present in 23% of elderly
atrial flutter patients, correlates with a 175 ms P-wave
duration, 16 ms longer than in younger cohorts (Ślimak et
al., 2022).
A study on guinea pigs across their lifespan revealed
that sinoatrial node (SAN) dysfunction with age is linked to
a progressive loss of connexin 43 (Cx43) protein (Jones et
al., 2004). This manifests as QT interval prolongation,
which increases by 10 ms per decade in adults without
hypertension (Zhang et al., 2021).
Resting heart rate also tends to rise with age,
especially in those with schizophrenia, partly due to
antipsychotic-induced sodium channel blockade (Blom et
al., 2014). Structurally, changes like prolonged P-waves
suggest right atrial enlargement, while fragmented QRS
complexes are signs of conduction issues common in older
adults with increased heart mass (Ślimak et al., 2022;
Piccirillo et al., 2022).

1.4 Biomarkers of neurological and psychological
ageing

Ageing induces a complex of structural, metabolic, and
molecular changes in the brain, which manifest as distinct
neuroimaging and electrophysiological signatures. It is
accompanied
by
psychological
and
cognitive
transformations that reflect both neurobiological changes
and psychosocial adaptations.
Progressive cortical thinning and subcortical volume
loss represent hallmarks of brain ageing. The hippocampus
exhibits an annual atrophy rate of 1–2% after age 50,
correlating with episodic memory decline. Expansion of the
lateral ventricles by 2–3% per decade reflects parenchymal
volume loss and disrupted cerebrospinal fluid dynamics
(Dartora et al., 2024).
Quantitative susceptibility mapping reveals agerelated iron accumulation in the substantia nigra and
subthalamic nucleus, increasing by 0.8–1.2 ppb/year
(Falahati et al., 2024). This iron dysregulation contributes
to oxidative stress, accelerating neuronal loss in
Parkinson’s disease.
White matter hyperintensities increase by 1.5–3.0
cm³ per decade, reflecting small vessel disease and bloodbrain barrier leakage (Melo Van Lent et al., 2023).
Recently, studies have shown that microplastics
(MPs)
introduce
many
complications,
including
accelerated ageing. MPs induce chronic activation of
microglia, leading to elevated interleukin-6 and tumour
necrosis factor-alpha levels, as demonstrated in rodent
models of global cerebral ischemia (Kim et al., 2025).
Prolonged inflammation accelerates neuronal death and
synaptic loss, processes detectable via MRI as reduced grey
matter
volume
and
increased
white
matter
hyperintensities.
Subjective age — the discrepancy between felt and
chronological age — serves as a robust predictor of health
outcomes. Individuals who perceive themselves as older
than their actual age exhibit higher all-cause mortality risk
and accelerated epigenetic ageing (Zhavoronkov et al.,
2020; Stephan et al., 2015). For example, rheumatoid
arthritis patients report 18% older subjective ages than
healthy peers, mediated by chronic pain and mobility
limitations
(Schönstein
et
al.,
2020).
Conversely,
centenarians in Sardinia demonstrate psychological
resilience linked to social desirability and community
engagement, which buffer against age-related stress
(Fastame MC, 2020). Observed deviations between
predicted brain age and chronological age, defined as the
brain age gap, are gaining increasing recognition as a
clinically relevant index of neurological health and
biological ageing.
Self-reflection and lifelong learning build cognitive
reserve, delaying dementia onset by 5–7 years. Older
adults with high reserve maintain intact memory despite
amyloid positivity, likely through enhanced default mode
network connectivity (Bartrés-Faz et al., 2023).
Alzheimer’s disease (AD), the most common form of
dementia, can be diagnosed and is a strong marker for BA.
It is characterized by amyloid-β plaques, neurofibrillary tau
tangles, and synaptic loss, which correlate with progressive
cognitive decline. Epigenetic ageing accelerates in AD
patients and shows strong associations with neuritic
plaque burden and amyloid load (Thrush et al., 2022;
Levine et al., 2015). Vascular contributions to dementia,
particularly cerebral small vessel disease, further
complicate the ageing-neurodegeneration link (Inoue et
al., 2023).

| 2. Predicting biological ageing with machine
learning

Ageing involves numerous factors, causing quantifiable
and previously unquantifiable changes throughout the
body. ML and AI are now enabling us to measure these agerelated deteriorations, particularly in tissues that can be
photographed or scanned. This section will explore various
cutting-edge ML models that leverage different biological
data sources to predict biological age.

2.1 Face image analysis

The look of a face is one of the most prominent markers of
how well an individual age. It’s how humans tend to assess
BA every day (e.g., someone ages well when their face
doesn’t change much over a long period).
PhotoAgeClock (Bobrov et al., 2018) primarily focuses
on predicting CA, not BA. It does so use high-resolution
images of human skin around the eyes. The image and
label data used to train the model were sourced from
public facial image datasets: FG-NET, MORPH, Adience,
and IMDb-WIKI. To benchmark the model's performance
against other existing methods of determining BA (like the
already mentioned GrimAge DNA methylation test and
blood biochemistry age estimators).
Using eye-corner images, the team trained Xception
NNs and then added skip connections to increase accuracy.
The study sought to determine how important the eyecorner area is for making an accurate age prediction.
Wrinkles, pigmentation, and fine skin details around the
eye corner were hypothesized to be strong indicators of

Another model, FaceAge, sourced its training data

Unlike

focuses
on

the model’s design is tailored to capture markers of BA.
This is reflected in the results. For the entire dataset,
MAE = 5.87 years; however, in the curated dataset for the
60+ age range (which was prioritized): 4.09 years. Finetuned models showed lower MAE (<3 years) for healthy

2.2 Chest X-ray analysis

X-ray imagery gives insight into the internal structures of
the body of an individual. This is useful for BA prediction,
as not all health features are visible on the outside. The
chest area is highly potent in degrading over time
(Hockhegger et al., 2012). Hence, using chest radiography
as a parameter for determining BA could be a promising
method.
Raghu V.K. et al. developed CXR-Age, a deep learning
model designed to estimate biological age from chest Xrays (CXR) (Raghu et al., 2021). The model was trained on
data from 116,035 individuals and validated on two
separate testing sets: 75% of the CXR dataset of 40,967
from the PLCO (Prostate, Lung, Colorectal, and Ovarian
Cancer Screening Trial) and the CXR dataset of 5,414 from
the NLST (National Lung Screening Trial).
The development of CXR-Age consisted of two stages.
In Stage 1, a pre-training model was provided with a CXR
image to give an estimate of chronological age. The Stage
1 model was the starting point for a second model trained
to estimate BA from a CXR image. To train the model, it was
necessary to assign a BA label to each training dataset
participant, as opposed to the CA label linked to each
participant in the Stage 1 model. Biological age labels were
calculated using actuarial life tables and survival models.
For those who had died, labels were based on differences
between expected and actual age at death, while for
others, time-to-death was estimated using statistical
models. These labels were solely for model training and
were not reported in the results.
Overall, CXR-Age outperformed chronological age
and traditional radiographic findings in predicting all-cause
and cardiovascular mortality. Most individuals had a CXR-
Age within five years of their chronological age (PLCO: 73%;
NLST: 68%). However, a subset showed significant
deviation, with CXR-Age at least five years older or
younger, more prevalent in NLST due to heavier smoking
histories.

2.3 Brain MRI analysis

The degradation of the brain over time is likely the most
detrimental effect of ageing. It is the primary risk factor for
Alzheimer’s
disease,
memory
loss,
and
other
neurodegenerative disorders (Yankner et al., 2008).
Analyzing brain MRIs would not only help estimate the BA
of the whole body but also assess the BA of the brain with
greater accuracy (which is likely more important).
Age-Net is a deep learning (DL) model that analyses
brain MRIs to predict the age of a subject (Armanious et al.,
2021). The model used the open-source IXI dataset of brain
MRI scans from 562 healthy subjects. From the 562 scans,
420 were used for training, 92 for validation, and 50 for
testing.
A custom deep convolutional neural network (CNN)
architecture was used. It's a hybrid design incorporating
Inception v1 and SqueezeNet architectures. Two input
strategies were tested: 1) feeding the entire 3D GM
volume to the network, and 2) dividing the volume into

2.4 Blood biomarkers analysis

Mamoshina et al. developed a blood marker-based model
to determine the age of an individual (Mamoshina et al.,
2018). The team used four datasets containing 20,699
samples for the Canadian population, 65,760 samples for
the South Korean population, and 55,920 samples for the
Eastern European population. Each sample contained, 15
biochemistry
markers,
including
albumin,
glucose,
haemoglobin, cholesterol, sodium, urea, LDL cholesterol,
triglycerides, hematocrit, HDL cholesterol, total protein,
calcium, creatinine, potassium, and total bilirubin, and four
cell count markers, including erythrocytes, and platelet
count (19 features in total). The data was split into an
80/20 train-test ratio.
The model takes a vector of blood test values and
returns a single value of patient age. The performance of
three separate DNNs were trained and tested on four
distinctive datasets: The South Korean, Canadian, Eastern
European, and the combined dataset (all three) with either
19, 20, or 21 input features.
For the Canadian-trained model, the MAE validated
on Canadian patients was 6.36 years. For the South
Korean-trained model, the MAE validated on S. Korean
patients was 5.59 years. For the Eastern-European-trained
model, the MAE was validated on E. European patients
were 6.25 years. When the model trained on the combined
datasets was compared to the combined populations, the
MAE was 5.94 years; however, when the same model was
compared to an independent NHANES (National Health
and Nutrition Examination Survey) dataset (so US
patients), the MAE was 9.93 years. The Canadian-trained
model performed even worse with the NHANES test, with
an MAE of 12.65.
Another study developed by Sagers L. et. al. also
makes an age prediction from lab data (Sagers et al., 2020).
The study aimed to predict chronological age using 356
blood biomarkers (e.g., serum glucose, creatinine) across
67,563 individuals. Data were obtained from the CDC
NHANES datasets spanning the years 1999-2016. This
dataset includes a representative sample of the noninstitutionalized U.S. population.
The team used the random forest technique to train
their models. Models trained on one age group generally
performed poorly when applied to others, demonstrating
the need for age-specific models. Hence, separate models
were trained and validated for four age groups.
The study identified nonlinear trajectories for 28.4%
of analytes, with significant slope changes around the 16-
17-year mark, suggesting a critical biological transition in
late adolescence. The four models trained on the four age
groups displayed the following MAEs: 0.87 years (1-18),
5.15 years (18-45), 2.20 years (45-65), and 4.30 years (65+).
Mamoshina et al. and Sagers et al. differ in their
approaches to age prediction from blood markers.
Mamoshina's DNNs, using 19 features across diverse
populations, struggle with cross-population applicability.
Sagers' random forest models, with 356 biomarkers,
achieve better accuracy using age-specific models,
highlighting
nonlinear
trajectories
around
late
adolescence.

2.5 ECG analysis

Heart activity is impacted by age (Craft et al., 1995). Older
individuals exhibit slower heart rates, reduced heart rate
variability (HRV), as indicated by smaller deviations in R-R
intervals, slower electrical signal transmission from the
atria to the ventricles, and prolonged signal conduction
times, just to name a few.
One of the most insightful markers for heart activity
is the ECG. Baek Y. et. al. developed an AI model that
sought to analyze this marker to predict the mortality of a
given patient. ECG-heart age was estimated using standard
12-lead ECGs (Baek et al., 2023). Data from 522,109 ECGs
from a sample of 226,476 adults aged over 18 years was
used. An 80/20 train-test split was used.
The deep neural network, incorporating multi-head
attention and bi-directional LSTM blocks, processed data
from eight ECG leads (I, II, V1–V6) sampled at 500 Hz over
10 seconds, excluding derived leads (III, aVR, aVL, aVF). The
study evaluated biological heart age predictions against
various
outcomes,
including
all-cause
mortality,
cardiovascular-related
mortality,
major
adverse
cardiovascular
events
(MACE),
and
cardiovascular
hospitalizations, based on ECG features such as PR interval,
QRS duration, and QT interval.
A holdout test of 97,058 ECGs (34,317 patients) was
used to validate the model. The team reported the heart
age prediction of their model to have an MAE of 5.8 ± 3.9
years.

 2.6 Psychological and cognitive analysis

Psychological and cognitive function-based tests are not as
developed yet as other ways of determining BA, however,
they deserve further investigation due to their particularly
non-invasive nature (Erema et al., 2022). There are a few
ways of testing for BA using psychological and cognitive
markers. The most common category would rely on
marking behavioral features (this would be similar to tests
for dyslexia).
Zhavoronkov A. et. al. developed PsychoAge and
SubjAge in 2020 to predict chronological age based on
these parameters in a cohort of mentally healthy
individuals (Zhavoronkov et al., 2020). PsychoAge was
trained to approximate chronological age, and SubjAge –
subjective or biological age. The models were trained using
data from the MIDUS study (6071 individuals in total). 50
psychosocial features were selected as input variables for
the models. These features were categorized based on
their MIDUS descriptions, including aspects of health,
relationships, life changes, occupation, community
involvement,
personality,
psychological
beliefs,
demographics, and well-being.
Deep neural networks (DNNs) were used for both
PsychoAge and SubjAge. Five-fold cross-validation was
employed during training. Cross-validation helps to assess
the model's ability to generalize to unseen data and
prevent overfitting.
In the end, the models achieved an MAE of 6.7 years
for PsychoAge and 7.3 years for SubjAge. The subsequent
feature importance analysis allowed for the ranking of the
features and the selection of the most potent markers of
ageing among them. For both models, sex life, vigorous
activity (like being out of breath), taking prescription blood
pressure medication, and marital status were the most
important features, both physically and mentally.

| 3. Evaluation of the models

The models presented above are primarily designed to
determine an individual's age from data that is either easily
accessible or obtained while testing for something else.
While some models display low MAE, others do not. The
survey in Section 2 reveals a consistent, defining pattern: a
fundamental trade-off between predictive accuracy,
invasiveness, and accessibility. No single method is
superior on all fronts. When assessing the accuracy of each
model, there are a few things to consider:
First, the purpose of the model. There have been
multiple attempts at determining BA, which is a lot harder
to estimate than CA. While the majority of the models
presented do predict BA or have a BA-predicting
counterpart, some do not. For now, CA models tend to be
more accurate simply because data with truthful labels is
easier to obtain. The exception is the most accurate model
— Age-Net, which uses brain scans from which a lot more
data can be extracted per subject than, for example, from
a face image. It is the only model presented that surpassed
the 2-year MAE; however, this precision comes at the
highest cost and lowest scalability, restricting its utility. On
the other end of the spectrum lie the most scalable and
accessible methods. Models using facial images (FaceAge)
or psychological surveys (PsychoAge) offer unparalleled
ease of deployment but with currently lower accuracy
(MAE > 4 years).
Some models just use markers whose changes have a
lower correlation to the ageing of a subject. Changes in the
psychological markers and ECGs, for example, may very
likely have a weaker correlation to the BA of a subject
when compared to, e.g., brain scans or face images.
The quality and often complexity of the model also
contribute to its accuracy. This may be influenced by the
novelty of the particular markers. Newer, less developed
markers will likely have lower-quality models. This may be
the cause for the low accuracy in PsychoAge and SubjAge.
One of the most challenging obstacles to overcome
with BA prediction is acquiring large amounts of accurate
training and testing data. Most of the models presented
either have a lot of mediocre data, sourced from opensource databases, solely to gather as much data as
possible, or little, very high-quality, accurate data, which
was often manually curated. For a valid prediction, the
model should be trained on a lot of high-quality data,
which is difficult to acquire, especially for BA prediction.
For accurate BA prediction models, data points need
to be labelled with the biological ages of the subjects.
There are a few ways of achieving this:
Using the time of natural death of a subject to
determine their BA, as done by e.g., Fekrazad A., where the
data was taken from deceased individuals whose images
were found on the internet [Fekrazad, 2023]. This
approach, however, requires all the subjects to be
deceased, which would either yield data points with
insufficient parameters from the subjects (when data was
sourced from past studies) or only data on older individuals
who are not fully representative and wouldn’t produce a
good model for younger age groups. Raghu et. al. used a
hybrid with CXR-Age (Raghu et al., 2021). In this study, the
researchers defined BA as the age at which an individual's
risk of mortality matched the risk of an average person of
that age, according to actuarial life tables. For participants
who died during follow-up, their BA was calculated using
Figure 2.

Figure 2. Biological age is calculated by adding the difference between the expected age at death and actual chronological age at

The expected age at death was derived from 2015
actuarial life tables published by the U.S. Social Security
Administration. So, if an individual died exactly and their
expected age of death (expected age at death = CA at
death), their BA is the same as their CA.
For participants who did not die during follow-up,
their BA was estimated using sex-specific Gaussian survival
models. These models 1) were trained on data from the
control arm of the PLCO trial; 2) predicted each
participant's time-to-death based on their sex and other
survival-related variables; 3) used the newly predicted age
at death for the CA at death in the formula above.
Armanious et. al addressed the lack of “ground-truth
labels” by developing an iterative data-cleaning strategy to
find correct BA labels in a dataset with CA labels
(Armanious et al., 2021). The process aimed to refine a
"typical-ageing dataset" where BA ≈ CA by systematically
removing outliers. The researchers used the Age-Net-

calculated to flag outliers exhibiting accelerated ageing (BA
> CA) or delayed ageing (BA < CA). Patients falling outside
the typical ageing range were flagged as outliers and
removed. The process was repeated with a reshuffled
dataset, and the model was retrained from the beginning.
To prevent removing typically ageing individuals, only
those flagged in multiple iterations were excluded.
Iterations continued until no outliers were detected in
three consecutive runs, and all atypically ageing patients
were assumed to be removed. Hence, the model was
trained on individuals whose CA was very similar to their
BA, so their calendar ages could be used as labels. This
could yield inaccurate results for individuals whose BA
deviates a lot from their CA, as the models were not
trained on such cases.
The method of validation also differs from one model
to another. The models use different data as a validation
set. For models that predict BA, the labels in the validation
set were often derived in the same way as the training set,
leaving room for error in the label prediction process itself.
It can have a decent inaccuracy without being detected.
Hence, establishing labels cheaply and easily remains the
greatest obstacle in determining BA with ML and AI.

| Conclusions

Promoting the measurement of BA is important due to its
significant health benefits in enhancing the overall wellbeing of a population or individual. As one of the most
reliable indicators of overall health, a BA lower than CA
suggests opportunities for individuals to intervene more
effectively and can even serve as motivation to take
proactive health measures.
CA predictors discussed in this article lack direct
medical applications but can be useful for statistical
purposes, such as recording the age groups frequenting
certain locations with tools like PhotoAgeClock.
The biggest challenge in determining BA is
assigning accurate BA labels to individuals cost-effectively
and efficiently. Most of the studies reviewed rely on
workarounds such as statistical models, probabilities, and
outlier elimination. However, there remains a significant
shortage of high-quality, reliable, and validated BA data to
serve as robust labels for these models.  The most accurate
BA prediction model identified was Age-Net, achieving an
MAE of less than two years. It might even prove useful in
some medical scenarios (for example, as a preliminary age
test). Discussing the least accurate models is less
meaningful, as new, less effective models may continually
emerge.  Studies that trained multiple models, one for
each age group, often demonstrated higher accuracy when
tested on the respective age groups than the
corresponding general model. This demonstrates a need
for multiple models.


| Future perspectives

In making models less invasive, privacy concerns must be
addressed. For instance, models accessible via a web app
that requires users to share potentially sensitive
information with the app can raise privacy issues,
particularly as public awareness of data security grows.
Some of the models discussed are open source, allowing
them to be downloaded, run locally without server
dependence, and even modified or improved by others as
enhancing an existing model may often be more practical
than creating one from scratch. For example, models such
as FaceAge or ECG-Age may soon aid in clinical triage or
preventative screenings by highlighting patients whose BA
significantly deviates from their CA, prompting further
evaluation.
The comprehensive, cross-modal analysis conducted
in this review not only maps the current state of the art but
also illuminates the most critical directions for future
research. Based on our comparison of accuracy,
invasiveness, and accessibility, an ideal BA model must:
1. use a marker that highly correlates with BA;
2. be trained on data with real or highly accurate BA labels;
3. consist of age-group-specific models to improve
accuracy;
4. use validation data with accurate BA labels, ideally
derived from an independent method if the training data’s
accuracy is uncertain;
5. be open source to potentially address privacy concerns
and encourage collaborative improvement;
6. if released via a website, employ proper data safety
precautions and anonymize users.
