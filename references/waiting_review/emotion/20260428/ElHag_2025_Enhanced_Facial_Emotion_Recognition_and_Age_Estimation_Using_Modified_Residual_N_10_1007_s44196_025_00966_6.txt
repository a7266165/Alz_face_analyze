Abstract
Facial emotion recognition and age estimation are critical for human–computer interaction, yet existing methods struggle with variable image quality, diverse expressions, and aging patterns. To address these challenges,
this study proposes a novel hybrid model combining optimized image enhancement with deep feature extraction and machine learning classification. The methodology integrates three key phases: (1) image enhancement
using white balancing and adaptive gamma correction to improve edge intensity (82.235 vs. 62.89 in original
images) and texture clarity (gradient: 8.248 vs. 6.183); (2) feature extraction via a modified ResNet-18 trained
from scratch with monochromatic inputs; and (3) SVM classification of concatenated features from original and
enhanced images. Evaluated on the UTKFace dataset (1,000 images) and savory/unsavory expression datasets (600
images), the model achieves 98.75–100% accuracy, outperforming AlexNet (94.17%) and ResNet-KNN alternatives. Key findings demonstrate: (a) 5.4% higher age estimation accuracy (96.41% vs. 87.41%) with enhanced
gradient metrics, (b) 3.75% improvement in emotion recognition through edge preservation, and (c) interpretable
Grad-CAM visualizations validating feature relevance. The proposed system reduces misclassification errors by
60% for subtle expressions (e.g., fear vs. surprise) compared to state-of-the-art models. Practical applications in
healthcare show 40% faster patient check-in processing and robust performance under low-light conditions. This
work establishes that integrating physics-based enhancement with deep residual networks significantly improves
reliability for real-world deployment. Future research will optimize computational efficiency for edge devices
and expand multi-modal biometric integration.

serve as a pivotal conduit for social interaction and communication. Among the myriad modalities through which
emotions manifest, including facial expressions, body gestures, biological cues, and speech, facial emotions are
particularly salient. They are a primary vehicle for non-verbal communication in human interactions. Although
individuals can manipulate facial expressions to some extent, these expressions are predominantly involuntary,
intrinsically linked to underlying emotional states. This involuntary nature of facial expressions makes them a

The field of face emotion detection has emerged as a cornerstone in the domains of pattern recognition and

areas such as marketing and education.
In the realm of automatic face recognition, advancements have been made by exploiting various facial features.
These features are broadly divided into two categories: geometric properties and appearance-based features.
Geometric properties refer to the structural aspects of the face, such as the shape and size of facial components,
while appearance-based features encompass the texture and color variations within the face. Each category offers
unique insights and contributes to the overall efficacy of emotion recognition systems [4]. The confluence of these
methodologies heralds a new era in emotion detection, one where the nuanced complexities of human expressions
are more accurately deciphered by machines.
The quest to achieve both rapid and reliable performance in facial recognition systems remains a central challenge within the field. Facial recognition, as a sub-discipline of computer vision, grapples with the intricacies
of human facial expressions. Humans are capable of exhibiting a broad spectrum of emotions, including the six
fundamental emotions: happiness, sadness, fear, disgust, anger, and surprise. Beyond these, more nuanced expressions such as pain or complex emotional states further complicate the task of emotion recognition. The accurate
interpretation of these expressions via computer vision technology is pivotal for advancements in psychological
analysis and enhancing the efficacy of human–computer interactions [5].
Artificial Intelligence (AI), defined as the simulation of human intelligence in machines programmed to replicate and execute human-like actions and thought processes, has increasingly become a focal point of scientific
research. This burgeoning field encompasses a myriad of advancements in technology, signifying a transformative era in various sectors. AI aims to replicate aspects of human cognition, including learning, reasoning, and
problem-solving. This ambitious endeavor has profound implications across multiple domains, forecasting a

In response to the identified challenges in facial recognition, our research presents an integrated AI-based
model for face recognition and age estimation. The primary contribution of this paper is the development of a
hybrid model combining a modified ResNet-18 architecture with a Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier. The
model operates in three distinct phases: image enhancement, feature extraction, and simultaneous face emotion
recognition along with age estimation.
For the image enhancement phase, the model employs white balancing and gamma correction techniques, which
are instrumental in optimizing contrast and luminance in facial images. The modified Resnet-18 architecture is
then utilized, uniquely configured to initiate training from scratch with randomly assigned network parameters.
In a significant methodological adaptation, the number of input channels is reduced to one, facilitating training
with monochromatic images. This tailored approach allows the network to efficiently process and learn from the
image data.
Upon extracting the features, the SVM classifier comes into play. Its selection is predicated on its compatibility
with the modified Resnet-18 and its proven efficacy in classification tasks. Our empirical findings demonstrate
that the SVM achieves superior accuracy and performance in the post-feature extraction stage, validating the
effectiveness of this integrated approach.

3  Proposed Face Emotion Recognition Model
Deep learning, a subset of machine learning, is characterized by its employment of artificial neural networks
designed to mimic human cognitive functions. It has been widely implemented in fields such as image classification and speech recognition. In the context of facial emotion recognition, our proposed model leverages
the capabilities of residual networks, specifically Resnet-18. Resnet-18, a pre-trained deep learning network, is
represented by Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and is renowned for its efficiency in feature representation and extraction.
The architecture of the proposed model encompasses three primary stages: pre-processing, feature extraction,
and image classification (recognition) coupled with age estimation. The pre-processing stage focuses on image
enhancement to refine the quality of face images. This stage involves preparing, collecting, enhancing, and resizing input images to a uniform dimension of 224 × 224 pixels. Subsequently, these images are divided into training
and testing sets, adhering to an 80–20 split.
During the feature extraction stage, the ResNet-18 network is employed. This network facilitates the extraction
and concatenation of features from both enhanced and original face images, thereby enriching the dataset for the
subsequent classification task. This process is automated, requiring minimal human intervention.
For the classification phase, a SVM is utilized. The SVM classifier is fed with the distinct features extracted
by Resnet-18, capitalizing on the network’s ability to discern nuanced characteristics in facial expressions
automatically.
The model’s efficacy is assessed through rigorous testing with various face images to evaluate its predictive
capabilities. The datasets employed encompass a diverse range of facial images, including both savory and
unsavory expressions, and span different age groups, children, youth, and the elderly. The model demonstrates
versatility by performing both binary and multi-classification tasks with commendable accuracy. Figure 1 illustrates the workflow of the proposed model, showcasing the integration of image enhancement, feature extraction,
and classification stages in the context of face recognition and age estimation. The proposed framework operates

3.4  Face Image Enhancement Technique
The initial stage in our proposed model involves the enhancement of face images to optimize their contrast and luminance, which is critical for accurate feature extraction and subsequent recognition tasks. The enhancement process
commences with the application of a white balancing technique on the original images. The primary objective of
this step is to refine the image appearance by eliminating any unwanted color casts, thereby ensuring a more natural
and accurate color representation in the images. However, it is noteworthy that white balancing can have significant
effects, particularly as the absorbed colors in the original image may be challenging to recover fully. The proposed
model employs white balancing and gamma correction to enhance facial images before feature extraction. Key
metrics, edge intensity, average gradient, and entropy, are used to quantify the effectiveness of these enhancements.
Below, we analyze how these metrics directly impact the model’s recognition and classification accuracy.

In the feature extraction stage, we utilize the ResNet-18 deep learning network. This network is specifically
chosen for its ability to efficiently extract significant features from facial images, which are crucial for accurate
facial emotion recognition. ResNet-18 is designed to address the challenges associated with training deeper networks, particularly the problem of accuracy degradation.
In contrast to traditional networks where layers are directly learning the required mapping, residual networks
like ResNet-18 employ a different approach. They are configured to learn a residual mapping function, denoted
as F(x), through a few stacked layers. The rationale behind this approach is the assumption that if multiple nonlinear layers can asymptotically approximate a complex mapping function, they can also approximate the residual
function, expressed as H(x). The relationship between these functions is mathematically represented as follows:
In this architecture, stacked layers focus on recognizing the residual function H(x), which has been shown to
be optimized more effectively than the original function. The core idea of residual learning is to allow layers to
learn a residual function F(x) = H(x) − xF(x), where H(x) is the desired mapping and x is the input. The network
then reformulates the mapping as H(x) = F(x) + xH(x). This approach enables the optimization process to focus
on learning only the residual F(x), which is often easier than learning the complete transformation H(x) directly,
particularly in deep networks. By incorporating identity shortcut connections, ResNet-18 effectively mitigates the
vanishing gradient problem and allows deeper models to converge more efficiently. In our model, this characteristic helps capture subtle variations in facial features, essential for both emotion and age estimation, by enabling
deeper feature extraction without degradation in learning.
The Resnet-18 network, consisting of 18 layers including convolutional layers with a 3 × 3 filter size and a
stride of 2, is modified for our specific application. This modification involves training the network from scratch
with randomly initialized parameters and reducing the number of input channels to one for image processing.
3.6  Classification Task by SVM
In the realm of machine learning, the SVM algorithm plays a pivotal role, particularly in classification tasks such
as facial recognition. Post feature extraction, SVM is utilized to create optimal decision boundaries, which are
crucial for accurately categorizing multi-dimensional data into distinct classes. The primary goal of the SVM
algorithm is to determine the most effective hyperplane that segregates the n-dimensional space into categories,
thereby facilitating the correct classification of new data points.
The hyperplane is essentially the decision boundary that SVM seeks to establish. The algorithm identifies
and leverages the extreme points in the dataset, known as support vectors, which are critical in defining this
hyperplane. These support vectors are essentially the data points that are closest to the decision surface and play
a significant role in determining the position and orientation of the hyperplane (Fig. 5).

FPS, demonstrating flexible deployment scalability.

ResNet-RF/KNN variants, as shown in Table 14.

6.4  Limitations and Mitigation Strategies
While our model achieves state-of-the-art performance, we acknowledge several limitations requiring attention.
First, the dual enhancement and feature extraction pipeline incurs 40% higher computational costs (18.2 G FLOPs)
compared to AlexNet (12.9 G), though quantization to INT8 precision could reduce latency by 3.2 × with minimal
accuracy loss (< 1%) for edge deployment. Second, ethnic bias persists, with an 8.7% accuracy drop for African
faces due to UTKFace’s underrepresentation of darker skin tones (only 12% coverage); we are addressing this
through hospital partnerships to collect balanced multi-ethnic data by 2025. Third, occlusion sensitivity remains
a challenge, as accuracy declines 15–22% for masked or rotated faces (> 30° yaw), which we plan to mitigate via
attention masks in future work. Fourth, age estimation granularity degrades for elderly subjects (MAE increases
from 1.8 to 3.2 years for ages 60 +), a limitation we aim to resolve using StyleGAN-based synthetic aging augmentation. Finally, real-time performance currently lags behind lightweight models (14 FPS vs. MobileNet’s
23 FPS), but adopting depthwise separable convolutions could reduce parameters by 60% without sacrificing
accuracy. These targeted improvements will enhance the model’s robustness and deployability across diverse
real-world scenarios.
To address the noted bias due to underrepresentation of darker skin tones in UTKFace (only ~ 12% African
descent samples), we are actively exploring bias mitigation techniques. Preliminary experiments using Style-
GAN2-based synthetic data augmentation increased the minority class representation by 28%, leading to a 3.6%
improvement in classification accuracy for African facial images. Additionally, we applied reweighting techniques
during SVM training, assigning higher weights to underrepresented groups to balance decision boundaries. Fairness evaluation using the Disparity Ratio metric (Accuracy_minority/Accuracy_majority) improved from 0.892
to 0.942 post-mitigation, suggesting enhanced demographic parity. We plan to expand this direction through
real-world multi-ethnic data collection in partnership with regional hospitals.
Our model demonstrates exceptional discriminative power across both threshold-dependent and threshold-free
metrics. The ROC analysis reveals near-perfect AUC scores of 0.992 (95% CI: 0.989–0.995) for age estimation
and 0.987 (0.983–0.991) for emotion recognition, significantly outperforming ResNet-50 (0.963) and VGG16
(0.941) respectively. The steep left-upward trajectory of our curves (Fig. 16a) indicates superior true positive rates
(> 98%) even at stringent false positive thresholds (< 0.1), crucial for healthcare applications where false alarms
are costly. Precision–Recall analysis further confirms robustness, with macro-average AUPRC scores of 0.981
(age) and 0.979 (emotion), highlighting particular gains for challenging minority classes: “Fear” expressions
show a 0.21 AUPRC improvement over ResNet-RF, while elderly subjects (70 + years) gain 0.18 AUPRC due to
our enhanced wrinkle detection. These metrics collectively demonstrate that our hybrid architecture maintains
high precision across all recall levels, unlike end-to-end CNNs which typically sacrifice precision for marginal
recall gains.

To mitigate class imbalance and fairness issues, particularly the underrepresentation of African facial images

6.5  Ethical Considerations and Responsible Deployment
As facial analysis systems increasingly move toward real-world deployment, particularly in sensitive domains such
as healthcare, ethical and societal implications must be thoroughly addressed. Our system integrates Grad-CAM
visualizations to enhance interpretability, allowing clinicians to understand which facial regions the model uses
to make age or emotion predictions. While strong technical performance is crucial, it must be accompanied by
ethical safeguards to ensure trust and fairness in deployment.
Privacy is foundational. Any clinical or institutional application of our model must comply with HIPAA or
GDPR regulations. Facial image data should only be collected with explicit user consent, and must be processed
using secure and anonymized protocols to protect patient identity.
We also acknowledge the risks of bias in machine learning systems, particularly when training data lacks
demographic balance. As discussed in Sect. 6.1, the underrepresentation of certain skin tones can lead to accuracy disparities. To address this, we applied GAN-based augmentation and reweighting techniques, improving
fairness metrics such as the Disparity Ratio, which increased from 0.892 to 0.942. Continued efforts to evaluate
and report fairness metrics will be integral to future system updates.
Accountability is equally important. In high-stakes healthcare scenarios, such as patient monitoring or psychological assessment, misclassifications can have real-world consequences. We recommend a human-in-the-loop
approach, where AI outputs are reviewed by clinicians before action is taken. This hybrid decision-making model
ensures both accuracy and ethical soundness.
Finally, to support responsible deployment, future iterations of the system will integrate explainability dashboards, user feedback loops, and adherence to ethics-by-design principles. These features will help foster transparency, patient trust, and long-term acceptance of AI-assisted systems in clinical environments.
7  Conclusions and Future Work
This study advances facial analysis research by demonstrating that physics-based image enhancement (adaptive gamma correction, white balancing) and hybrid deep learning architectures (ResNet-18 + SVM) synergistically improve model interpretability and performance. Key theoretical insights include the superiority
of residual learning for age-invariant feature extraction, reducing gradient vanishing in deep networks by
37% compared to AlexNet. Feature concatenation from original/enhanced images enriches representation
diversity, increasing inter-class separation by 22% (verified via t-SNE plots). Grad-CAM interpretability
reveals that enhanced edges (intensity > 82.2) and textures (gradient > 8.2) are primary drivers of accurate
classification. The proposed system offers tangible benefits for real-world deployment: (1) Reduces patient
check-in processing time by 40% at Riyadh General Hospital (pilot data), with 98.2% ID matching accuracy
under variable lighting, (2) Achieves comparable accuracy to commercial solutions (e.g., Face++) using
60% fewer parameters, lowering cloud compute costs, and (3) Monochrome input adaptation enables us with
low-cost IR cameras (tested with Raspberry Pi v4). This work makes four key contributions to the field: (1)
