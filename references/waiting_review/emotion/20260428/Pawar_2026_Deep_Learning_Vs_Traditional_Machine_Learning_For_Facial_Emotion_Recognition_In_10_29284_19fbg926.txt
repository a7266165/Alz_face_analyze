Abstract
The early diagnosis of mental illnesses including stress, worry, and depression may be
greatly aided by facial expression identification. In order to classify six fundamental
emotions (anger, disgust, fear, happiness, sadness, and surprise), this study assesses the
performance of five machine learning models: K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN), Support
Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest (RF), Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), and a general
Neural Network (NN). Using the PyTorch and scikit-learn frameworks, experiments were
carried out on a system with an Intel i7-12700F CPU, 32 GB RAM, and an RTX 3070 GPU.
Accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score were used to compare models. With an overall
accuracy of 95% and high F1-scores across all emotion classes, the results show that the
MLP model performs better than all other approaches, proving its capacity to capture
intricate, non-linear emotional patterns. While a traditional NN performed worse (81%),
KNN, RF, and SVM obtained modest accuracies (62–84%). While simpler models like KNN
are still competitive for small datasets, deep neural networks perform better for larger or
more homogeneous data, according to analysis across several datasets. The results
encourage the inclusion of AI-driven technologies into real-time mental health monitoring
and intervention systems by highlighting the efficacy of deep learning for accurate emotion
recognition.


1. Introduction
This work uses a number of machine learning and deep learning classification techniques
to predict face emotions. A method called facial emotion recognition (FER) is used to
examine emotions in a variety of media, including pictures and movies. 55% of emotion
detection is attributed to facial expressions, a type of nonverbal communication that
offers clues about human feelings [1]. In human-computer interaction and psychology,
the interpretation of emotional emotions has grown in importance. The study of emotion
recognition's technological applications is being intensively researched because of its
significant importance [2]. Machine learning is a discipline of Artificial Intelligence (AI)
that focuses on constructing systems capable of learning from data without the need for
explicit programming by humans [3]. Conversely, Deep Learning (DL) is a branch of
Machine Learning. learning that uses a set of nonlinear transformation functions
organised in a deep and layered framework to create algorithms to represent data
abstractions at a higher level [4]. Several Machine Learning (ML) classification algorithms
were used in the modelling experiment, including: (1) Support Vector Machine (SVM),
which uses a hypothesis space in the form of linear functions on high-dimensional
features, trained using optimization-based algorithms to produce classification or
regression models [5]; (2) K-Nearest Neighbour (KNN), which can perform classification
without requiring modelling of data distribution, making it suitable for datasets of varying
sizes [6]; and (3) Random Forest (RF), an ensemble learning algorithm that builds
predictive models using a bootstrap (bagging) approach [7]. Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNN), a Deep Learning technique, are used to recognise expressions with great
accuracy by learning complex features [4]. Because Python supports multiple

2. Literature Review
Facial Emotion Recognition (FER) is described in the research journal by Yousif
Khaireddin and Zhuofa Chen as an essential component of human-computer interaction,
especially in clinical practice and behavioural assessment, which aids in the detection of
mental health conditions like depression, anxiety, and mood disorders. However, the
variety of human faces and image alterations, including variations in facial positions and
lighting conditions, continue to pose challenges to the accuracy and robustness of FER
models. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and other deep learning-based models
have demonstrated significant promise because of their computational efficiency and
capacity for automatic feature extraction. Without using any extra training data, a recent
study that used the VGGNet architecture obtained the maximum accuracy of 73.28% on
the FER2013 dataset. Hyperparameter tuning and the investigation of optimisation
techniques were credited with the model's effectiveness. Because of their high accuracy,
CNNs have a lot of potential for incorporation into mental health detection systems,
offering automated insights into an individual's emotional state that can facilitate early
diagnosis and more effective mental health treatment [12]. Simcock et al.'s study
investigates the connection between early adolescents' mental health and their capacity
to recognise facial emotions. Forty twelve-year-old children participated in the study.
They completed a face expression detection test and a mental health questionnaire to
evaluate symptoms of anxiety, depression, and somatization. The findings demonstrated
that teenagers with more psychiatric problems tended to process emotions biasedly,
especially when it came to negative expressions like fear and rage. Although more
research is still needed to understand the neural mechanisms causing this bias, it may
point to underlying mental health difficulties from an early age. These results
demonstrate how facial expression recognition can be used as a tool to identify early
indicators of mental illnesses, which is crucial for quicker teen diagnosis and treatment
[13]. Facial Emotion Recognition (FER) employing artificial intelligence (AI) technology
has demonstrated tremendous potential in supporting medical professionals, notably in
the realm of mental and emotional health. Previous research has shown that FER systems
that employ deep learning and machine learning methods, including Convolutional

A. The dataset
The Face Emotion Recognition Dataset, which Jonathan Oheix made available on the
Kaggle platform, is used in this study. The dataset is made up of photos of people's faces
that have been categorised into seven different emotions: Anger, joy, sadness, surprise,
neutrality, fear, and disgust. The intricacy of the emotion recognition process is increased
by the fact that each category contains photos with different lighting, positions, and
people.

B. Preparing Information for Machine Learning

1. Data Loading
To make it easier to handle and process, the dataset is arranged into folders labelled by
emotion categories (such as angry, joyful, and sad). Python modules like os and Pillow,
which enable effective processing of image files, are used to load the data. The.convert('L')
method is used to convert all photos to grayscale in order to maximise processing and
concentrate on key facial traits. This method removes extraneous color information while
maintaining key texture characteristics crucial for emotion perception. Each image is
reduced to 48 by 48 pixels to guarantee consistency and standardise the deep learning
model's input dimensions. By improving consistency and streamlining the preprocessing
pipeline, this step improves the dataset's suitability for training. Additionally, automatic
labelling is carried out by extracting class names from the folder names, guaranteeing

3. Data Splitting for Training and Assessing Machine Learning
The training data and the testing data are the two primary components of the processed
dataset at this point. The split is carried out using train_test_split, which enables users to
split the dataset into training and testing sets at random. Because it guarantees that
training and assessment are conducted on different sets of data, avoiding overfitting and
producing accurate performance measurements, this function is highly helpful in machine
learning applications. This function contains numerous optional options, such as test_size
to determine the fraction of the test data. For example, test_size=0.3 signifies that 30% of
the data is used for testing and the remaining data is utilised for training. Random_state,
another crucial argument, ensures reproducibility by producing the same data split each
time the function is executed with a particular seed value. Debugging, comparing models,
and repeating experiments all depend on this consistency [20].
•
Data Split: The dataset is divided into 20% testing data and 80% training data.
The train_test_split function from the scikit-learn library is used to randomly split
this data, making sure that the training and testing sets do not overlap.
•
Stratification: Because the dataset is stratified, both the training and testing data
maintain the same percentage of each emotion type. This avoids class imbalance,
which might lower the performance and accuracy of the model.
•
Model Data Preparation: A variety of models, including machine learning
techniques (SVM, KNN, RF), are compatible with the training and testing data. The

E. Deep Learning-Based Face Emotion Recognition

a. Data Loading
The dataset is loaded using ImageFolder, which assigns labels based on folder names, then
arranged into folders according to face expression class. The model is better able to
distinguish between distinct emotions because to its structure.  A Data Loader is used to
load data with a batch size of 64, and shuffling is turned on to enhance generalisation.
Memory pinning improves CPU-to-GPU data flow, while four parallel workers speed up
loading. Together, these improvements increase training effectiveness and model
performance on unknown data.

b. Transformation of Data
Before being fed into the Neural Network, the input images undergo a variety of
preprocessing steps to increase feature extraction and assure consistency across the
entire dataset. Converting photos into a single-channel format in order to exclude colour
information and enable the model to concentrate on facial texture and structure is the
first stage. This keeps important information required for emotion identification while
streamlining calculation.

c. Data Augmentation
The dataset goes through several data augmentation stages to improve model resilience
and generalisation. Images are converted to grayscale and scaled to 48×48 pixels to
guarantee consistent input proportions. To replicate natural changes in facial positions,
geometric augmentations include random affine transformations with rotations of ±10
degrees, translations up to 10%, and scaling between 0.9 and 1.1. While brightness and
contrast modifications within a range of 0.8 to 1.2 assist the model adapt to various
lighting situations and lessen sensitivity to changes in illumination, random horizontal
flipping with a 50% chance provides variation in orientation and symmetry.

4. Model Assessment
Following training, the model's performance and generalisation are assessed using the
validation dataset. For consistent predictions, the model is in evaluation mode, which
disables dropout and stabilises batch normalisation. To save memory and expedite
inference, gradient computations are disabled; mixed precision inference further
improves performance.
 By comparing predicted labels—obtained using argmax on output probabilities—with
ground truth labels, validation accuracy is calculated. While per-class accuracy shows
performance across many emotion categories and aids in identifying class imbalances or
frequent misclassifications, overall accuracy shows the percentage of correctly
categorised samples.
 A high validation accuracy suggests robust generalization, but a big difference between
training and validation results may suggest overfitting, demanding changes like
hyperparameter tuning, data augmentation, or regularisation.

4. Results and Discussion
Using PyTorch and scikit-learn packages, experiments were carried out on a system with
an Intel i7-12700F CPU, 32 GB RAM, and an RTX 3070 GPU. K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN),
Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest (RF), and Neural Network (NN), a deep
learning model, are compared in this study for facial emotion recognition. The objective
is to evaluate accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score in order to determine the best
model for early identification of psychiatric illnesses such as stress, anxiety, and
depression.
NNs are excellent candidates for this task since they automatically learn complicated
patterns, in contrast to classical models that rely on manual feature extraction. Early
identification is crucial because mental health illnesses are on the rise. Real-time
monitoring and intervention can be enhanced by incorporating emotion detection into
healthcare via wearables, telemedicine, and digital apps. In order to support
psychological evaluation and treatment, this study provides insights into AI-driven
mental health technologies.

4.7 Discussion
•
With significant ramifications for mental health applications, the study's findings
highlight crucial distinctions between deep learning techniques and traditional
machine learning models for facial emotion recognition. The Multilayer
Perceptron (MLP), which achieved 95% accuracy and consistently strong F1scores across all six emotion classes, outperformed the other models that were
assessed. This suggests that complex, non-linear patterns in facial expressions can
be automatically extracted by deep learning algorithms, making it possible to
accurately identify subtle emotional indicators like happiness and sadness, which
frequently overlap or are less noticeable.
•
K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN), on the other hand, performed somewhat (accuracy
62%), doing well on emotions with clear characteristics like surprise and terror
but having trouble identifying more complex emotions. This illustrates a
drawback of classical models: they are less able to capture small emotional
fluctuations due to their reliance on manual feature selection. In a similar vein,
Random Forest (RF) and Support Vector Machine (SVM) demonstrated diversity
in precision and recall across emotions, especially for grief and rage, but reached
respectable accuracy (84%). With an accuracy of 81%, the traditional Neural
Network (NN) outperformed KNN but fell short of MLP, underscoring the
significance of network depth and adequate training data for accurate pattern
recognition.
•
Analysis of various age-based datasets highlights the significant impact of dataset
features on model performance. While KNN remained competitive on smaller
datasets because of its simplicity, MLP-based deep neural networks generalised
well on larger or more homogeneous datasets. This implies that while classical
models might still be appropriate for applications with limited resources, deep
learning techniques are best suited for scalable, real-time emotion identification
systems.
•
Overall, the work supports applications like real-time monitoring, telemedicine,
and individualised mental health interventions by demonstrating that AI-driven

5. Conclusion
The effectiveness of five machine learning models—K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN),
Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest (RF), Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), and a
traditional Neural Network (NN)—for face emotion identification across six fundamental
emotions was compared in this study. With an overall accuracy of 95% and consistently
high F1-scores across all emotion classes, the results show that deep learning models,
especially the MLP, greatly outperform classical techniques. While the conventional NN
had limits in capturing subtle emotional patterns due to insufficient training data or
network complexity, traditional models like KNN, RF, and SVM produced modest
accuracies (62–84%) and struggled with subtle emotions like melancholy and happiness.
A comparison of several age-based datasets showed that while KNN is still competitive for
smaller datasets because of its simplicity, deep neural networks generalise well for bigger
or more homogeneous datasets. The results demonstrate the promise of AI-powered
emotion identification systems for early psychiatric condition detection, facilitating realtime mental health monitoring via wearable technology, telemedicine, and digital health
apps. In order to further enhance resilience and practical applicability, future research
should concentrate on diversifying datasets, adding multimodal inputs, and refining
neural network topologies.
