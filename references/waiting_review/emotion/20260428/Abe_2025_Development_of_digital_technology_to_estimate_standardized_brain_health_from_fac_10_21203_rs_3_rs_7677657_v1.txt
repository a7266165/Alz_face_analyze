Abstract: In recent years, the effects of reading and art on the human brain have been
17

attracting attention. However, it has not been clarified how they affect the brain structure
18

of healthy middle-aged people. This is due to the lack of technology to easily measure the
19

state of brain structure. Therefore, in Study I, by analyzing the correspondence between
20

gray matter volume (GMV) calculated from MRI images of the brain and facial expression
21

data when four types of facial expressions are made (happiness, anger, sadness, and sur-
22

prise), we constructed an algorithm to estimate brain health from facial expressions and
23

developed a digital application to estimate brain health. In study II, we conducted corre-
24

lation analysis between the estimated GMV and creative activities and reading habits
25

measured by questionnaire using 113 middle-aged men and women who considered
26

themselves healthy. It was revealed that GMV of the default mode network (DMN) and
27

central executive network (CEN) correlate with creative activity and reading habit, respec-
28

tively. This is the first study to show that art and reading can affect the brain structure of
29

more people than previously believed using the estimated GMV.
30

Keywords: art; estimated gray matter volume; facial expressions, reading; triple network.

1. Study I: Development of digital technology to estimate standardized
33

brain health from facial expressions
34

1.1. Introduction
35

In recent years, in developed countries, the reduction of medical and nursing care
36

costs due to the decline in the working-age population and the aging of society has be-
37

come a social issue, and extending healthy life expectancy is considered to be an important
38

factor. In addition, lifestyle habits, aging, and mental and neurological diseases account
39

for a large portion of medical expenses, and there is a demand for the establishment of
40

health care services that improve lifestyle habits. In the healthcare field, inferring a pa-
41

tient's health through face-to-face meetings is common, but it is not necessarily scientific.
42



2 of 25


A study by Cannavacciuolo et al. [1] compared facial emotional expressivity in pa-
43

tients with Parkinson's disease, Alzheimer's disease, and healthy controls, and found that
44

patients with Parkin-son's disease and Alzheimer's disease shared a common tendency to
45

have lower facial emotional expressivity compared to controls [1]. These symptoms are
46

associated with atrophy of cortical and subcortical areas in the limbic system [2]. Inability
47

to regulate emotional facial expressions can have a negative impact on an individual's
48

socio-emotional environment and has been linked to depression, schizophrenia [3], flat-
49

tened affect [4], and reduced levels of trait empathy in healthy adults [5]. Alterations in
50

socioemotional attitudes, including deficits in spontaneous emotion expression, empathy,
51

emotion reading, and expression of blunt emotion, are also found in patients with behav-
52

ioral variant frontotemporal dementia (bvFTD), right temporal frontotemporal dementia
53

(rtvFTD), semantic variant primary progressive aphasia (svPPA), and Alzheimer's disease
54

(AD) [6,7,8,9,10,11]. Impaired intentional emotion expression was predicted by general
55

expressiveness and emotion reading deficits across neurodegenerative patients, and imi-
56

tation abilities were significantly impaired in bvFTD and rtvFTD patients compared to
57

controls [12].
58

Blunted facial affect, or reduced facial ex-pression of emotion, is a transdiagnostic
59

com-ponent of serious mental illness [13,14,15,16,17]. Blunted facial affect is a key compo-
60

nent of blunted affect, which involves reduced facial expression through many channels,
61

including facial, vocal, and gestural expressions. Blunted affect is a symptom of schizo-
62

phrenia and is also commonly seen in mood disorders (e.g., psycho-motor retardation in
63

depression), autism spectrum, and neurodegenerative disorders [17,18]. Blunted facial af-
64

fect is associated with poorer social and occupational functioning [19,20,21] and increased
65

risk of suicide [22]. Therefore, in recent years, research has been conducted using AI tech-
66

nology to identify facial expression features characteristic of people with severe mental
67

illness [23], identify depression using facial expressions and images [24,25], and predict
68

neural networks from facial expressions [26].
69

On the other hand, there is a certain accumulation of research on predicting brain age
70

from GM images [27]. The difference between brain age and chronological age is quanti-
71

tatively associated with several age-related risk factors and general physical health, such
72

as reduced grip strength, reduced lung function, history of stroke, increased frequency of
73

alcohol consumption, and increased risk of death [28], as well as declines in cognitive
74

functions such as fluid intelligence, processing speed, semantic language fluency, visual
75

attention, and cognitive flexibility [29,30,31]. Brain age prediction models tend to perform
76

better using GMV than white matter volume [32]. Therefore, it is possible to predict the
77

relationship between facial expressions, which have been shown to be related to dementia
78

and mental illness, and brain age, as expressed by GMV. However, no algorithms have
79

been developed to predict GMV from facial expressions. Considering the cost and time
80

required to measure brain state using MRI, it is difficult to measure frequently, and the
81

development of technology to easily measure GMV from facial expressions could greatly
82

contribute to people's health and welfare.
83

 To date, the authors have been developing evaluation technologies to visualize and
84

quantify the state of the human mind and body, including human senses, emotions, and
85

physical strain, with the mission of contributing to society by creating better products and
86

services through the use of analytical technology to add new value to our customers' prod-
87

ucts and services. They also possess original facial expression analysis technology [33].   88

On the other hand, focusing on the "brain" that controls the human mind and body, Yama-
89

kawa et al. [34] have proposed the Brain Healthcare Quotient (BHQ) as an index to meas-
90

ure brain health [35]. The BHQ was internationally standardized in 2018 [36] and is easy
91

to use even for non-experts, so it is expected to be used in a variety of solutions, such as
92

allowing users to understand their own brain health and take action to improve their
93



3 of 25


lifestyle habits. However, the BHQ is an index calculated from Magnetic Resonance Im-
94

aging (MRI) images of the brain, and requires MRI imaging at a hospital or other facility,
95

so users cannot easily measure it.
96

 To solve this problem, we used our facial expression analysis technology to ana-
97

lyze the correspondence be-tween BHQ and facial expression data, and discovered the
98

relationship between the two. Using the results, we built an algorithm to estimate BHQ
99

from facial expression data, and developed a digital application that allows users to easily
100

and quickly learn about their standardized brain health.

102

Data was acquired to analyze the relationship between the BHQ calculated from

A total of 58 healthy male and female subjects (47 males and 11 females) aged 25 to 63
106

Data acquisition was performed after obtaining informed consent from all subjects.
108

109

116

117

118

119

(Fractional Anisotropy-BHQ), which in-dexes the quality of white matter where many
120

brain nerve fibers are present [37]. GM-BHQ represents the average of 116 regions in the
121

AAL atlas [38]. We also calculated the average values for regions associated with the
122

Default Mode Network (DMN), Central Executive Network (CEN), and Salience
123

Network (SN). The AAL atlas regions used for each network are shown in Table 1.

1.2.3. MRI Data Acquisition
128

A 3 Tesla MRI scanner (MAGNETOM Prisma, Siemens, Munich, Germany) with a
129

32-channel head array coil, three-dimensional (3D) T1-weighted magnetization-prepared
130

rapid acquisition gradient echo pulse sequence, and spin-echo echo-planar imaging (SE-
131

EPI) with generalized autocalibrated partially parallel acquisition (GRAPPA) were used
132

for MRI data collection and structural imaging. The following parameters were used: rep-
133

etition time (TR) 1900 ms, echo time (TE) 2.52 ms, inversion time (TI) 900 ms, flip angle 9°,
134

matrix size 256 × 256, field of view (FOV) 256 mm, and slice thickness 1 mm.
135

1.2.4. MRI Data Analysis
136

T1-weighted images were segmented into gray matter (GM), white matter (WM), and
137

cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) using Statistical Parametric Mapping 12 (SPM12; Wellcome
138

Trust Center for Neuroimaging, London, UK) running on MATLAB R2022b (MathWorks
139

Inc., Sherborne, MA, USA). Segmented GM images were then spatially normal-ized using
140

diffeomorphic anatomical registration using the exponential lie algebra (DARTEL) algo-
141

rithm [39], which included incorporating a modulation step in-to the preprocessing model
142

and smoothing images using a Gaussian kernel with a full width at half maximum
143

(FWHM) of 8 mm. The smoothed GM images were then converted to proportional GM
144

images by dividing by the intracranial volume (ICV) and used to create mean and stand-
145

ard deviation (SD) images. By averaging these in-formation and the local GM quotients
146

using the automatic anatomical labeling (AAL) atlas [40], the GM-BHQ was created with
147

a mean of 100 and a SD of 15 points.
148

Previous research has confirmed that BHQ tends to decline with age and can be an
149

indicator of the youthfulness of the brain6). Research into GM-BHQ's relationship with
150

lifestyle has also been conducted; for example, resting and doing housework on weekdays
151

has a positive effect on GM-BHQ, while taking too much time off on weekends tends to
152

be bad for GM-BHQ. It has been shown that lifestyle is related to brain health, and it is
153

hoped that reviewing lifestyle habits will contribute to maintaining and improving brain
154

health6). In addition, in previous studies, the whole-brain GM-BHQ has been related to
155

dietary balance [41], lifestyle [42], social performance [43], curiosity and grit [44], and di-
156

versity understanding [45]. These studies were conducted in healthy middle-aged adults,
157

not elderly people or people with underlying diseases, so it is expected that using the
158

same index will add depth to the interpretation of the study. Our study, which focuses on
159

GMV, a function of surface area and cortical thickness, is more comprehensive than stud-
160

ies using other neural biomarkers such as functional MRI (fMRI), which is excel-lent for
161

examining brain responses [46], and cortical thickness, which is suitable for observing ge-
162

netic influences [47].

202

In analyzing the relationship between whole-brain GM-BHQ and facial expres-
207

208

GM-BHQ from the age average (approximation line) (hereinafter referred to as ΔGM-
209

210

We also checked the relationship between age and GM-BHQ in the DMN, CEN,

1.4.2. Building a BHQ estimation model
222

Using each Δ component as the objective variable and the reaction time in the X
223

and Y directions of each facial landmark as the variables, the analysis was carried out in
224

the following steps to extract facial expression features that can estimate each Δ compo-
225

nent.
226

Step 1: Extract variables correlated with the objective variable.
227

Step 2: In order to exclude variables with multicollinearity, variables with high cor-
228

relation coefficients were grouped together, and only the variable most correlated with
229

the objective variable for each group was extracted.
230

Step 3: Construct a linear multiple regression model using the forward variance
231

method.
232

In selecting the model, cross-validation was performed on linear regression models,
233

linear mixed models, and random forest models using the Leave One Group Out
234



7 of 25


method, and it was decided to adopt the linear regression model with the highest accu-
235

racy.

1.5. Discussion
327

By analyzing the correspondence between GM-BHQ, which indicates the state of
328

brain health, and facial ex-pression data when four types of facial expressions (happiness,
329

anger, sadness, and surprise) were made, we were able to extract facial expression features
330

that are important for estimating GM-BHQ. In addition, we constructed an estimation
331

model using these features to estimate the GM-BHQ of the entire brain, and the correlation
332

coefficient between the GM-BHQ from brain MRI images and the GM-BHQ estimated
333

from facial expressions was 0.75, and the MAE was 3.12. Similarly, when we estimated
334

the GM-BHQ of each brain region, the DMN, CEN, and SN, the correlation coefficients
335

between the DMN, CEN, and SN from brain MRI images and the DMN, CEN, and SN
336

estimated from facial expressions were 0.58, 0.63, and 0.74, respectively, and the MAE was
337

4.14, 3.87, and 4.05, respectively, although the estimation accuracy was lower than that of
338

the GM-BHQ of the whole brain.
339

 Furthermore, we developed a PC application called "Estimated BHQ" that esti-
340

mates brain health from facial expressions, and have conducted various demonstration
341

experiments to confirm user needs and acceptability for measuring brain health [48,49,50].
342

Through these demonstration experiments, we have demonstrated the possibility that by
343

providing unprecedented new value of being able to measure brain health in a fun and
344

easy way, we can arouse users' interest in brain health, and by visualizing brain health,
345

we can encourage them to try measuring it again. These results suggest that measuring
346

brain health can be a catalyst for encouraging behavioral changes in users.
347

Currently, it is possible to visualize brain health, but it is not yet possible to present
348

specific actions on how users can maintain and improve their brain health based on the
349

measurement results. In the future, we plan to explore measures to improve brain health
350

while also pro-posing improvement actions tailored to each user. This research is the first
351

to develop technology to predict whole-brain and triple network GMV from facial expres-
352

sions. We hope to continue developing brain health estimation technology and proposing
353

measures to improve brain health, thereby contributing to maintaining and improving
354

people's brain health and realizing well-being in their lives.
355

1.6. Limitations
356

The current research has some limitations. The algorithm developed in this study is
357

intended to obtain an estimate of GMV, not an exact value. Therefore, it should be used
358

with caution in research that requires greater accuracy, such as in the medical field. In
359

addition, this study was conducted on Japanese people, so the results cannot be applied
360

directly to other countries. At the same time, as symbolized by the bias in the gender ratio
361

of the sample, the results of this study do not reflect the average image of Japanese people.
362

In the future, the results of this study should be verified using a sample with a larger
363

number of diverse backgrounds.

2. Study II: An application of estimated GMV to neuroaesthetics re-
366

search
367

2.1. Introduction
368

In recent neuroscience, the effects of artistic activities and reading have been attract-
369

ing attention as a way to reduce the risk of dementia in the elderly. Sensing beauty acti-
370

vates the entire brain, including the brain's reward system, such as the frontal orbital cor-
371

tex and striatum [51,52]. Therefore, it has been reported that there is a relationship be-
372

tween high creativity and several brain regions. In particular, it has been revealed that
373

DMN cooperates with CEN to improve creativity and artistic performance [53,54]. Crea-
374

tive activities may have a positive effect on the brain not only by performing them yourself,
375

but also by watching and feeling the state of the creative activity. It has been reported that
376

the DMN is engaged when people are deeply moved by a work of art or when they feel it
377

is beautiful [55,56]. On the other hand, when learning something from literature, we con-
378

nect various events and imagine the mental scenes of people at that time. Such cognitive
379

acts are related to the activity and connectivity of the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex
380

[57,58]. Previous evidence suggests that there is a relationship between the retrieval of
381

autobiographical memories and CEN [59,60]. However, the effects of such artistic activi-
382

ties and reading on the brain structure of healthy middle-aged adults remain unclear. The
383

possibility that engaging in intellectual activities at a young age may prevent or delay the
384

onset of dementia has been asserted and supported by research on "cognitive reserve"
385

[61,62,63].
386

Therefore, in this study, we focus on the GMV of the Healthy adult brain. GMV was
387

determined as the GM-BHQ value originally based on MRI image analysis. This value can
388

be estimated by measuring facial expressions as shown in Study I. The representative
389

brain networks are three networks that control rational judgment, emotion control, other
390

recognition, self-recognition, and behavior evaluation. Of these, CEN, composed of the
391

dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and posterior parietal cortex, is critical for the active reten-
392

tion and manipulation of information in working memory, attention, problem solving,
393

decision-making, and self-awareness [64,65]. The SN is also a network that includes the
394

ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (VLPFC) and anterior insula (collectively referred to as the
395

fronto-insular cortex FIC), as well as the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) [66], and re-
396

sponds to subjective salience, whether cognitive, homeostatic, or emotional [67]. The SN
397

also acts as a switch between the CEN and the DMN, inhibiting the latter and activating
398

the former when salient stimuli or cognitive tasks are at hand, a process essential for at-
399

tention and flexible cognitive control [68,69,70,71,72,73]. On the other hand, the DMN in-
400

cludes the medial posterior cortex, including the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) and
401

parts of the frontal lobe, the medial prefrontal cortex (MPFC), and the posterior temporal
402

region around the temporoparietal junction (TPJ), including the inferior parietal lobe (IPL)
403



13 of 25


[74,75,76]. The DMN is preferentially activated when individuals are not focused on the
404

external environment [75] and are engaged in various areas of cognitive and social pro-
405

cessing. That is, the medial prefrontal cortex (MPFC) plays a key role in the social under-
406

standing of others, and the connections between the medial prefrontal cortex (aMPFC)
407

and the posterior cingulate cortex and anterior cingulate cortex mainly contribute to self-
408

other discrimination. On the other hand, the connection between the dorsal MPFC
409

(dMPFC) and the temporoparietal junction (TPJ) is mainly related to the understanding
410

of others' mental states [77].
411

The interactions between these networks are related to daily self-regulation and em-
412

pathy for others [78,79,80,81,82,83]. However, to our knowledge, there have been no stud-
413

ies that have addressed the relationship between the brain structure centered on these
414

triple networks and artistic activities and reading habits. Therefore, in this study, we con-
415

tribute to the development of related research by clarifying the characteristics of the brain
416

structure centered on the triple network in healthy adults using information obtained
417

from questionnaire surveys and estimation of GMV using facial expressions.

2.2.2. Estimating brain information using facial expression information
440

In this study, we used estimated GM-BHQ measured from facial expressions. To
441

measure facial expressions, we used an application developed in Study I. The reason for
442

using the estimated GM-BHQ is that GM-BHQ has been used primarily in studies of
443

healthy middle-aged adults, as in this study, rather than in elderly people or people with
444

underlying diseases, and correlations with various psychological indices have been con-
445

firmed. In our previous studies, whole-brain GM-BHQ was positively correlated with cog-
446

nitive ability [84], job satisfaction [85], dietary balance [41] and negatively with unhealthy
447

lifestyle [42], fatigue and stress [86], and polluted residential environment [87]. Further-
448

more, in a recent pilot study, the DMN, CEN and SN of GM-BHQ were positively corre-
449

lated with understanding of gender and origin diversity [45]. Also, consistent with neu-
450

roplasticity claims [88], GM-BHQ has been shown to change with certain interventions
451

[89,90].

2.3. Psychological Test
453

2.3.1. Creative Behavior
454

This study used the Aesthetic Responsiveness Assessment developed by Schlotz et
455

al. [91] and adapted by Magsamen and Ross [92]. The AReA originally is a short (14-item)
456

questionnaire for assessing how responsive a person is to aesthetic experiences, such as
457

with visual arts, music, dance and performance. Among the three subscales of AReA,
458

namely Aesthetic Appreciation (8 items), Intense Aesthetic Experience (3 items), and Cre-
459

ative Behavior (3 items including “I sculpt, paint, draw, direct films, or do design work”),
460

we used a revised creative behavior scale consisting of four items: original Creative Be-
461

havior three items plus one item from Aesthetic Appreciation, “I visit museums or go to
462

musical/dance performances.” This revision was made because the added item is an item
463

that represents behavior, like the three items in Creative Behavior, and a factor analysis
464

using test data from 80 people collected through general recruitment showed that the
465



15 of 25


factor was more stable when it was included in Creative Behavior rather than Aesthetic
466

Appreciation, and also because an improvement was observed in the Cronbach's alpha
467

reliability coefficient of the factor (available upon request). Each item was answered on a
468

5-point Likert scale, and each response was assigned a score from 1 to 5. The creative be-
469

havior was calculated as the average of 4 items. Cronbach's alpha reliability coefficients
470

of creative behavior were 0.662.
471

2.3.2. Reading habits
472

The study used two items from the adult version of the OECD Programme for Inter-
473

national Student Assessment (PISA): “How often do you read books in your current job?”
474

and “How often do you read novels or non-fiction books outside of work?” [93]. Each item
475

was answered on a 5-point Likert scale, and each response was assigned a score from 1 to
476

5. The creative behavior was calculated as the average of 2 items.
477

2.3.3. Control variables
478

Sex (male = 1; female = 0), age (years old), educational background (years) and BMI
479

(kg/m2) were used as control variables.

2.6. Discussion
511

The results of this study suggest that the brain GMV of healthy adults is related to
512

intellectual activities such as creative behavior and reading habits. The DMN and CEN
513

seem to play a major role in these activities. This result is consistent with previous studies
514

that have shown that the DMN is associated with artistic activities [53,54,55,56] and that
515

the CEN is associated with reading [57,58,59,60]. The participants in this study were male
516

and female workers who considered themselves healthy. Therefore, the results of this
517

study indicate that engaging in intellectual activities such as art and reading while young
518

and healthy may not only maintain their brain health and have a positive effect on their
519

daily work, but may also contribute to extending their future healthy lifespan. This idea
520

has been discovered or supported by previous studies as "cognitive reserve" [61,62], but
521

the results of this study add biological knowledge to this discussion.
522

In a previous study in which 16 young adults were tasked with rating images of art-
523

works, areas of the medial prefrontal cortex, measured in an fMRI scanner, were posi-
524

tively activated in the best-rated trials. This means that the rating-specific neural response
525

is attributable not to the characteristics of the particular artwork, but to the aesthetic ex-
526

perience. In other words, DMN activity is due to the fact that a particular artwork fits the
527

unique structure of the individual so well that it accesses neural substrates that are in-
528

volved in the self, leading to a feeling of being "moved." This explanation is consistent
529

with the contemporary idea that an individual's taste in art is linked to a sense of identity
530

[94,95]. The involvement of the DMN in painting appreciation is further supported by the
531

results of a recent meta-analysis [96].
532

On the other hand, an experimental study of the reading comprehension of 60 school-
533

age children showed that executive functions make a significant contribution to reading
534

comprehension [97]. Subsequent meta-analysis also supported a moderate positive asso-
535

ciation (r = 0.36) between executive function and reading comprehension [98]. Consistent
536

with these, a recent neuroscience study of 55 school-age children showed that the discrep-
537

ancy between the ability to convert printed words into sounds and reading comprehen-
538

sion of text was associated with a frontoparietal network including the GMV of the left
539

dorsolateral prefrontal cortex [99].
540

The results of this study showing that the DMN is involved in artistic activities and
541

the CEN in reading are highly suggestive. This is because, in light of the accumulation of
542

research showing that whole-brain GMV is involved in cognitive functions [100], it sug-
543

gests the importance of having a variety of hobbies. Normally, human memories are
544

stored in the hippocampus as short-term memories containing a lot of concrete infor-
545

mation. However, because the hippocampus has a capacity, it cannot store the details of
546

concrete information for long. Much of the information gradually loses concreteness and
547

is stored in the brain as abstract information over a long period of time. People will be-
548

come interested in information they saw on TV yesterday, such as health information, and
549

will search for books driven by the desire to know more about it in more detail. At this
550

time, people will search for books, read, and think while referring to the concrete infor-
551

mation in their brains that is still fresh, so they will rely on the executive function of the
552

CEN. However, for information that has been fragmented in the brain and does not rise
553

to the conscious mind, it may be difficult to search for it of their own volition and find
554

relevance in the book they picked up. At that time, people will feel a vague sense of dis-
555

satisfaction. However, that person may suddenly start a little artistic activity one day, per-
556

haps inspired by a view they saw casually. In the process of looking at art in a museum
557

or fumbling around to draw a picture by themselves, one day they suddenly come across
558

a piece that moves them in a way that they cannot put into words. At this time, the DMN
559

plays a central role. The DMN responds to aesthetic experiences that they are not aware
560

of and have forgotten. Artistic activities are a little more difficult to get into than books
561



18 of 25


that can be easily picked up at a bookstore, and many people do not have the opportunity
562

to come into contact with the results of neuroaesthetics research, so at present, it is thought
563

that those who are lucky enough to come across them are involved in art and maintaining
564

their brain health. However, if more people could learn about their brain condition using
565

a machine that can be easily measured, the effect of casually engaging in art on the brain
566

would become clearer, and it may be possible to get more people involved in art and im-
567

prove their brain health. The results of this study have the potential to contribute to im-
568

proving the well-being of such people in the future. In particular, reliable information
569

based on abundant evidence is needed to encourage greater participation in artistic activ-
570

ities among elderly people, who are considered to be conservative and unwilling to
571

change their ways of thinking, and to prevent dementia.
572

Thus, the results of this study are consistent with this series of previous studies, de-
573

spite the use of a simplified method of facial expression recognition. Previous neurosci-
574

ence research has required expensive and heavy equipment such as MRI. This has made
575

it difficult for ordinary workers to easily measure their brains. On the other hand, by plac-
576

ing the simple measuring device used in this study in workplaces and commercial facili-
577

ties and measuring the state of the brain easily and on a daily basis, just as if measuring
578

weight or blood pressure, people may be motivated to pay more attention to their own
579

and others' brain states and maintain brain health. It is also clear from neurofeedback re-
580

search that knowing the state of the brain leads to maintaining and improving brain health
581

[101,102]. Therefore, the results of this study will make a meaningful contribution to the
582

social implementation of brain science.
583

2.7. Limitations
584

This study has some limitations. Firstly, it should be noted that the results of this
585

study were estimated from facial expressions, not GMV calculated from MRI images, so
586

the reproducibility of the results of this study needs to be tested using GMV. Secondly,
587

this study was conducted on Japanese people, and caution is required if the results are
588

applicable to other countries. Japanese people have a unique sense of beauty, such as
589

wabi-sabi and kawaii, and such peculiarities may have biased the results. They are also
590

known to be conservative and, compared to other developed countries, enjoy reading but are not
591

very active in artistic activities. Thirdly, because general measures of art and reading were
592

used, the results of this study cannot suggest specific activities. Fourthly, this study is a
593

cross-sectional analysis and does not show a causal relationship. For example, rather than
594

the arts and reading enhancing brain health, it may be that brain health drives people's
595

choices about the arts and reading. Fifthly, the small sample size limits the generalizability
596

of the results. Sixthly, in this study, sex, age, educational background and BMI were used
597

as control variables. However, background information not adopted in this study, such as
598

socioeconomic status such as income and differences in the community to which the par-
599

ticipants belong, may have influenced the results. Beauty is not a distinct, independent
600

element embedded in a color, food, nature, landscape, idea, or even a face, nor is it some-
601

thing an artist "puts" into a work as a distinct entity; it is a property that emerges in the
602

beholder's brain. That aesthetic response is shaped by the beholder's cultural experiences,
603

life events, education, genetic inheritance, and perhaps gender, age, and health. Individ-
604

ual variation in the intensity and extent of the response has not been systematically ex-
605

plored, and there remains much to be explored [103]. Seventhly, this study was unable to
606

clarify the relationship between SN and art and reading. This is an issue that remains for
607

future efforts to improve brain health at the whole-brain level. Eighth, even if art and reading
608

are good for the brain, this study does not allow us to infer the extent to which their effects are
609

compared to other activities. With the advancement of neuroscience, there are many activities that
610

are said to be good for the brain (exercise, diet, brain training, etc.). Since the general public has
611



19 of 25


limited free time and money, the relative importance of neuroaesthetic-related activities, how often
612

and for how long they are effective, and above what level the incremental effect disappears, need
613

to be gradually clarified in the future. Ninth, the present study was limited to a triple network: a
614

recent meta-analysis showed that neural responses to artistic beauty recruit a broadly distributed
615

network in both hemispheres, including content-dependent brain regions in the ventral visual
616

stream [104]. Previous findings have also shown that the originality of a art is associated with the
617

DMN, while beauty is associated with a network of areas including the occipital and frontal lobes
618

[105]. As such, the latest findings in brain research indicate the possibility of a more diverse and
619

complex network of neural responses to beauty, leaving room for further development of the sim-
620

plified measuring device used in this study. Finally, most previous studies have used fMRI,
621

which differs from the data of this study, which is based on MRI. Although fMRI is excel-
622

lent for measuring brain responses, there is insufficient evidence as to whether the re-
623

sponses have a lasting effect on brain structure. As neuroaesthetics research using MRI
624

becomes more active and the relationship with brain structure becomes clearer, the value
625

of the simple measurement used in this study is expected to increase even further. Future
626

research should seek to verify the results of this study using other methods, such as MRI
627

and fMRI, in a longitudinal manner that includes a variety of intervention methods and
628

control variables, with a larger number of people in different cultures, in order to obtain
629

further new findings.
630

3. Conclusions
631

In Study I, by analyzing the correspondence between the BHQ calculated from MRI
632

images of the brain and the facial expression data when four types of facial expressions
633

(happiness, anger, sadness, and surprise) are made, an algorithm was constructed to esti-
634

mate brain health from facial expressions, and an application was developed to estimate
635

brain health. This allows users to easily find out the state of their brain health in a short
636

time by simply measuring their facial expressions.
637

In Study II, a survey was conducted on 113 healthy Japanese adults using GMV esti-
638

mated from facial expressions and questionnaires, and it was shown that there was a pos-
639

itive correlation between local DMN and CEN GMV and artistic activities and reading
640

habits, respectively. These results suggest that estimated GMV and art and reading habits
641

are effective in paying attention to, caring for, and intervening in the brains of healthy
642

people to maintain future brain health and reduce the risk of dementia.
