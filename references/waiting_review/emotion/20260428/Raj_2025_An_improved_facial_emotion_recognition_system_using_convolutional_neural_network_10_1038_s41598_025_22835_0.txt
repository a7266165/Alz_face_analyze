Artificial intelligence (AI) has been effectively augmenting the features of robotics applications,
including surveillance, medical support, aid services for the elderly or disabled, and many more
uses. Most robotics applications need a variety of human-robot interactions (HRI) for effective and
accurate use. Computer vision plays a vital role in improved and precise HRI. The human facial emotion
recognition (FER) technique is one of the important types of computer vision that is crucial for the
enhancement of HRI. This paper presents a study that uses algorithms based on computer vision and
machine learning (ML) to identify the emotional states of humans in photographs and videos while
users interact with various visual objects. Using ML techniques and a digital image processing pipeline,
this study aims to reveal the development of software that can recognize emotions in human gestures.
This research shows that software creation and training based on emotional expressions, together
with the use of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for emotional identification, are feasible. The
main objective of this study is to find the essential facial gestures that the CNN framework with FER
emphasizes. Finally, the proposed model is comparatively analyzed against FER2013, the Real-world
Affective Faces Database (RAF-DB), and CK+ datasets. The rate of accuracy is highest for the CK+
dataset, which is around 95% and lowest for the FER2013 dataset, which is around 64%. The findings
of this research will advance our knowledge about neural networks and help enhance the efficiency of
computer vision.

duties, including manufacturing activities, medical support, customer service to consumers, security and civil
surveillance, and more5,6. The ability to simulate empathy for various controllers is required7, that is, to give
robots the ability to identify, understand, and therefore produce adaptable actions using recognized information,
which is a major driving force behind studies in this domain.

Related work
Automatic FER is still an extremely challenging and fascinating task in computer vision. DNNs are being used
more and more to build discriminatory models for automatic FER as a result of the evolution of FER beyond
controlled research environments, too difficult in wild situations, and the present popularity of ML techniques
in a variety of domains. In recent decades, various researchers have focused on facial gesture recognition to
enhance HRI.
Mollahosseini et al.36 present a deep neural network (DNN) framework that can be used to solve the FER
issue in several popular standard facial data sets. In particular, this network is composed of four Inception layers,
two convolutional layers, and max pooling. The network, which consists of a single component design, classifies
input photos of faces into either the neutral or the six fundamental expressions. In terms of accuracy and
training time, the recommended design performs as well as or more effectively than state-of-the-art techniques
and conventional convolutional neural networks (CNN).
Jung et al.37 present a collaborative fine-tuning in DNNs for FER. The properties of temporal information
are helpful in identifying facial expressions. However, it takes a lot of work to manually develop features that are
beneficial. In this study, the deep learning approach is utilized, which is thought of as a method to automatically
obtain important characteristics from unprocessed data. In order to minimize this effort. Two distinct models
form the foundation of this deep network. One deep network uses imagery sequences to acquire temporal
aspect features, and another uses temporal face landmark points to derive temporal geometrical information.
Compared to conventional techniques such as feature synthesis and weighted summation, this new integrated
technique yields more precise findings.
Kotsia et al.38 present two new techniques for FER in face picture sequences. There are landmarks in the
picture series that the user must personally configure part of the Candide grid’s points to face in the initial frame.
Using flexible models, the grid-tracking and deformed technique employed follows the grid in a series of video
frames as the emotion changes continuously unless it reaches the frame with the strongest emotion. A unique
multiple-class Support Vector Machine (SVM) framework of classifiers that operates to identify both the six
fundamental facial gestures and an array of picked facial Action Units (FAUs) uses the geometrical movement
of some chosen Candide nodes, which can be expressed as the variation among the node coordinates among the
initial and highest emotion intensity frames. According to FAU recognition, the findings in the Cohn-Kanade
database provide a recognition precision of 95.1% for FER and 99.7% for facial emotion identification utilizing
the suggested multiclass SVMs.
Oh et al.39 create a deep learning (DL) model for emotion classification using facial expressions that are
resistant to distortion within the picture data. 53 participants were asked to express four distinct reactions for
15 seconds at a time; then, feelings were classified as positive or negative. After extracting facial emotions from
the captured photographs and storing them as image data, noise was added in five stages to analyze the data set.
Furthermore, when the noise was applied, its predictability was verified. This work verified that emotions might
be identified from facial expressions using a DL model that is resistant to noise in a given data set.
Liu et al.40 study the multimodal emotion integration process and companion robot empathic reactions.
Natural HRI requires humanoid robots to be able to understand sympathetic facial reactions and respond
accordingly. In order to create a multimodal emotion classification mechanism, this work integrates the GPT-3.5
system with a facial emotion-recognizing system. This work also tackles the problem of accurately replicating
human facial expressions through the mechanical structure of a humanoid robot. The linked GPT-based
multimodal emotion identification system employing text and images works better than existing approaches,
according to studies conducted on benchmark databases, and has unique empathic reaction capabilities.
Kim et al.41 suggest a new FER system design that depends on layered DL. In a system with hierarchy,
the geometrical feature and the feature taken from the looks of the feature-based network are merged. The
visual feature-based model uses the preprocessed picture to identify the holistic elements of the face, while the
geometric feature-based network uses action unit landmarks to learn the position shift of a muscular system
that moves primarily during facial emotions. With the help of this method, the extraction of variable facial
characteristics is possible without the need for sequential information among the neutral and emotional photos.
Filippini et al.42 suggest a highly efficient convolutional neural network (CNN)-based FER system that can
be used to improve the understanding of the NAO robot of human facial reactions while providing it with the
ability to determine the level of arousal of an interlocutor. In fact, the model evaluated in HRI was less successful
in identifying neutral and furious emotions and 75% accurate in identifying shocked and afraid expressions. It
was also 90% accurate in identifying happy and distressed facial expressions, respectively.

Martinez et al.43 provide a facial emotion recognition technique that relies on the most recent ML techniques
released for the state of the art and was built using the Robot Operating System (ROS), Noetic version. The
system can identify emotions including rage, happiness, sadness, or shock. Using an inexpensive multipurpose
board, the Raspberry Pi 4 Model B, comprehensive tests were carried out in a real-world setting to make these
approaches more effective and to demonstrate that they could be implemented in real time using an inexpensive
board. The whole FER system described in this paper can perhaps operate in real time with higher accuracy.
Alonso-Martín et al.44 provide a multimodal approach for social robots to recognize users’ emotions. The
purpose of this framework is to facilitate HRI. It is a component of the Robotics Dialog System (RDS), which
is the robot’s whole communication system. The Gender and Emotion Facial Analysis (GEFA) technique
was subsequently created to detect emotions in facial expressions. To establish the ultimate decision rule and
evaluate the accuracy of every aspect, many tests were conducted with actual users. The results of the trials
using this decision rule demonstrate a high accuracy rate in automatically identifying the emotions of the user,
outperforming the results provided by the two sources of information (image and audio) separately.
Yan et al.45 offer a multilayered CNN model utilizing a cross-attention mechanism (MCNN-CA) for precise
emotion recognition. The proposed paradigm facilitates the automatic extraction of pertinent features from
multimodal data and the integration of feature maps from various sources as modules for future emotion
recognition. During the feature extraction phase, several CNNs were developed to extract essential information
from multidimensional characteristics. The feature fusion module was employed to improve the intercorrelation
among features via a channel-efficient attention method. This innovation demonstrates efficacy in integrating
unique characteristics inside a singular mode and across other modes. The model was evaluated by EEG emotion
recognition studies utilizing the SEED and SEED-IV datasets. Multimodal emotion studies, using EEG and
textual data from the ZuCo dataset, assessed the efficacy of the proposed model. A comparative examination
with contemporary studies demonstrates that the proposed model surpasses others in accuracy, F1, precision,
and recall scores.
Aguang et al.46 examine the application of deep learning for FER utilizing the Emognition dataset, which
has ten target emotions: amusement, wonder, enthusiasm, liking, surprise, anger, contempt, fear, sorrow, and
neutrality. A sequence of data preparation steps was executed to transform video data into photos and enhance
the dataset. This paper presents CNN models developed via two methodologies: transfer learning (fine-tuning)
utilizing previously trained models Inception-V3 and MobileNet-V2, and constructing models from the ground
up employing the Taguchi method to identify an efficient combination of hyperparameter settings. The suggested
model exhibited commendable performance across various testing procedures, achieving an accuracy of 96%
and a mean F1-score of 0.95 on the simulated data.
The suggested CNN-based system presents a competitive and lightweight alternative, demonstrating robust
performance on controlled datasets. Although leading models in the literature frequently utilize ensemble
learning or transformer-based techniques to achieve accuracy exceeding 90% on datasets such as RAF-DB or
AffectNet, these approaches incur heightened computational complexity and inference duration, rendering them
less appropriate for real-time human–robot interaction contexts. Conversely, our model attains an accuracy of up
to 95% on CK+ and has commendable performance (about 64%) on FER2013 without extensive preprocessing
or ensemble methods. This equilibrium between precision and efficiency underscores the appropriateness of
our methodology for implementation in real-time robots and assistive technologies. Moreover, in contrast to
numerous opaque deep models, our research prioritizes interpretability by pinpointing the facial motions that
significantly impact the CNN’s decision-making, hence enhancing transparency in emotion inference. This
study offers multiple significant contributions to the domains of HRI and computer vision-based FER:
•	 The study highlights the importance of facial expression detection in improving human-robot interactions,
especially in areas such as surveillance, elderly support, and medical assistance. It examines how AI may en­
hance robotic systems’ capacity to comprehend human emotional states via facial expressions.
•	 A digital image processing pipeline was developed and executed using computer vision and machine learning
methodologies to identify human emotions based on facial expressions in photos and videos.
•	 The research effectively utilizes CNN architectures for the automatic extraction and classification of face emo­
tions, illustrating the efficacy of deep learning in emotion identification tasks.
•	 A novel aspect of the study is the examination of which facial motions provide the greatest influence on
CNN’s emotion classification mechanism. This offers details on the interpretation and decision-making func­
tion of the overall system.
•	 The suggested model undergoes a thorough evaluation utilizing three prominent emotion datasets: FER2013,
RAF-DB, and CK+. The model attains excellent accuracy on the CK+ data set (about 95%), indicating its
trustworthiness in controlled settings, while exhibiting comparatively lower accuracy on FER2013 (around
64%), underscoring difficulties in real-world scenarios.
•	 The work enhances the awareness of dataset biases and the generalization ability of CNN-based FER frame­
works by evaluating the proposed model’s performance over datasets with differing complexity and noise
levels.
•	 The results and the implementation strategy indicate practical relevance in real-time human-robot interac­
tion systems, facilitating the development of more emotionally intelligent robots and assistive technologies.

CNNs are an established class of deep learning algorithms utilized by computer vision, including automatic
identification of emotions48. CNNs are made to autonomously recognize and identify features from pictures that
might be used to predict the emotions of a person based on the picture49,50. CNNs might be trained using huge
databases of pictures with matching emotional labeling in the framework of emotion identification to discover
patterns and features linked to various emotions. Once trained, CNN might be utilized to predict new, unlabeled
images by feeding them via the network and choosing the label that has the best chance of prediction. Next,
a trained CNN might be used to predict the emotional impact of new images51. There are several benefits in
using CNNs for emotion identification over more conventional techniques for image processing. For instance,
CNNs might be trained to autonomously identify relevant characteristics from pictures instead of needing the
tedious and mistake-susceptible process of human feature extraction. Furthermore, advances in DL have made it
possible to handle different poses, brightness, and other environmental aspects that can alter the look of pictures,
especially with the incorporation of approaches, including CNN and other supportive approaches52.
CNNs are highly effective at solving complicated issues such as classification of images and analysis of
medical images because they do away with the requirement to manually extract features. Initially, we observe
a basic CNN template comprising many components that we may readily comprehend and associate with the
suggested CNN model. A fundamental CNN has three sorts of layer: input, hidden, and output. After entering
CNN along its input layers, the data pass through several hidden layers and eventually arrive at the output
layer53,54. The output layer represents the network forecast. The output of the network is contrasted with the
real labels to detect any error or loss. A foundational component of data processing is provided by the network’s
hidden layers. It is possible to break down each layer into its four sub-functions: convolutional layers, pooling
layers, normalization, and activation. Multiple layers of neurons make up CNNs, and the most significant layers
are the convolution layers and the pooling layers. Figure 3 shows the basic architecture of the CNN model and
its implications for FER. The input image shows a happy emotion instance associated with a human emotional
situation.
Convolutional layers
In a CNN, convolutional layers are primarily responsible for extracting features from the input images. Each
convolutional layer contains a set of filters (or kernels) which are trainable parameters learned during the training
process. Typically, the filters’ size is less than the picture’s real size. Every filter produces an activation map by
convolving using the picture. A mathematical process known as convolution integrates two functions to explain
their overlap. The filter is slid over the image’s width and height during convolution, and at all geographical
points, the product of dots among each filter component and the source data is computed. When two functions
are combined, one is “slid” over the other, multiplied at all points of overlap, and the results are added to produce
a novel function. Through this procedure, a novel function is created that shows the interactions between the
two primary functions. Traditionally, convolution represents an integral, as shown in Eq. (1)55, which could be
used to indicate the degree to which one function, p(t), overlaps another function, q(t), when it is moved over
the other.

Pooling layers
The pooling layer is a crucial element of CNNs. It is generally used in conjunction with the convolutional layer
to decrease the spatial extent of feature maps and minimize both the quantity of data and the variable value.
The pooling layer functions by dividing every feature map into predetermined areas and performing actions,
including identifying the largest value (max pooling) or computing the mean value (average pooling) inside
each area57. The two primary categories of pooling procedures are average pooling and max pooling. The max
pooling is the largest score found in each zone, while the average pooling is the average score computed for each
zone. Figure 5 shows the estimate of the average and max pooling. By exhibiting a certain level of translation
invariance and aiding in the preservation of important properties, these processes strengthen the resistance of
the network to even a small amount of changes in the input.
In CNNs, the pooling layer has two main functions. First, it shortens the learning process and minimizes
computing complexity by decreasing the physical dimensions of the feature maps. Secondly, it improves the
generality and interpretability of the framework by extracting important features and eliminating unnecessary
information58. A stochastic method known as pooling enables the aggregation of the outputs at a designated
location into a singular value. The pooling is an essential phase in convolutional methods to reduce the
dimensionality of the extracted features. The pooling approach reduces the computational costs of the top layers

Fully-connected layers
A fully connected layer denotes a neural network configuration in which each neuron performs a linear
transformation on the given input vector via a weight matrix. Consequently, all potential layer-to-layer linkages
exist, indicating that each element on the input vector affects each component of the output vector. In classification
tasks, fully connected layers convert high-level information into scores, which are usually processed using a
softmax function to obtain probabilistic class forecasts.
ReLU activation function
A well-liked activation function that is commonly utilized in deep learning models is the Rectified Linear Unit
(ReLU) activation function. The ReLU activation function can be used to give the framework as a whole nonlinearity without affecting the reception fields of the convolutional layer. The function is a simple but powerful
tool for modeling neural networks since it yields a result that is exactly proportionate to the input. Whenever
the input is positive, the ReLU function provides the value directly; if not, the result is zero. Because it solves
the problem of disappearing gradients and enables models to train more quickly and efficiently, it has been the
standard activation function in a variety of neural networks61. The ReLU function can be represented as Eq.

Simulation and experimental analysis
It has demonstrated significant efficacy in determining and obtaining patterns from visual input, which makes
it particularly suitable for applications, including recognizing objects and classifying images. CNNs differ from
multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) in that they possess hidden layers known as convolutional layers. The proposed
approach relies on a multilevel CNN structure. The proposed study uses a CNN for FER tasks, leveraging 48x48
grayscale images as input for both the FER2013 and CK+ datasets. Additionally, the RAF-DB dataset, which
measures 100 × 100, incorporates colored graphics. The model includes Conv2D layers that work quickly and
reduce the number of variables while still effectively extracting important features. The design has four groups

from 64 to 512, allowing it to distinguish complex face behaviors. After the convolutional layers, a Flatten layer
is added, then a fully connected dense layer with 512 neurons, and finally a softmax layer that has 7 outputs
for the 7 basic emotions. We train the model using categorical cross-entropy loss and an optimizer, such as
Adam, which enhances its performance on the FER2013, RAF-DB, and CK+ datasets. The initial suggested level
provides background removal, utilized to extract emotions using a picture63. This work used FER2013, RAF-DB,

This code presents an algorithm based on deep learning designed to recognize emotions on faces accurately
in images. The foundation of this facial emotion recognition system consists of a CNN architecture developed
using the TensorFlow and Keras packages. We aimed to assess the model’s ability to recognize diverse facial
gestures by training it on a comprehensive collection of images and subsequently evaluating its performance
with that same dataset. CNNs are distinguished by their enhanced depth, attained by the sequential stacking
of many convolutional layers. The suggested CNN approach for FER employs many deep learning techniques,
such as data augmentation and learning rate reduction. These techniques augment the model’s capacity for
generalization to novel data and mitigate overfitting. The proposed work has the potential to achieve optimal
results using the FER2013, RAF-DB, and CK+ datasets. The values of each pixel in the picture are normalized
to a range between 0 and 1, a common pre-processing procedure for the image data. The process facilitates a
reduction in the complexity of the model and accelerates training. Next, we apply data augmentation techniques
to the training data, thus enhancing its diversity and adaptability to various types of images. We particularly
rotate, modify the dimension and size, shear, enlarge, and horizontally flip the photographs. Throughout
training, various augmentations are arbitrarily implemented on each picture gathering, producing a novel array
of images dynamically for each epoch.
The system will comprise four primary components: data collection, data pre-processing, deep learning
models, and facial emotion recognition (FER) results. The data-gathering component will collect a dataset
of facial images from live webcams. This data set will train deep learning models for FER and restore images
with the FER 2013 dataset. CNNs will be used to construct deep learning frameworks for FER. We suggest
that by using the complete datasets FER2013, RAF-DB, and CK+, improving data quality through cleaning
techniques, and carefully addressing class imbalance, we can develop a new deep CNN model that performs
better than existing ones. CNNs require adequate training data to ensure adaptability for a specific recognition
task. However, most publicly available data sets for facial emotion recognition lack a sufficient number of
photos for training purposes. Consequently, data augmentation is an essential process for deep FER. Data
augmentation methods may be categorized into two types: spontaneous data augmentation and offline data
augmentation. The preliminary stage entails the purification of the dataset and the rectification of labels for
specific occurrences to produce a revised version of the FER2013, RAF-DB, and CK+ datasets. Subsequently,
we focused on examining the impact of various balancing procedures on this dataset. Moreover, FER 2013
constitutes a substantial dataset; training and testing procedures were executed using the datasets it offers. To
mitigate overfitting, we meticulously selected the optimal weight according to the system’s learning trajectory
to get sufficient performance. In addition, intensity normalization was employed to increase the contrast in face
photographs. A data augmentation method was used to address the limited quantity of training photos in the
FER challenge.

as separate processes, deep networks might perform facial emotion recognition in an end-to-end manner,
effectively addressing the challenge of lighting variations. A loss layer is incorporated at the end of the proposed

Conclusions
This paper presents an image-driven computer vision methodology for advancing CNN techniques for
performing FER using different datasets. The proposed framework is suitable for real-time recognition of
emotions in many applications, including augmented reality, HRI, and robots. Advanced approaches, including
data augmentation, transfer learning, and optimizing hyperparameters, can help improve the FER framework
even more. This work proposes an implementation of a framework capable of predicting and identifying
facial emotion via the use of the CNN technique and methods to extract features. The proposed approach has
great potential for FER and can make major contributions to AI and computer vision. A significant problem
in previous studies was their reliance on static pictures that do not evolve over time, rendering them timeconsuming and unsuitable for real-time applications. In contrast, our method can immediately forecast people’s
emotions using different FER datasets, including FER2013, RAF-DB, and CK+. These findings have substantial
implications for FER since they illustrate the ability of ML algorithms to analyze facial expressions with precision
and efficiency in real time. Employing advanced methodologies, including transfer learning, data augmentation,
and hyperparameter tuning, can further enhance the model. The suggested model exhibits substantial potential
for emotion recognition and could significantly advance the fields of computer vision and machine learning.
