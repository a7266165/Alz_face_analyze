Abstract

This study presents a systematic comparison and implementation of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for Facial Emotion
Recognition (FER) across multiple public datasets, namely FER-2013, FER+, RAF-DB, and AffectNet. Unlike previous studies
that focused on a single dataset or different model architectures, The main contributions of this research consist of three aspects.
First, a five-layer integrated CNN architecture is used to enable fair cross-dataset evaluation within a consistent training and
testing framework. Second, structured hyperparameter tuning is performed, including variations in learning rate, batch size, filter
configuration, and dropout rate, resulting in a stable and reproducible model configuration. Third, an in-depth analysis was
conducted to explore the impact of annotation quality and dataset complexity on model performance. The experimental results
show that FER+ achieved the highest accuracy and weighted F1 score thanks to better label consistency, followed by RAF-DB,
while FER-2013 and AffectNet experienced a decline in performance due to label noise and higher pose and lighting variations.
Further confusion matrix analysis shows that happy and neutral expressions are classified more reliably, while negative emotions
such as anger, fear, and disgust remain challenging. To validate practical application, the best-performing model was
implemented in a webcam-based facial expression recognition prototype using Python and OpenCV, demonstrating reliable
frame-level emotion inference under controlled real-time conditions.

Keywords: Convolutional Neural Network, Facial Emotion Recognition, Hyperparameter Analysis, Webcam Implementation




I. INTRODUCTION
Facial emotion recognition (FER) plays an important role
in human-computer interaction by enabling intelligent
systems to recognize and respond to human emotional states.
In applications such as social robots, healthcare assistants, and
interactive systems, the ability to recognize facial expressions
allows machines to adapt their responses more naturally and
contextually. Without reliable FER mechanisms, human–
machine interaction remains rigid and lacks emotional
awareness, limiting its effectiveness in real-world scenarios
[1].
Facial expressions are one of the most important channels
of nonverbal communication [2]. Classic studies by Ekman
and Friesen identified six basic emotions, namely happiness,
sadness, fear, disgust, surprise, and anger, that are considered
universal across cultures. However, recent studies show that
facial expression recognition performance is influenced by
additional factors such as age, gender, ethnicity, and cultural
context [3]. In addition, real-world conditions present
significant challenges, including variations in pose, lighting,
occlusion, and spontaneous expressions, which are often not
present in controlled datasets.
 Convolutional
Neural
Networks
(CNNs)
have
demonstrated strong capability in addressing these challenges
by automatically learning hierarchical feature representations
from facial images [4]. Despite this progress, existing FER
studies often evaluate models on a single dataset or use
different architectures and training strategies, making it
difficult to objectively analyze the influence of dataset
characteristics on model performance. Furthermore, many
studies emphasize architectural complexity while providing
limited analysis of how key hyperparameters and annotation
quality affect generalization across datasets.

To overcome these limitations, this study examines the

public FER datasets with diverse characteristics, namely
FER-2013, FER+, RAF-DB, and AffectNet. The main
contributions of this study lies in (1) conducting a systematic
hyperparameter analysis using a single CNN architecture, (2)
evaluating the impact of annotation quality and dataset
complexity on recognition performance, and (3) validating the
best-performing model through deployment in a webcambased FER prototype. This approach allows for a fair and
critical comparison between datasets while demonstrating the
practical feasibility of the proposed model.


II. RESEARCH METHODOLOGY
This study compares the performance of Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN) models on several dataset and
hyperparameter configurations. Figure 1 illustrates the
overall flow of this research, which begins with the collection
of public datasets (FER2013, FER+, RAFDB, and
Affectnet), followed by data preprocessing and simple data
augmentation, hyperparameter tuning, CNN model training
on each dataset with the best configuration obtained, and
model evaluation and implementation on a webcam-based
facial emotion recognition system.

A. Data Description
The research process began with the collection of datasets
to be used, namely FER-2013, FER+, RAF-DB, and
AffectNet (Table 1). These datasets were selected based on
the diversity of data characteristics in each dataset so that
they could support a more in-depth analysis of model
performance. FER-2013 was selected because it is a standard
dataset that is often used as an evaluation standard in facial
recognition research [5][6]. FER+ is a re-annotated version
of FER-2013 with better quality so that it can be used as a
comparison [7]. RAF-DB was used because it presents color
(RGB) facial images with variations in pose and lighting in
accordance with real conditions [8][9]. Meanwhile,
AffectNet is one of the largest datasets with a wide variety of
expressions, so this dataset can be used to provide an
overview of model performance in large-scale data scenarios
[9].

B. Data Preprocessing and Basic Data Augmentation
At this stage, all data undergoes consistent preprocessing
and is also adjusted to the characteristics of each dataset. In
general, this preprocessing stage involves image size
adjustment, color conversion, pixel value normalization, data
division, and basic data augmentation on the training data
from each dataset.

1. Preprocessing on the FER-2013 and FER+ datasets
The public dataset already provides cropped face images,
so there is no need for additional face detection. At the
beginning of the preprocessing stage, the image size for the
FER-2013 and FER+ datasets is adjusted to 48x48 in
accordance with the original resolution of the dataset [10].
This size is also used as an initial reference because it is
commonly used in various FER studies with CNN. In
addition, no color conversion is performed, so grayscale
image  format is used in training with the FER-2013 and
FER+ datasets.  Then, all data is normalized to the range [0,1]
to ensure training process stability and improve model
performance consistently.

2. Preprocessing on the RAF-DB and AffectNet datasets

Unlike the FER-2013 and FER+ datasets, RAF-DB and
AffectNet provide colored (RGB) facial images with more
complex variations in pose and illumination. Therefore, the
RGB format is preserved during training to maintain the
original data characteristics. While RAF-DB provides
images with a fixed resolution of 100×100 pixels and
AffectNet contains images with varying resolutions due to
web-based collection, all facial images are resized to 48×48
pixels in this study. This resizing is performed to standardize
the input resolution across all datasets, enabling the use of a
consistent CNN architecture and reducing computational
complexity.
Although resizing RGB images from RAF-DB and
AffectNet to 48×48 pixels may result in the loss of finegrained facial details, this resolution is intentionally selected
to ensure fair cross-dataset comparison and architectural
consistency. Previous FER studies have demonstrated that
discriminative emotional features can still be effectively
captured at lower resolutions using CNN-based models.
Moreover, the use of a uniform input size allows observed
performance differences to be primarily attributed to dataset
characteristics rather than variations in input resolution or
network design.

3. Data division and basic augmentation
This study follows the provided data separation, which is
to use the entire train section as training and validation data,
while the dataset's default test folder is used as test data. In
the train section, 85% of the data is used as training data and
15% as validation data. Then, all data is normalized to the
range [0,1] to ensure the stability of the research process and
improve model performance consistently.
Basic augmentation such as random rotation up to 10°,
horizontal flip, and small width and height shifts were
applied to the training data using ImageDataGenerator. This
augmentation technique aims to increase the diversity of
training images without changing the main characteristics of
the face, while also helping the CNN model to be more robust
against minor variations in the data. No additional
augmentation was performed on the validation and test data
in order to represent the model's performance with the
original data without modification.

To further prevent overfitting, early stopping was also set

checkpoint was also applied, so that the final model evaluated
was the best model.

D. Hyperparameter Tuning Procedure
Hyperparameter
tuning
was
performed
using
a
controlled, iterative experimental strategy. At each stage, one
hyperparameter, such as batch size, learning rate, or
convolutional filter configuration, was changed while the
others remained constant, allowing the impact of each
parameter to be analyzed independently.
Unlike exhaustive grid search or Bayesian optimization
methods, which require significant computational resources,
this approach was chosen to improve readability and
computational efficiency. Model performance was evaluated
based on validation accuracy and loss stability across the
dataset.

1. Initial configuration selection
The initial configuration used in the training refers to the
general pattern often used in CNN-based FER research,
namely input sizes that follow the original FER-2013 format
of 48×48, Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate in the
range of 1e-3, batch sizes between 32 and 64, several multilevel convolution blocks, and the use of dropout to reduce
overfitting. Initial testing was first conducted on public
datasets to obtain baseline performance and training curve
patterns before further variations were made.

2. Hyperparameter variation experiments
In the next stage, a series of controlled experiments were
conducted by changing one to two hyperparameters at a time
to be assessed and further evaluated for their effect on the
performance of the resulting model. The variations tested
were as follows:
● Learning rate: With the aim of determining the effect of
learning speed on training stability, tests were conducted
on learning rates of 1e-3 and 1e-4 with an exponential
decay scheme [14]. The results showed that using a
learning rate of 1e-4 produced better performance than
using a higher learning rate.
● Batch size: A comparison of model performance was
conducted using batch sizes of 32 and 64. The experiment
showed that the model performance with a batch size of
64 produced more stable and efficient results. This is also
in line with deep learning literature recommendations,
which often use medium batch sizes for image data.
● Configuration of the number of filters per convolution
block: In this study, two filter configurations were tested,
namely 64–128–256–256–512 and 32–64–128–128–256.
Based on the comparison results, it was finally decided to
use the higher filter, 64–128–256–256–512, in this study.
● Use and value of dropout: In each convolution block,
dropout with a value of 0.3 was used to reduce the risk of
overfitting. The use of dropout was determined from
initial experiments comparing the performance results of
architectures with and without dropout.

Based
on
the
experimental
results,
the
final
hyperparameter combination that provided the best
performance
and
efficiency
was
determined.
This
combination was then used as the final configuration for each
group of public datasets. The aim was to evaluate the
generalization ability of the model with the resulting tuned
configuration.

E. Experimental Scenarios
To analyze the influence of dataset characteristics and
hyperparameter configurations on CNN performance,
several controlled experimental scenarios were designed
using the same CNN architecture and final hyperparameter
configuration. It is important to note that these scenarios aim
to perform cross-dataset performance evaluation, rather than
domain generalization testing, as each dataset was trained
and evaluated independently within its own data split.

1. Scenario 1
In the first scenario, the CNN model was trained and
evaluated using the FER-2013 and FER+ datasets with an
input size of 48x48 and grayscale format. This scenario
serves as a baseline evaluation to analyze model behavior on
datasets with relatively simple image characteristics and
controlled annotation formats.

2. Scenario 2
Training in the second scenario was conducted using the
RAF-DB and AffectNet datasets, which have more complex
variations in pose and lighting. The image format is
maintained in its original format, which is color (RGB).
Although these datasets originally provide higher image
resolutions, all images were resized to 48×48 to maintain
architectural consistency. This scenario aims to examine how
increased dataset complexity affects CNN performance
under identical model configurations.
By designing these scenarios, performance differences
can be attributed primarily to dataset properties rather than
architectural or resolution factors.

G. Webcam Deployment
To demonstrate practical application, the best CNN model
was integrated into a webcam-based facial emotion
recognition prototype using Python and OpenCV. This
prototype performs real-time emotion prediction at the frame
level under controlled conditions, where subjects are placed
directly in front of the camera with stable lighting.
This implementation is intended as a functional proof of
concept rather than a comprehensive real-world evaluation.
Measurements of latency, testing for resilience to extreme
lighting changes, obstructions, or multi-face scenarios are not
included in the scope of this study. Future work will focus on
evaluating system latency, resilience to environmental
variations, and performance stability in uncontrolled realworld environments.


III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
This section presents the results of the implementation
and evaluation of the CNN model on all datasets. In all
scenarios, all models were trained using the same CNN
architecture and final hyperparameter configuration, with
adjustments to the size and images according to the
characteristics of the dataset, so that the assessment could be
carried out fairly.

The experiments described below are designed to further
explore how different dataset characteristics affect CNN
model performance. This approach allows the evaluation of
the results of CNN training to show the influence of dataset
characteristics on CNN performance.

systematic influence on the performance of CNN-based
facial emotion recognition. Rather than relying solely on
accuracy, the use of macro and weighted average F1 scores
provides additional insight into the classification behavior
per class under varying dataset conditions.

Despite higher variation in poses and lighting, RAF-DB

AffectNet is the most challenging case, with the lowest
accuracy (63.92%) and lowest macro F1 score (59.31%),

relatively low macro and weighted F1 scores indicate that
increasing the dataset size alone does not guarantee improved
generalization. High intra-class variation due to uncontrolled
facial expressions, lighting changes, and pose diversity
causes overlapping feature distributions between negative
emotion classes such as anger, fear, and disgust. Without
additional augmentation strategies or domain adaptation,
CNNs struggle to learn robust and discriminative features
under these conditions.
Overall, these results indicate that annotation reliability
and expression consistency play a more critical role in the
performance of CNN-based facial emotion recognition
systems than dataset size alone. When a unified architectures
and hyperparameter configurations are applied, performance
variations can be systematically attributed to specific dataset
properties rather than architectural biases. This analysis
highlights the importance of dataset selection and
characterization as fundamental steps in developing reliable
facial emotion recognition systems. Although causal
inference cannot be fully proven, the consistent performance
patterns observed across various evaluation metrics provide
strong empirical evidence that dataset annotation quality and
intra-class variability significantly affect the performance of
CNN-based FER.

B. Hyperparameter Influence Analysis
In addition to looking at the performance of CNNs on
several datasets, this study also analyzes the effect of
hyperparameter variations on CNN performance. In this
study, the final hyperparameters obtained through the
analysis of the dataset variation experiment were used,
namely learning rate, batch size, configuration of the number
of filters per convolution block, and the use of dropout.
In the learning rate test, an initial experiment was
conducted using a smaller learning rate, namely 1e-4 with an
exponential decay scheme. Next, a comparison experiment
was conducted using a learning rate of 1e-3 with an
exponential decay scheme. The test results showed relatively
similar performance. The learning rate of 1e-3 produced a
slightly higher test accuracy (82.87%) but with a higher loss
value (0.6017). Conversely, the learning rate of 1e-4
provided an accuracy of 82.01% with a lower loss (0.5572)
and a more stable training curve. Therefore, the learning rate
of 1e-4 was used throughout the study, considering the tradeoff between accuracy, convergence stability, and probability
calibration.
Batch size testing was also conducted by comparing the
test results from experiments using batch sizes of 32 and 64.
The test results also produced similar accuracy, namely
82.01% for batch size 64 and 82.59% for model test accuracy
with batch size 32. However, the test loss produced by the
model with a batch size of 32 was very large compared to 64,
namely 4.7103, while the test loss in the batch size 64 model
was only 0.5572. Therefore, a batch size of 64 was used in
all training because using a batch size of 32 could result in an
unstable training process and poor probability calibration.
For the configuration of the number of filters per
convolution block, a comparison was made between the use
of smaller 32-64-128-128-256 filters and 64-128-256-256-
512 filters. The lighter configuration resulted in slightly
higher test accuracy, namely 82.52% with a higher loss value
(0.6487). Meanwhile, the higher configuration resulted in a
slightly lower test accuracy of 82.02% with a lower loss
value (0.5572). Considering the very small difference in

C. In-Depth Evaluation of the Best Model
Based on the evaluation results on the entire public
dataset, the CNN model trained using the FER+ dataset
showed the best performance because it produced the best
accuracy and F1-score values, namely 81.86% and 81.54%.
In addition, a confusion matrix analysis was also carried out
to see the distribution of predictions for each emotion class.
Figure 3 is the confusion matrix from training the CNN

Figure 3. Confusion Matrix of the Best Model with FER+

The confusion matrix shows that the CNN model
consistently achieves high positive accuracy rates for
expressions of happiness and neutral, which have distinctive
and stable facial features even on low-resolution grayscale
inputs. On the other hand, systematic classification error
patterns are observed in the negative emotion classes,
particularly anger, fear, and disgust. These emotions have
similar facial muscle activations and subtle visual
differences, causing overlapping feature representations
learned by the CNN.
Furthermore, classification errors between sadness and
fear further indicate that the model has difficulty
distinguishing emotions with similar expression intensities
and shape variations. This behavior is reflected in the
evaluation metrics, where the macro precision and macro
recall values are lower than the weighted F1 score. This
discrepancy indicates that although the model performs well
on dominant classes, recognizing less frequent or visually
ambiguous emotions remains challenging. These results
suggest that the observed classification errors are driven by
intrinsic visual similarities between emotion classes rather
than random prediction noise.
The FER+ model was selected for further evaluation not
only based on its highest accuracy and F1-weighted score,
but also because it demonstrated the most stable and balanced
performance profile across various evaluation metrics. The
relatively consistent difference between the macro average
metric and the weighted metric indicates an improvement in
class separation ability and a decrease in sensitivity to
annotation ambiguity compared to other datasets.
This performance stability indicates that the refined
annotation process in FER+ allows CNNs to learn more
discriminative and consistent emotion representations under
a unified architecture and hyperparameter configuration.
Furthermore, from an implementation perspective, the use of
48×48 grayscale images together with the selected
hyperparameters (learning rate 1e−4, batch size 64, dropout
rate of 0.3, and filter configuration of 64–128–256–256–512)
provides an effective balance between recognition accuracy,
convergence
stability,
and
computational
efficiency.
Therefore, the FER+ model is considered the most
representative candidate for integration into webcam-based
facial emotion recognition systems.

D. Webcam System Evaluation
In the final stage of this research, an implementation was
carried out in a webcam-based facial expression recognition
system to demonstrate the practical applicability of the
proposed CNN model (Figure 4). The implementation was
developed using the Python programming language, while
OpenCV was utilized to access the webcam video stream and
perform image processing tasks [24].
Each frame captured from the webcam is converted to
grayscale, followed by face detection using the Haar Cascade
algorithm. This method was selected due to its computational
efficiency and its ability to detect multiple faces in a single
frame with sufficient accuracy for demonstration purposes
[25]. The detected face region is then cropped, resized to a
resolution of 48×48 pixels, and normalized using the same
preprocessing scheme applied during the training phase. The
processed facial image is subsequently fed into the trained
CNN model to generate emotion class probability
predictions. This implementation is intended to demonstrate
that the proposed model is not limited to offline evaluation
but can also be deployed in interactive, frame-based
applications using live webcam input.

IV. CONCLUSION
This study examines the performance of a single CNN
architecture with fixed hyperparameter configurations on
various public facial emotion recognition datasets with
different characteristics. The experimental results show that,
when trained with identical architecture and training settings,
CNN models exhibit different levels of performance
depending on the quality of dataset annotation, class balance,
and data complexity.
The results indicate that datasets with more detailed
annotations and more consistent label distributions, such as
FER+ and RAF-DB, tend to produce higher accuracy and F1scores compared to datasets with higher label noise or greater
variation, such as FER-2013 and AffectNet. This indicates
that dataset characteristics play a significant role in
influencing CNN-based FER performance, even when the
same model configuration is applied.
From the hyperparameter influence analysis, the selected
configuration—learning rate of 1e-4 with exponential decay,
batch size of 64, dropout rate of 0.3, and filter configuration
of 64–128–256–256–512—provided a stable balance
between accuracy, loss convergence, and training stability
across the dataset. Based on these findings, the CNN model
trained on the FER+ dataset achieved the most consistent
performance and was selected for further implementation.
A webcam-based facial expression recognition system
was implemented as a functional demonstration to show the
feasibility of applying the trained model in interactive
scenarios. This implementation aimed to demonstrate
deployment feasibility rather than provide a quantitative
evaluation of real-time system performance.
The main contribution of this research lies in the
systematic analysis of CNN performance on various public
FER
datasets
using
uniform
architectures
and
hyperparameter settings, as well as highlighting the influence
of dataset characteristics on model behavior. Future research
will focus on the application of advanced data augmentation
techniques or domain adaptation, as well as conducting
comprehensive real-world performance evaluations to
improve model robustness and generalization.
