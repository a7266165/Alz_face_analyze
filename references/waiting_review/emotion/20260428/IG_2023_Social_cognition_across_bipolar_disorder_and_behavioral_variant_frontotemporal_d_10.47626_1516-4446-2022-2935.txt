Abstract
Objectives: Bipolar Disorder type 1 (BD1) and behavioral variant frontotemporal dementia
(bvFTD) share similar behavioral and cognitive symptoms, rendering the differential diagnosis
between them a clinical challenge. We investigated the accuracy of social cognition measures to
differentiate bvFTD from BD.
Methods: We included three groups of participants: early-onset BD1 (in remission, n = 20),
bvFTD (n = 18), and cognitively healthy controls (HC, n = 40), matched for age, schooling, and
sex. All participants underwent cognitive assessment including the facial emotion recognition
(FER) and the modified faux-pas tests, which assess mentalizing.
Results: Compared to HC, BD1, and bvFTD patients underperformed on both social cognition
measures. BD1 and bvFTD did not differ on the FER’s or faux pas tests’ total score, although
bvFTD patients had significantly higher difficulties than BD1 to detect social faux-pas (p <
0.001, d = 1.35).
Conclusions: BD1 and bvFTD share deficits in the core social cognition functions. These
findings should be considered in the development of tasks aiming to improve the clinical
differentiation between both disorders.

Introduction
Bipolar Disorder (BD) is a chronic, disabling, and complex mental illness, associated with poor
functionality and reduced interpersonal functioning1. BD mood swings Patients with BD might
exhibit psychiatric and behavioral symptoms that are also criteria for the diagnosis of behavioral
variant frontotemporal dementia (bvFTD)2, including impulsivity, disinhibition and apathy.
Besides neuropsychiatric manifestations, BD and bvFTD also share similar cognitive deficits
such as executive dysfunction, commonly present in both disorders3,4. Social cognition (SC)
refers to a set of cognitive functions involved in the processing of social signals and are critical
for social adaptation and appropriate interpersonal exchanges. Emotion recognition and
mentalizing have been described as its core processes. Impairments of SC are frequent in
bvFTD4, but could be present in BD as well1, 3.
The differential diagnosis between bvFTD and primary psychiatric disorders (PPD) is a key
challenge, particularly in behavioral disorders initiated after the age of 50 years5.  For most
clinicians, the distinction between FTD and BD may be challenging, and a thorough revision of
psychopathology is required. BD seems to present more frequently grandiosity, often
accompanied by increased energy and decreased need for sleep, disinhibition and euphoric
features, and a past history of depression.  Instead, some FTD individuals may show and
stereotyped and inadequate sexual behavior, such as public masturbation, and may show
repetitive motor behavior and stereotypies. bvFTD is the most common cause of young-onset
dementia after Alzheimer’s disease and frequently manifests with marked behavioral and

personality changes, such as disinhibition, loss of empathy, and social misconduct. Patients with
bvFTD may also present psychiatric symptoms frequently retrieved in PPD such as mania,
delusions, and hallucinations2. These shared features may led to an important delay in the
diagnosis of bvFTD as at least 50% of patients receive a psychiatric diagnosis prior to the correct
diagnosis6. Almost 25% of patients with bvFTD might present the BD diagnosis during the
lifetime7. In this context, BD might present as the most challenging differential diagnosis to
bvFTD6.
In the absence of biomarkers, the use of SC tasks might be a tool to help to differentiate these
conditions2. However, data are critically lacking to support these recommendations, as the studies
that have performed a direct contrast between early-onset dementia and late-onset psychiatric
conditions are scarce5. To date, only one study5 directly compared SC in patients with BD and
bvFTD, and demonstrated poorer performance on a mentalizing test in bvFTD. Here, we aimed to
compare the performance in SC’ core processes of remitted BD type 1 (BD 1) and bvFTD
patients.

Methods
We included 20 remitted early-onset BD 1 patients, 18 bvFTD patients, and 40 cognitively
healthy controls (HC) in the present study. All patients were recruited at the University Hospital
of the Federal University of Minas Gerais (Belo Horizonte, Brazil). BD 1 were assessed with the
Mini-International Neuropsychiatric Interview; the 17-items Hamilton Depression Rating Scale
(HDRS), and the Young Mania Rating Scale (YMRS) to determine the severity of depressive and

manic symptoms. Remission was deﬁned by YMRS and HDRS scores <7 points, for at least 8
weeks.
bvFTD patients fulfilled consensual diagnostic criteria for probable bvFTD 8, i.e., patients had a
consistent history of personality and behavioural changes associated to functional decline and
evidence of focal atrophy or hypoperfusion in frontotemporal regions on brain magnetic
resonance imaging (MRI) or on single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT),
respectively. The previous diagnosis of BD or schizophrenia was an exclusion criterion for this
group. HC were recruited from the local population and had no history of psychiatric or
neurologic disorders. They did not present cognitive complaints and had normal scores on the
Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE).
All participants underwent a cognitive evaluation that included the MMSE, the Frontal
Assessment Battery (FAB), and the Figure Memory Test (FMT) from the Brief Cognitive
Screening Battery (BCSB).
SC was assessed with the mini Social cognition and Emotional Assessment (Mini-SEA),
composed of a Facial Emotion Recognition (FER) and the modified faux-pas (mFP) tests
assessing mentalizing. For the mFP, in addition to the total score, subscores were also considered:
detection, attribution, identification, knowledge, intentionality, and empathy. Moreover, two
components of the test were distinguished: the correct recognition of social norms violation (the
sum of scores for questions 1, 2 and 3); and mentalizing ability (the sum of scores for questions
4, 5 and 6). The total Mini-SEA score is the sum of the FER and mFP scores, with higher scores
indicating better performance.

The local Ethics Research Committee approved the study. All participants or their legal
representatives provided written informed consent.
Statistical Analyses
Statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS 22.0 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, Illinois). Shapiro-Wilk
test refuted the assumption of normality. Non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis, Mann-Whitney tests &
effect size (Cohen’s d) were used for group comparisons. We adopted Bonferroni’s correction for
multiple comparisons; the level of significance (α) was set at 0.025. The accuracy of SC
measures was investigated with Receiver Operating Characteristics (ROC) curve analyses.

Results
Table 1 presents the demographic, clinical and neuropsychological data. Participants did not
differ on age, sex and schooling. Compared to HC, bvFTD and BD1 patients presented poorer
performances on all cognitive measures. Remitted BD 1 and bvFTD patients had similar
performance on all SC scores, except on the detection of faux-pas (to identify whether or not
there is a faux-pas), with bvFTD performing worse than BD1 (p<0.001, d=1.29) (Figure 1).

Discussion
This is the first study to compare the performance on SC’ core processes (facial emotion
recognition and mentalizing) between remitted BD1 and bvFTD patients. These clinical groups
did not differ in all cognitive and SC measures, except for the detection of faux-pas.
BD1 and bvFTD had lower scores than HC on MMSE, FAB and episodic memory scores, but did
not differ between each other. Executive and episodic memory impairment are commonly
observed in bvFTD4, and might be present in BD3. Our findings highlight that standard cognitive
measures do not contribute to the diagnostic differentiation between bvFTD and BD.
Clinical follow-up is typically envisaged as useful to distinguish both conditions in the
challenging context of global cognitive deterioration as longitudinal studies demonstrated
cognitive performance remained stable in BD over a 6-year period9,10. Recent systematic review
suggested that BD is associated with a three-fold increased risk of dementia, although authors
appointed high heterogeneity of studies might be an important factor with the low strength of
evidence11. Interestingly, some case reports suggested an association between bvFTD and BD12.
This observation highlights the need to find specific cognitive markers to reliably distinguish
both disorders2.
As expected, BD and bvFTD had lower performance than HC in emotion recognition and
mentalizing, two core dimensions of SC, in agreement with previous studies1,3,4. A unique study5
reported that bvFTD patients had lower scores than BD (types I and II) on the Reading the Mind
in the Eyes, which is controversial regarding the necessary involvement of mentalizing abilities
(vs. emotion recognition) in the task’s performance. In our study, aiming to assess the core
components of SC, we employed the mFP, which does involve both mentalizing and social

context decoding abilities, alongside the FER. Our methodology also relied on strict correction
(Bonferroni) for multiple comparisons, thus increasing the reliability of our findings. Our results
suggest that the decreased ability to detect faux pas could be considered as a specific marker of
bvFTD – which is in line with previous studies13,14. Interestingly, while BD patients are able to
detect faux pas, they were unable to explain the faux pas correctly, similarly to bvFTD patients.
Future studies should explore the qualitative differences in the responses given as potential
indicators of diagnosis.
BD and bvFTD patients did not differ on the FER. Previous data show that bvFTD4 and BD15
have impairment on emotion recognition although, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first
study to directly compare BD and bvFTD on this ability. Previous study demonstrated that FER
might differentiate bvFTD and depressive disorders16, but more studies are warranted to
investigate the utility of such tests to differentiate bvFTD from PPD.
We acknowledge that sample sizes as well as narrowing the assessment of executive functions to
the FAB are limits to this study. It is therefore not excluded that a more accurate distinction
between BD and bvFTD could be provided by an extended cognitive and SC assessment. The
effect of medications on cognitive performance was not considered here, which might also be an
issue given the use of antipsychotics and mood stabilizers drugs in both disorders, although this is
a common limit in the field.
In the absence of biomarkers, the distinction between bvFTD and BD remains a challenge.
Altogether, our results should be considered in the development of tasks aiming to improve the
clinical differentiation between bvFTD and BD. While the use of SC tests has been recently
consensually recommended in this context2, our study emphasized the importance of

single one, our findings also underline the utility of a more qualitative approach in the assessment
of SC as differences were retrieved in a single dimension of the mFP, but not the total score.
