Abstract
Facial Action Units (AUs) have recently been used in dementia detection, pain detection, talking head
generation, and even facial reconstruction tasks. The success of each of these applications is at least
partially due to the performance of the underlying AU recognition model. The commonly reported
metric for comparing AU recognition is the average F1 score. Improving the average F1 score for AU
recognition will directly improve the performance of each application that requires AU recognition.
To improve the average F1 score, we propose simultaneously generating the nodes and edges for the
graph neural network, strategically using landmark data, present additional AU recognition multitask learning methods, and introduce ensemble learning to AU recognition. Although most current
solutions for AU recognition generate the nodes and the edges separately, our proposed method
demonstrates the improvement that comes from simultaneously generating the nodes and edges. In
addition, our method proposes to use the available landmark data in a multi-task learning method.
Our solution also applies ensemble learning to AU recognition. Through extensive experimentation,
we demonstrate an improvement in the state-of-the-art average F1 score from 66.3 to 67.3 and from
66.9 to 67.8 on the BP4D and DISFA datasets, a considerable improvement in this ﬁeld. These
results underscore the substantial improvements our proposed method brings to the application of
AU recognition.
Keywords: Ensemble learning, Facial AU recognition, Facial landmarks, Graph neural network, Multi-task
learning, Transformer
1 Introduction
Facial action units (AUs) have been used recently
in dementia detection [1], pilot mental workload
assessment [2], talking head generation [3], artiﬁcial expression synthetization [4], and other tasks
[5]. Each of these applications takes AU recognition as the input for its task. The performance
of the underlying AU recognition model drives
at least part of the success for each of these
applications. Improving the average F1 score for
an AU recognition model will also enhance the
performance of each of these applications.
Current solutions for AU recognition commonly use Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) [6–12].

Now that many use GNNs to improve AU recognition performance, the discussion has transitioned
to focus on the method that generates the optimal nodes and edges. Current methods generate
the nodes and edges separately [6–12] with some
methods even generating the nodes and edges in
separate training runs [11]. In addition, both the
BP4D [13, 14] and DISFA [15, 16] datasets contain
facial landmarks. Methods use these landmarks as
an intermediate prediction to crop to certain areas
of the face.
Despite the success achieved by generating
nodes and edges separately, there remains potential for improvement in simultaneously creating
both nodes and edges. A similar argument is found
in related ﬁelds through model fusion or model
consolidation. Model consolidation argues for better performance by merging multiple models into
one model [17]. The reasoning behind model consolidation is that when a model learns multiple
tasks, it can improve the overall performance on
each task individually. The same reasoning can be
applied to learning the nodes and edges simultaneously during training. In addition, many of the
methods that do not crop to certain areas of the
face throw away the landmark data. Ignoring the
available data limits the overall performance of a
system.
The current approach of generating nodes and
edges separately is in contrast to the beneﬁts
found in model consolidation. In addition, the current use of landmark data limits which models
can use this additional training data. We propose a solution that simultaneously learns the
nodes and edges for the GNN and strategically
uses the landmark data. We name our solution Ensemble Learning Encoder GrAph Neural
Multi-Task Learning (ELEGANT). The simultaneous node and edge creation happens with our
Attention Graph Generator (AGG) module. Later
in the model’s architecture, the landmark data is
used to introduce landmark prediction as a Multi-
Task Learning (MTL) method in our COntrastive
Au-co-prediction Landmark (COAL) module. In
addition, ELEGANT combines two base models
in an ensemble learning approach, which increases
performance even further. Notably, ELEGANT
outperformed all previous AU recognition solutions on the average F1 metric even without
ensemble learning. ELEGANT’s contributions are
compared with previous methods in Table 1.
In summary, the contributions of this work
include:
1. The proposal of a neural network architecture, ELEGANT, which simultaneously learns the
nodes and edges within the AGG module, allowing
for interaction before being passed into the GNN.
2. We introduce using landmark predictions
as an MTL approach for AU recognition within
our COAL learning module. This additional training data provides a more complete understanding
of the face in general. Previous solutions could
only use the additional landmark data when their
model architecture directly used it.
3. We also introduce additional MTL learning methods in the COAL learning module and
ensemble learning for AU recognition. Our solution uses established deep learning methodologies
speciﬁcally applied to AU recognition in areas that
have not been extensively explored.
4. Extensive experimentation that provides
evidence that the ELEGANT solution outperforms all other current state-of-the-art methods on
the generally used AU recognition datasets: BP4D
[13, 14] and DISFA [15, 16].
The subsequent sections of this paper are organized as follows. Section 2 provides an overview
of the necessary background, followed by Section
3 with the proposed methodology. Then, Section
4 presents the associated results. Each element of
our model is analyzed through the ablation study
given in Section 5, and we provide a discussion of
our ﬁndings in Section 6. Finally, we conclude in
Section 7.

2 Background
AUs were originally developed as part of the Facial
Action Coding System (FACS) to describe human
facial motion using anatomical movements [18].
Real-world applications ﬁrst began with depression analysis [19], micro-expression recognition
[20], emotion recognition [21–23], and have now
been applied in various ﬁelds [5]. The BP4D
[13, 14] and DISFA [15, 16] datasets were released
in 2013 to serve as benchmarks for AU recognition.
Example images are shown in Figure 1 while Table
2 lists the AUs in these datasets. The average F1
score is generally recognized as the best metric to
compare performance across diﬀerent research in
this ﬁeld [9–12, 24–29].
2.1 Landmarks in AU Recognition
Both the BP4D [13, 14] and DISFA [15, 16]
datasets provide landmark data for training purposes. Some methods use these landmarks for
cropping to areas of the face [30], normalization
[25], pre-processing techniques [24], cropping [8],
or an anchor for their predictions [12]. Many of
these methods have improved the average F1 score
by using the provided landmark labels.
However, an increasing number of studies do
not use landmark information [9–11, 26, 29, 31].
Some claim that using landmarks is restrictive
[29]. Many others claim that their method performed better without using this additional information, but seem to suggest that having this
additional data would improve results. However,
these researchers have no way of including the
landmark data in their solution’s architecture and
simply must set these data aside. Our solution
proposes using these landmark data in our COAL
learning module in a way that can be applied to
all future research projects.
2.2 Graph Neural Network with
AU Recognition
GNN based AU recognition approaches began in
2018 [6], and many have continued to use GNNs
in their AU recognition architecture [7–12]. GNN
based AU recognition architectures have focused
improvements on providing probailistic solutions
[7], using the edges as correlation between AUs
[8], addressing class imbalance [9], and moving beyond maximum likelihood estimation [10].
Recent improvements have focused on making
the edges in a GNN multidimensional [11] and
combining region and global feature learning [12].
These methods generate the nodes and the
edges in diﬀerent locations within the network.
Some methods, such as ME-GraphAU, generate
the nodes and the edges even in separate training
runs [11]. ELEGANT builds on previous advancements by simultaneously generating edges and
nodes while uniquely incorporating established
deep learning methodologies, oﬀering a higher
average F1 score in AU recognition.
2.3 Ensemble Learning
Ensemble learning, combines multiple models to
improve performance. The ﬁrst deep ensemble
appeared in 2016, merging Bayesian and non-
Bayesian neural networks [32]. Despite ensemble
learning’s long history, ELEGANT is the ﬁrst AU
recognition model to employ ensemble learning,
leveraging its beneﬁts to enhance AU recognition
performance.

3 Methods
In this paper, we propose a new architecture, ELE-
GANT for AU recognition. In our approach, the
image is passed through pre-processing, the visual
backbone, and then our AGG module generates
the nodes and edges simultaneously. Next, the
edges and nodes are passed to the GCN, which is
then passed to both our COAL learning module
and the AU ﬁnal prediction head. The base learner
of our ELEGANT solution is given in Figure 2.
To maintain the baseline and accurately compare with previous studies, ELEGANT also uses
MTCNN [33] to crop and align the face as preprocessing. The visual backbone is a ViT [34],
speciﬁcally a Swin Transformer base model [35].
The graph neural network is a gated GCN [36].
As these three items are used in previous articles
[11, 12], we do not give a further explanation in
this article concerning these topics. However, we
provide an in-depth discussion of our unique contributions to modifying and including the AGG,
COAL learning, and Ensemble Learning modules
for AU recognition.
3.1 AGG Module
While previous methods generate the nodes and
edges independently [8–12], our method generates the nodes and edges simultaneously. This is
accomplished by using attention as deﬁned as:
Attention(Q, K, V ) = softmax(QKT
√dk
)V
(1)
where Q is the query, K is the key, and V is
the value as deﬁned by Vaswani et al. [37]. Using
the attention mechanism, the nodes and edges
are generated simultaneously, allowing for each to
learn from the other before any combination in the
GNN.
Before the attention layers, we use a learned
positional encoding that passes the input vector
through a linear layer for each input, resulting
in learning a unique representation for each positional location. Positional encodings are critical
to attention heads. We have the total number of
input and output nodes for the attention layers to
be:
N = n × n + n
(2)
where N represents the number of inputs into
the Encoder and n represents the number of AUs
being classiﬁed. This results in having enough outputs to then split the ﬁrst n outputs from the
encoder to be the nodes and the remaining n × n
elements to be the edges. We combine multiple
encoder layers, using three for the BP4D dataset
and ﬁve for the DISFA dataset. We provide a
diagram to better visualize the AGG module
in Figure 3. The output is then passed to the
single-layer GNN.
3.2 COAL Learning
The COntrasitive AU-co-prediction Landmark
(COAL) learning module is built to perform MTL
and strategically use the landmark data included
in the datasets. We include three additional
tasks to the AU predictions to increase the overall performance: landmark prediction, contrastive
embedding learning, and AU co-prediction. A
schematic illustrating the COAL learning module
is provided in Figure 4.
Including landmark predictions as an MTL
method allows us to use the landmark data
included in the datasets as additional training
data. Using these data as training data allows us

3.4 Training Details
We ﬁrst pretrain a base learner on the FEC
dataset. We then train the AU recognition model
with the COAL learning module, and then ﬁnetune the model directly on the AU prediction task
without the COAL learning module.
For pretraining, we use the AdamW optimizer
[38] with a learning rate weight decay of 5e-4. The
pretraining is done on the FEC dataset task.
After pre-training, we train with the COAL
learning module included. For our ensemble, we
have one base model that uses the pretrained
weights and another base model that uses randomly initialized weights. We use a learning rate of
1e-4 for both networks, regardless of their initialization method, across both datasets. We weight
each loss from the COAL learning module by
weights that were found by inspection, although
we maintained the weights across folds to prevent
overﬁtting. The initial training was conducted
for 5 epochs, and the best-performing epoch was
selected while ensuring that each fold used the
same epoch to again prevent overﬁtting.
Upon completing the ﬁrst training stage, we
removed the COAL learning module, decreased
the learning rate, and ﬁne-tuned the model
directly on the AU recognition task. We now train
for 20 epochs, again selecting the same epoch
across all folds to prevent overﬁtting. To maintain
a valid comparison, we perform three-fold crossvalidation using the same train and validation
split as previous work [11, 12].
3.5 Data
BP4D [13] is a manually annotated dataset that
includes facial videos in response to tasks that
elicit diﬀerent emotions. The dataset also contains
ground truth 2D landmark points. The 41 participants pictured in the dataset ranged from 18
to 29 years old. Of the 41 participants, 23 were
women and the remaining 18 were men. Six of
the participants were Black, 11 were Asian, four
were Hispanic or Latino, and 20 were White or
Caucasian.
DISFA [15] is another manually annotated
dataset that contains facial videos. The dataset
also included 66 facial landmark points for each
image. The dataset contains 27 participants aged
18 to 50 years old. 12 of the participants were
women and 15 were men. One of the participants
was Black, three were Asian, two were Hispanic or
Latino, and 21 were White or Caucasian.
FEC [39] is not a AU recognition dataset.
Rather, it is a dataset that contains triplets of
images and includes a label for which image is the
most diﬀerent from the other two. The dataset is
a large-scale facial expression dataset with 87,517
diﬀerent images downloaded from Flickr. As this
dataset does not contain any AU labels, we use it
in the pre-training step of the ELEGANT method.
4 Results
We ﬁnd that across the literature, not one solution
dominates performance over all the selected AUs
in the BP4D and DISFA datasets. This is seen
with the improvements from line to line not fully
outperforming all AUs in the previous line in Table
3 and Table 4. Rather, the focus is on the average
F1 score across all AUs. Across AU recognition
literature the average F1 score is used as the main
metric for comparison [9–12, 24–29].
In Table 3, we compare against other AU
recognition methods on the BP4D dataset. We
ﬁnd that the ELEGANT method obtains the best
average F1 score on the BP4D dataset. Similarly, we compare the ELEGANT method against
the same top-performing methods on the DISFA
dataset in Table 4. The ELGEGANT method
again outperforms all other methods that benchmark results with the average F1 score. Our
ELEGANT solution achieves the highest average
F1 score on both the BP4D and DISFA datasets.

6 Discussion
Improving the average F1 score for AU recognition
can enhance the performance of certain downstream applications. We ﬁnd that simultaneously
generating the nodes and edges while using landmark data as additional training data within our
COAL learning module improves the average F1
score. Additionally, we ﬁnd that while pretraining on the FEC dataset increases the average F1
score, it hinders performance on certain AUs.
Many previous methods generate nodes and
edges in separate locations in the network, and
even across diﬀerent training runs. We ﬁnd that
using our AGG module to generate the nodes and
edges simultaneously in the same location within
the network architecture improves the average F1
score. We hypothesize that this is due to the
nodes and edges attending to each other before
being passed into the GNN. Future researchers
can apply this ﬁnding by exchanging their graph
generation method with positional encodings and
transformer layers, as we described in our AGG
module.
Before this article, in AU recognition, landmark data was used only in intermediate steps,
such as cropping to certain locations. We ﬁnd
that using landmark predictions as an additional
MTL method in the COAL learning module also
improves the average F1 score. Future AU recognition models can apply this ﬁnding by adding
8

an MTL head for landmark prediction, which will
make the network learn a more comprehensive
facial representation.
We discovered that while pretraining on the
FEC dataset improved the average F1 score, it
actually decreased the F1 score for certain AUs.
AUs such as inner brow raiser, brow lowerer,
chin raiser, and lip tightener decreased in performance when the model was pretrained on the FEC
dataset. We hypothesize that this is due to the fact
that these AUs being smaller facial expressions.
The FEC dataset labels which of the three images
contains the most diﬀerent facial expressions. As
such, these smaller facial motions were primarily
overlooked as the labelers were instructed to focus
on the larger diﬀerences in facial expressions. As
the model is pretrained on this dataset, it is taught
to ignore these smaller facial motions, making it
more diﬃcult to relearn them during model training. Future researchers can apply this ﬁnding as
we did by using a stacking ensemble or other methods that take into account the tradeoﬀs of using
the FEC dataset for pretraining.
We acknowledge that our solution does not
account for variation in lighting, nor extreme head
rotation. In addition, as with many face recognition datasets [42], our solution is not tested in
variation across race, gender, and age. These limitations are primarily due to the BP4D and the
DISFA datasets being collected in a controlled
environment. Future research can focus on developing evaluation sets that contain this variation
or designing solutions to address these directly. As
these limitations are outside the scope of this article, we maintain our position on the importance
of our contributions to the ﬁeld of AU recognition
while simultaneously encouraging future research
to address these limitations.
Future researchers can apply the ﬁndings of
this article by using attention layers for generating the nodes and edges simultaneously, as we
do in our AGG module. Researchers can also add
landmark prediction as a way to incorporate additional data in an MTL learning approach, as we do
in our COAL learning module. Researchers may
also be interested in adding more MTL learning
approaches, such as contrastive learning and AU
co-occurrence predictions. Finally, researchers can
be intentional with their use of pre-training data
and acknowledge the tradeoﬀs that come from
using it.
7 Conclusion
In conclusion, this study presents an AU recognition solution, ELEGANT, which incorporates
a new AGG module, a COAL learning module,
and ensemble learning. This solution introduces a
unique approach that simultaneously develops the
edges and nodes for the AU recognition task. It
also provides three MTL methods that contribute
to learning the AU recognition task more broadly:
AU co-occurrence prediction, contrastive learning,
and facial landmark prediction. Finally, this article brings the beneﬁts of ensemble learning to
the AU recognition ﬁeld. The experiments demonstrate the improvements from each of these contributions and ultimately achieve SOTA results on
the BP4D and DISFA datasets by increasing the
average F1 score by 1.0 and 0.9 percentage points,
respectively. The code for this method is available
at https://github.com/byu-rvl/ELEGANT.
