Abstract
With human-computer interactions on the rise, in use for even the most mundane
things like shopping, making travel arrangements and ordering food as well as
matters of national importance such as healthcare and security, Facial Expression
Recognition has become crucial to our lives. Currently used traditional facial
expression recognition systems rely on manual tuning by experts to get optimised
results, face challenges such as the demand of resources and expert-level knowledge
of a plethora of neural network architectures which prevents the technology
from being widely adopted. This paper demonstrates a practical application of
Automated Machine Learning using a framework known as AutoGluon to automate
model selection and hyperparameter tuning for Facial Expression Recognition,
achieving competitive accuracy (76.4%) on the challenging FER2013 dataset. It
also provides a comprehensive analysis of four model configurations (Default, Low
Resource, Balanced, High Quality), enabling users to balance computational cost
and performance. These models outperform several state-of-the-art manually tuned
models (RMN, ViT, MLFCC etc.) and highlight AutoML’s potential to democratize
FER development for non-experts. This paper also includes the use of metrics such
as precision, recall and f1-score along with accuracy to provide a more holistic
understanding of the models’ strengths and limitations, and includes ethical
considerations and future directions, addressing critical issues like bias mitigation
and real-world deployment challenges. This work confirms the potential of AutoML
to revolutionize facial expression recognition, making advanced facial expression
analysis more accessible and effective.

1  Introduction
Facial expressions have always been an elemental aspect of communication, conveying a
wealth of emotional and social information. From joy and sadness to anger and surprise,
facial expressions are a major aspect of interpersonal interactions, affective computing,
and human-computer interaction. Accurately interpreting facial expressions is crucial
to various domains, including psychology, medicine, marketing, security, and entertain­
ment. Recent advances in machine learning (ML) have transformed the field of facial
expression recognition (FER), enabling effective real-world applications, particularly in
the context of big data [1, 2]. Some of these applications are:
(i)	Emotion analysis in large-scale market research.
(ii)	Monitoring patients in healthcare department.
(iii)	 Real-time surveillance of public areas for suspicious behaviour.
(iv)	 Assessment of student engagement in online learning.
(v)	Detecting driver drowsiness to improve road safety.
More specifically, FER in healthcare [3] is of utmost importance as demonstrated
through the revolutionary company PainChek, which uses AI-driven facial analysis to
assess pain levels in patients who cannot communicate (such as those with dementia),
where the AI analyses micro-expressions associated with pain and generates an objec­
tive pain score. This allows for timely pain relief in residents who might otherwise suffer
silently [4]. Another application of FER systems in healthcare was at the Yokohama City
University Hospital, which developed an AI system to watch over sedated intensive care
patients by looking for subtle signs of discomfort in the patients’ faces and eyes [5].
FER, once solely a human domain, has been revolutionized by artificial intelligence
(AI), enabling machines to process vast image and video data to detect and interpret
expressions. As technology advances, FER plays a vital role in enhancing human-com­
puter interaction by enabling systems to recognize emotions and intentions. Given that
facial expressions convey 55% of the information being passed, with actual language
constituting only 7% [6], FER offers powerful insights into a person’s emotional and
mental state. Its applications span virtual assistants, emotion aware gaming, personal­
ized e-commerce experiences and more- making interfaces more intuitive, responsive
and human centric.
With the rise of the internet, abundant image data is now available to train models for
emotion recognition. Despite advances in FER technology [7], its real-world adoption
remains limited due to persistent challenges. These include cultural differences in inter­
preting expressions [8], and inconsistencies in image quality, such as variations in light­
ing, occlusions, and resolution—common issues in Big Data. Overcoming these requires
robust machine learning algorithms capable of generalizing across diverse conditions.
Another key obstacle is the substantial resource demand—time, expertise, and compu­
tation—needed to design and optimize high-performing FER models. Even skilled data

AutoML automates the end-to-end ML pipeline from raw data to model deployment
using frameworks like AutoGluon, Google Cloud AutoML, and TPOT [10]. These tools
simplify tasks such as model selection, hyperparameter optimization, feature engineer­
ing, and ensembling, making advanced model development accessible to non-experts.
By automating complex and time-consuming processes, AutoML enables researchers to
focus on higher-level insights, while efficiently identifying optimal model configurations
through sophisticated search strategies.
In the context of FER, AutoML offers several advantages and opportunities for
advancing the current state-of-the-art. Firstly, AutoML enables researchers to explore a
plethora of ML models and architectures, including many foundational FER algorithms
as well as convolutional neural networks (CNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs)
and ensemble methods, without requiring specialized expertise in each algorithm. This
flexibility allows for the identification of the most suitable model architecture for the
given task and dataset, potentially leading to improved performance and generalization.
Furthermore, AutoML frameworks facilitate efficient hyperparameter optimization,
enabling the fine-tuning of model parameters to enhance performance and robustness.
Hyperparameters play a critical role in determining a model’s effectiveness, and AutoML
techniques automate the process of searching for optimal hyperparameter configura­
tions, significantly reducing the need for manual experimentation and tuning. This is
done by employing optimization algorithms like Bayesian optimization and evolutionary
algorithms.
In this paper, AutoGluon is employed to bridge the gap between experts and nonexperts in the creation of models for FER. AutoGluon is used to automate the complex
tasks of selecting models, adjusting the layers in the neural networks and optimizing
hyperparameters and help the user create state-of-the-art models which are competitive
to those created by experts. The proposed AutoGluon-driven method attempts to cover
the underexplored area of AutoML in FER and succeeds in creating several models,
which outperform the conventional state-of-the-art in accuracy on the FER2013 data­
set [11]. The proposed AutoGluon model reported an accuracy of 76.4% on FER2013
dataset, higher than the traditional manually tuned models, which also shows its effi­
ciency when processing Big Data. This work demonstrates that AutoML has the poten­
tial to improve FER systems, making advanced facial expression analysis ubiquitous and
easy-to-use.

and Sect. 5 concludes the paper.
2  Related work
Facial Expression Recognition (FER) is a computer vision function aimed at categoriz­
ing emotional expressions. The focus of this task is to automate the detection of facial
expressions and thus the underlying emotional state by analysing various features and
mapping them to emotions like anger, happiness, and fear.
Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a computer program which can compute or “think” in the
way a human mind does. It is the ability of a program to transcend the limitations of

Page 4 of 23
Goel et al. Discover Applied Sciences          (2025) 7:1165
a regular program which is limited to computing exactly according to what the devel­
oper instructed it to, and instead creating its own mathematical structures and functions
to provide an output based on prior information given to it [12, 13]. Machine learning
(ML) [14] is a subset of AI, the ability given to a machine to learn and improve its perfor­
mance without the need for a human to explain how to accomplish the task [15]. It does
this by creating its own mathematical model based on patterns it identifies in data. In
this field, there have been recent innovations to use ML for skin cancer lesion analysis,
thus making ML an important part of the healthcare industry [16]. Deep learning (DL)
[2] is a subset of ML where computer scientists create “neural networks,” essentially lay­
ers of interconnected nodes or “neurons” to mimic the way the human mind processes
information. A neural network consists of such a web of neurons, each of which has a
mathematical algorithm (known as an activation function) to compute the information
passed to it by the previous layer and its own weights and biases to provide an output.
DL is thus the most efficient and intricate model, and it has the capability to work with
tabular data, images and recreate human language in the form of text and even speech.
Continuing the example, DL has also been used to further enhance the work of skin can­
cer diagnosis to improve the accuracy of the system [17].
FER systems cannot be handled by regular ML algorithms like Decision Trees and
Regression because of the difficulties in identifying relevant features in images. These
traditional ML algorithms are effective when the input features are explicitly defined
and inherently meaningful, like in the case of numerical data, so for them to be of effec­
tive, the attributes of the image (edges and shapes) must be manually engineered and
extracted into numerical representations before the algorithms can work with them. Raw
image data alone do not carry such meaningful information. However, DL algorithms
learn the hierarchical representations of images, with each layer extracting increasing
abstract features from the input images. This allows the algorithm to capture complex
patterns and relationships in the data, leading to superior classification performance.
Therefore, DL is the best way to perform facial expression recognition.
2.1  Review of literature
This subsection discusses the various models designed by researchers to automate
FER which build the expansive state-of-the-art which include CNNs, Attention-Based
Models, and Vision transformers. Saeed et al. [6] developed an automated framework
for facial detection, which they termed FD-CNN. The architecture consists of four con­
volutional layers and two hidden layers. The model is comprised of three processes;
preprocessing, feature extraction, and classification. The CK + dataset is applied for
both training and testing. Zhang et al. [18] introduced a novel approach dubbed Dual-
Direction Attention Mixed Feature Network (DDAMFN). The model enhances accuracy
with the use of dual-direction attention mechanisms (which enables attention to both
fine details and broader context) alongside mixed feature fusion. The model maintained
67.03% accuracy with the AffectNet dataset, 90.74% with FER+, and 91.35% overall in
RAF-DB. Later, Pham et al. [19] introduced a new approach for FER based on a Residual
Masking Network (RMN) which enhances the performance of CNN. The architecture
aims to achieve global and local feature capture. The architecture of the network com­
prises of residual blocks which capture image details on an intricate level. Moreover, it
utilizes masking layers to both suppress irrelevant and enhance crucial discriminating

features. The RMN captures relevant information from the input image through the use
of a segmentation network that partitions an image into several parts depending on the
class to which they belong. On the FER2013 dataset, RMN was able to surpass the previ­
ous benchmark with a dipatched accuracy of 74.14% and VEMO’s private dataset yield­
ing a 65.94%. Later, Krizhevsky et al. [20] devised a Deep Convolutional Neural Network
aimed for classifying the ImageNet database which harbours 1.2 million images parti­
tioned into 1000 distinct categories. Their final model comprises 5 convolutional layers
alongside 3 fully connected ones, employing a regularization technique known as ‘drop­
out’ to mitigate overfitting. This model was presented at the ImageNet ILSVRC-2012
contest, winning first place with 15.3% error rate against the runner up’s 26.2%. Later,
Jain et al. [21] introduced a model relying on a single Deep Convolutional Neural Net­
work (DNN) featuring convolutional layers and deep residual blocks. The authors argue
that incorporating residual blocks with CNN improves the results. The model was
trained on the CK + and JAFFE datasets, achieving an accuracy of 93.24% and 95.23%,
respectively.
On the other hand, Lopes et al. [22] 2022 developed a convolutional neural network
(CNN)-based model for facial expression recognition (FER) with specific image preprocessing techniques. It is claimed that these methods are competitive against other
approaches in the field of FER. These methods include the following: rotation correc­
tion, cropping, down-sampling, and intensity normalization. The model was evaluated
using the CK + and JAFFE and BV-3DFE datasets and achieved the highest accuracy of
96.76% on CK+. Wang et al. [23] suggested a novel approach to FER based on the use
of auxiliary models. The hypothesis is that using the entire face as input would result
in a wealth of extraneous data which would lead to the loss of important features. This
issue is mitigated through the use of multiple subregions of the face which are weighted
relative to the rest of the image. The model was tested on four datasets: JAFFE, CK+,
FER2013, and SFEW. Li et al. [24] proposed a new approach of FER aimed at enhancing
human-robot interaction by using a CNN for information extraction, long short-term
memory recurrent neural networks (LSTMs) for modelling the relation of facial expres­
sion changes over time with expressions in an image sequence and improved the mod­
el’s performance with transfer learning. The model in question was evaluated using the
JAFFE, CK+, FER2013, and SFEW datasets, obtaining accuracy values of 95.9%, 99.09%,
67.7%, and 59.97%, respectively. Later, Thacker [25] introduced three approaches to FER:
Multi-Layer Feature Fusion based Classification (MLFFC), Multi-Model Feature Fusion
based Classification (MMFFC), and a new model, EfficientNetB7, based on Normalized
CNN. For MLFFC, internal layer fusion is employed while in MMFFC, fusion is per­
formed using two models. EfficientNetB7 is implemented with Compound Scaling that
increases the scale of a CNN. The results are as follows: MLFFC achieved 99.69% accu­
racy on CK + and 70.29% on FER2013, MMFFC reached 92.04% on KDEF and 68.14%
on FER2013, and EfficientNetB7 achieved 57.5% on FER2013. Later, Cheng et al. [26]
presented two approaches that utilize ensembles of CNN classifiers for multiple-stage
classification. The first approach assigns different training sets to each CNN, while the
second changes the image size of the original data to create multiple training sets. Merg­
ing the results of the individual CNNs is done by majority voting. The two approaches
are tested using the ORL database, which includes ten images of forty different sub­
jects for a total of four hundred images. The first two models achieved 90.0% and 99.5%

accuracies respectively. Later Chantrapornchai et al. [27] assigned a model leveraging
an AutoML framework which integrates SqueezeNet (a CNN design meant to mini­
mize parameter count) and SE blocks with residual block merges. The authors suggest
such combinations can outperform traditional models without tuning outperform tradi­
tional models lacking hand tuning. The datasets used are CIFAR-10 and Tsinghua Facial

Additionally, the Swin-Fer network was proposed by Mei et al. in [29], built upon the
Swin transformer which captures global contextual information by using a fusion strat­
egy in the middle to deeper layers, and adopting data dimension conversion techniques
to obtain more spatial information. Amid the innovation, they also implemented a new
method with which they accomplished 100% accuracy on the CK + dataset and 71.11%
accuracy on FER2013. Also, Feng et al. [30] in their work advanced further by proposing
another method of fine-tuning the Swin transformer alongside Multiple Weights Opti­
mality-seeking. This model surpassed the state-of-the-art results for RAF-DB, FERPlus,
and AffectNet datasets. Tao and Duan [31] used hierarchical attention networks with
progressive feature fusion for FER. A range of studies showed that the introduced model
performed better than all the other FER methods. Naveen [32] used a combination of
Hopfield Networks, Deep Belief Network (DBN) architecture, and Lanczos interpola­
tion to identify obscured facial expressions. This paper greatly contributes the theory,
practice, and future research on facial expression recognition by providing a robust and
effective methodology. Zhang et al. [33] proposed the spatial channel cross-fusion dualattention network. The spatial channel focuses the features while the channel dimen­
sion offers a wider receptive field. Mehendale [34] advanced facial emotion recognition
(FER) using convolutional neural networks (CNN) by considering deep network and tra­
ditional machine learning approaches. The FERC obtains facial feature vectors by back­
ground extraction through a two-stage CNN. To address demographic biases in affective
computing AI systems, Singhal et al. [35] put forth the Demographic Biased Mitigating
Framework (DBMF). The criticism of AI-based automated FER systems for not consid­
ering multi-modal signals gave birth to Li et al. [36] FER-former, a transformer network
for FER in unconstrained settings with supervision from other modalities. To tackle
the issue of restricted receptive fields, a hybrid feature extraction pipeline that com­
bines cascaded CNNs and transformers is employed. Ge et al. [37] later developed an
occluded expression recognition model with a countermeasure network. The model
consists of two modules: face recognition and restoration of the obstructed face image.

3  Proposed work
3.1  Dataset
There are two broad categories of datasets, the first of which are the controlled or lab
datasets. These datasets are created in well-controlled environments like laboratories,
with attention to lighting, the actors’ posture, camera angles and the background. Exam­
ples include JAFFE, MMI, KDEF and CK+ [38]. The second type of dataset is known as
in-the-wild datasets. These consist of images captured in real-world scenarios and are
inherently challenging due to issues such as obstructions in the image, and variations in
lighting, background and poses. Some well-known datasets which fall into this category
are: FER2013, FER + and AffectNet [38–40]. These are harder to classify and will have
a lower accuracy as compared to the controlled ones due to the challenges mentioned
but are of great importance in the context of Big Data, as they most closely resemble the
data which real-world FER models aim to predict by virtue of being created by gathering
results on the internet.

pixel) images divided into seven broad expressions: anger, disgust, fear, happy, neutral,
sad and surprise. FER2013 dataset was originally used in the ICML 2013 Challenges in
Representation Learning and included uncontrolled images. Figure 1 reflects an instance
of the FER 2013 dataset. It is divided into two parts, training data (consisting of 28,709

3.2  Models
To ensure a comprehensive and rigorous analysis, four distinct models were employed,
each utilizing varied configurations tailored to address specific elements of the problem.
The Default Model (Sect. 4.1) utilized a medium-quality preset, along with a dynamic
selection of learning rate and batch size, using the Adam optimizer and a 10% valida­
tion holdout. The Low Resource Model (Sect. 4.2), optimized for CPU usage, employed a
medium-quality preset with a higher learning rate for faster training. The Balanced Con­
figuration Model (Sect.  4.3) sought to achieve a compromise between computational
speed and output quality by using a high-quality preset, a moderate learning rate (0.001),
a batch size of 32, and the Adam optimizer. Finally, the High-Quality Model (Sect. 4.4)
prioritized accuracy, employing the highest quality preset, a low learning rate (0.0005), a
batch size of 16, and the AdamW optimizer.

(ii)	Hyperparameter Optimization: Once the initial models are selected, AutoGluon

3.5  Evaluation metrics
The proposed model is evaluated on different metrics like Accuracy, Precision, Recall

4.7  Comparative analysis with other automl frameworks
In comparison to other AutoML frameworks, AutoGluon is specifically designed for
multimodal problems and supports deep learning frameworks, making it well-suited for
computer vision tasks like FER. In contrast, other frameworks primarily focus on tabu­
lar data using genetic programming and are not optimized for image-based tasks unless
pre-processed into numerical features. Similarly, Google Cloud AutoML Vision provides
a no-code interface for image classification but offers limited transparency and control
over model architecture, hyperparameters, and training strategy.
In terms of accuracy, AutoGluon’s default configuration achieved 76.4% on the
FER2013 dataset, outperforming state-of-the-art manually tuned models. While com­
parative FER results from TPOT are limited due to its focus on structured data, Google
AutoML Vision has shown competitive performance in general image classification but
lacks published FER benchmarks on FER2013. In terms of training time and resource
requirements, AutoGluon provides flexibility through different presets (e.g., Low
Resource, Balanced, High Quality), allowing users to balance performance with com­
putational cost. Google Cloud AutoML abstracts away resource configuration but often
requires longer cloud-based training cycles and incurs usage costs. TPOT, although
lightweight, is less suited for deep image models and typically requires external feature
engineering for FER. Overall, AutoGluon strikes a strong balance between performance,
control, and automation—making it particularly advantageous for FER tasks that require
deep image model customization and rapid experimentation.
4.8  Discussion
This paper demonstrated the practical and technical viability of using AutoML, par­
ticularly AutoGluon, for facial expression recognition (FER). Among the four proposed
configurations, the Default Model achieved the highest test accuracy (76.4%), outper­
forming both traditional FER methods and more complex AutoML configurations. This
suggests that AutoML can autonomously discover efficient architectures and hyperpa­
rameters without manual tuning.

Goel et al. Discover Applied Sciences          (2025) 7:1165
Interestingly, the High-Quality Model, despite having significantly more parameters
and longer training time, did not outperform the Default Model. This can be attributed
to over-parameterization and slower convergence. These findings highlight the AutoML
system’s ability to prioritize practical, efficient configurations over sheer complexity. The
results also affirm the ability of AutoGluon to manage imbalanced and noisy datasets
like FER2013, which are notoriously difficult for many manually tuned models. By using
automated hyperparameter optimization and ensembling, AutoGluon was able to gener­
alize well across emotion categories.
4.9  Limitations
Despite its promising outcomes, this research has several limitations mentioned below:
(i)	Computational Demand: AutoML still requires substantial computational resources,
particularly for high-quality configurations. The high-quality model took over 7 h to
train and may not be feasible for users with limited hardware.
(ii)	Dataset Specificity: The models were trained and evaluated on FER2013. While it is
a widely used benchmark, the generalizability of the results to other datasets such as
AffectNet or SFEW remains untested in this paper.
(iii)	 Black-Box Nature of AutoML: While AutoGluon simplifies the pipeline for nonexperts, it also abstracts internal processes, which may be a drawback for researchers
who wish to understand or manually modify specific layers or operations.
(iv)	 Bias in Imbalanced Data: Despite the use of advanced metrics (precision, recall,
F1-score), the performance remained weak for minority classes such as “Disgust,”
which constituted only 1.52% of the dataset. This imbalance could lead to biased
predictions in real-world applications.
(v)	Bias in Foundational Models: Due to the ensembling of pre-trained models, there
is a high probability of perpetuation of inherent biases which may be present in the
foundational models, which will lead to those same biases appearing in models created
by AutoGluon.
(vi)	 Inability of Ablation Studies: Due to the automation of model selection and
ensembling, it is not possible to do ablation studies on the proposed models which
might reduce transparency of the model.
(vii)	 Absence of statistical validation: The proposed work is unable to conduct significance
tests and obtaining confidence intervals due to resource constraints.
4.10  Future work
Future work can build on this research in the following ways:
(i)	Cross-Dataset Generalization: To validate robustness, the trained models should be
tested or fine-tuned on other FER datasets like AffectNet, FER+, or SFEW. This would
help assess generalizability.
(ii)	Model Interpretability: Incorporating explainable AI (XAI) methods can help interpret
the decision-making of AutoML-generated models, increasing their trustworthiness
in sensitive applications like healthcare or security.
(iii)	 Hybrid AutoML Strategies: Exploring hybrid pipelines that combine AutoML for
model exploration with expert-guided fine-tuning could yield both accessible and
highly optimized FER systems.

(iv)	 Deployment Optimization: Investigating lightweight AutoML models for mobile

(v)	Bias Mitigation Techniques: Future work should address the dataset imbalance by

learning to improve performance on underrepresented emotion classes.
(vi)	 Statistical validation: With the availability of high-end computing facility,
significance tests can be conducted to obtain confidence intervals.
4.11  Ethical considerations in FER applications
The deployment of FER systems, particularly in sensitive domains such as healthcare,
security, and education, raises critical ethical concerns. These include risks of misclas­
sification, bias, privacy invasion, and over-reliance on automated decisions. To mitigate
these issues, it is essential to ensure transparency, apply bias mitigation strategies, obtain
informed consent, and position FER tools as assistive rather than authoritative systems.
Addressing these ethical challenges is key to ensuring the responsible and equitable use
of FER technologies in real-world applications.
4.12  Real-World applicability
The increasing demand for affective computing systems across domains such as health­
care, transportation, education, and public safety necessitates FER systems that are not
only accurate but also deployable under real-world constraints. The proposed AutoMLbased framework offers practical advantages, enabling its use across a range of deploy­
ment environments.
(i)	Deployment Scenarios: The models can be deployed in the fields of healthcare
monitoring, driver drowsiness detection, online education engagement and public
surveillance.
(ii)	Computational Complexity: The current work includes four configurations, (Default,
Low Resource, Balanced and High Quality), each designed to work in different
environments with different computational resources.
(iii)	 Real World Efficiency: A comparative analysis between the models in terms of
training time, accuracy and performance metrics is shown in Sect. 4.5 and Table 12,
which allows users to choose a model which fits their deployment constraints.
(iv)	 Deployment using AutoGluon: AutoGluon automates pipeline creation, thereby
reducing the need for deep technical expertise and making the models suitable for
non-expert deployment.
5  Conclusion
In this paper, AutoML was explored to find the best model for facial expression detec­
tion to overcome the challenges of traditional methods. Experimental results dem­
onstrated that AutoML could effectively automate model selection, hyperparameter
optimization and ensemble learning, significantly reducing the manual effort tradition­
ally required for FER model development. These results also revealed that AutoML is a
viable alternative to traditional methods of model creation, as it outperformed the con­
ventional state-of-the-art during evaluation. It is also an effective method when deal­
ing with the large volume and variety of images inherent in Big Data. This can have

significant implications for real-world applications of FER, including emotion analysis in
large-scale market research, healthcare monitoring, real-time security and surveillance,
educational engagement assessment and driver drowsiness detection. Experiments on
the FER2013 dataset showed encouraging results, with the AutoML-generated models
achieving SOTA accuracies of 76.4%, 70.0%, 74.4% and 75.1%.
Employing AutoGluon in FER offers transformative benefits that directly address longstanding challenges in model development. Traditional FER systems require extensive
manual effort in model architecture design, hyperparameter tuning, and performance
optimization—processes that are time-consuming, resource-intensive and demand
expert knowledge. AutoGluon simplifies and accelerates these processes through auto­
mation, enabling non-experts to build high-performing models with minimal interven­
tion. Specifically, AutoGluon enhances FER by dynamically exploring a diverse set of
deep learning architectures (such as ResNet, ViT, and EfficientNet), fine-tuning them
using advanced techniques like Bayesian optimization, and ensembling top-performing
models to maximize generalization and accuracy. This not only reduces the risk of over­
fitting but also boosts performance on challenging, in-the-wild datasets like FER2013.
As demonstrated in the paper, AutoGluon achieved state-of-the-art accuracy of 76.4%,
outperforming several manually tuned models while significantly cutting down devel­
opment time and computational cost. By democratizing access to robust FER systems,
AutoGluon has the potential to accelerate real-world deployment in domains like health­
care, security, and emotion-aware applications, making intelligent emotion recognition
more scalable, adaptable, and inclusive.
However, there were also certain limitations revealed during the research, includ­
ing the dependency on the quality and diversity of the training data, the need for high
computational power and the possibility of perpetuation of bias which may be present
in foundational models. Future research could focus on addressing these limitations by
exploring data augmentation techniques, using multi-modal data and distributed com­
puting resources as well as making sure that the underlying data is scrutinized to prevent
the reinforcement of inherent biases in the models. In the current times, where artificial
intelligence and machine learning are being applied to every domain and have use cases
in almost every application, there is an urgent need to allow non-experts the path to
implement their own ML systems, a path that once remained blocked due to the skills
and knowledge required to build these systems. AutoML is the key to enabling the ubiq­
uitous use of ML technology and further integrate it into everyday applications, while
also furthering the state-of-the-art.
